How to Conduct Effective Interviews for Your Articles


Interviewing people for articles can be one of the most rewarding and insightful ways to gather information. However, the effectiveness of your interviews depends heavily on how you conduct them. Whether you’re writing a feature, news article, or a personal profile, learning how to conduct interviews that yield valuable insights is crucial. In this guide, we’ll cover the steps to conducting effective interviews, from preparation to post-interview follow-ups.

1. Preparing for the Interview

Effective interviews start with thorough preparation. You need to ensure that you have all the necessary background information on the subject of the interview, as well as a set of thoughtful and purposeful questions.

Research Your Subject

Before you even think about sitting down for an interview, make sure to conduct some preliminary research. This research helps you understand the context of the subject’s work, their expertise, and their relevance to the topic you’re writing about. Read through articles, books, or any other sources where the person has been featured. This research will help you formulate more specific and thoughtful questions.

You want to avoid asking general or basic questions that can be easily answered with a Google search. Instead, aim for questions that delve deeper into the subject’s experiences, opinions, or expertise.

Draft Your Questions

Once you’ve done your research, the next step is drafting a list of questions. These questions should serve as the foundation for your interview and help guide the conversation.

Start with open-ended questions that allow your interviewee to share their thoughts and experiences in detail. Open-ended questions typically begin with phrases like “how,” “why,” or “tell me about.” For example:

  • “What inspired you to start your career in this field?”
  • “How do you think the industry has evolved over the past decade?”
  • “Can you walk me through the challenges you’ve faced in your journey?”

In addition to open-ended questions, prepare follow-up questions that will help you dig deeper into the responses. Be flexible and ready to ask spontaneous questions based on the interviewee’s answers. This will allow for a more dynamic conversation.

Logistics and Scheduling

Arrange the logistics of the interview well in advance. Decide whether the interview will be conducted in person, over the phone, or through a virtual platform like Zoom or Skype. Confirm the time, date, and any technical requirements (for example, checking that your audio recording equipment works properly).

Consider the location of the interview. If it’s in person, choose a quiet place with minimal distractions, such as a private office, a quiet café, or a meeting room. If you’re conducting a virtual interview, ensure you have a strong internet connection and a backup plan in case of technical issues.

2. Building Rapport and Setting the Tone

When you first meet your interviewee, whether in person or virtually, it’s important to establish a comfortable atmosphere. The tone of the interview can significantly affect the quality of the conversation. Your goal should be to create a space where the interviewee feels at ease sharing their thoughts openly.

Start with Small Talk

Starting with some casual conversation can help break the ice. You could ask about their day, comment on the weather, or briefly discuss something personal about their work or background. This helps humanize you as the interviewer and allows the subject to relax before diving into more serious questions.

Explain the Purpose of the Interview

Clearly explain the purpose of the interview. Let them know how the information you gather will be used and reassure them that their answers will be handled with respect and professionalism. If the interview is for a sensitive topic, be sure to mention that their comfort and consent are important to you. This builds trust and sets the tone for a collaborative conversation.

Set Expectations

Let the interviewee know how long the interview will last and what the format will be. This ensures that both of you are on the same page and helps avoid any surprises. It also shows respect for their time, which can result in a more productive conversation.

3. Conducting the Interview

The interview itself is where the magic happens. It’s important to be an active listener, stay engaged, and keep the conversation flowing naturally. Here are some strategies for conducting an interview that will produce valuable material for your article.

Ask Open-Ended Questions

As mentioned earlier, open-ended questions are key to unlocking meaningful responses. These questions encourage the interviewee to share their thoughts in depth rather than giving simple yes or no answers. Allow your interviewee to elaborate on their answers, and avoid interrupting them. Your goal is to gather as much information as possible from them.

For example, instead of asking, “Did you enjoy writing this book?” you might ask, “What was the most rewarding part of writing this book?” This approach allows the interviewee to provide a deeper, more insightful response.

Be Flexible with Your Questions

While it’s important to have a list of questions, don’t feel constrained by them. If your interviewee gives an interesting answer, feel free to follow up on it. Follow-up questions show that you are listening closely and are genuinely interested in what the person is saying.

Some follow-up questions to consider:

  • “Could you elaborate on that?”
  • “What was going through your mind when that happened?”
  • “How did that experience shape your current perspective?”

This approach ensures that you’re not simply reading a list of questions but engaging in an organic conversation.

Maintain a Neutral Tone

Throughout the interview, make sure to maintain a neutral and unbiased tone. Your goal is to gather information, not to influence the interviewee’s responses. Avoid expressing judgment or inserting personal opinions during the conversation. This will allow your subject to feel more comfortable and open in their responses.

It’s important to be professional and respectful in your demeanor. While your questions can be probing, they should never come off as aggressive or confrontational.

Take Notes or Record the Conversation

Depending on the nature of the interview, it’s advisable to either take detailed notes or record the conversation (with permission). Recording can be helpful, as it allows you to capture the conversation verbatim, ensuring you don’t miss any crucial points.

If you choose to record the interview, let the interviewee know ahead of time and ask for their consent. Some people may feel uncomfortable being recorded, and you need to respect their wishes. Additionally, be prepared to transcribe the interview afterward.

If you opt for note-taking, make sure to jot down the key points, quotes, and any ideas that seem relevant to your article. You can always clarify points with the interviewee later if needed.

Stay Focused

It’s easy to get sidetracked during an interview, especially if the conversation turns to interesting but irrelevant topics. However, staying focused on your core objectives is essential. Politely steer the conversation back to the main points of your article when necessary, but always remain respectful.

If the interviewee shares personal anecdotes or off-topic stories that are engaging, don’t be afraid to explore those—just make sure you’re still gaining the insights needed for your article.

4. Post-Interview Follow-up

Once the interview is over, there are several important post-interview tasks that can help ensure the quality of your article and maintain a good relationship with the interviewee.

Review Your Notes and Recordings

After the interview, take the time to go over your notes or the recording to extract the most relevant quotes and insights. Pay attention to any quotes that are particularly vivid, insightful, or thought-provoking. These can help add depth to your article.

Send a Thank-You Note

It’s always a good idea to send a thank-you note after an interview. This gesture shows appreciation for the interviewee’s time and willingness to share their expertise. It also leaves a positive impression and can help maintain a professional relationship for future collaborations.

Clarify Any Details

If, upon reviewing your notes or recording, you find that something is unclear or requires further elaboration, don’t hesitate to follow up with your interviewee. Politely reach out and ask for clarification or additional information. This ensures the accuracy and quality of your article.

Fact-Check Your Information

Before incorporating any details from the interview into your article, make sure to fact-check everything. Verify any claims, statistics, or facts mentioned by the interviewee, especially if the interviewee is sharing data or referring to research. This helps you avoid spreading misinformation and ensures that your article is both credible and reliable.

5. Writing Your Article

After completing the interview and gathering all the necessary material, the next step is to write your article. Ensure that you organize your article clearly and cohesively, incorporating relevant quotes and insights from the interview.

A well-written article should:

  • Have a compelling introduction that captures the reader’s attention
  • Maintain a logical flow, with each section building on the previous one
  • Integrate quotes from the interview naturally, making them an integral part of the article
  • Be clear, concise, and informative

Make sure to edit and proofread your article carefully before publishing to ensure that it is free of errors and flows smoothly.

By following these steps, you can conduct interviews that yield valuable, informative, and engaging material for your articles. Remember, the key to a successful interview is preparation, active listening, and respect for your interviewee’s time and input. Conducting interviews effectively is a skill that improves with practice, so the more you interview, the better you’ll become at it.

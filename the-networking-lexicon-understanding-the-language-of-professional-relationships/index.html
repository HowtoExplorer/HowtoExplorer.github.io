The Networking Lexicon: Understanding the Language of Professional Relationships


Networking is the lifeblood of any successful career. It’s through networking that you can meet new contacts, learn about new opportunities, and form valuable professional relationships. However, many people find the language of networking confusing and off-putting. This is because the vocabulary of networking is thick with jargon, acronyms and abbreviations. In this post, we will demystify the terminology and break down the language of networking, so you can navigate your way through professional relationships with ease.

1. Building Blocks: The Basics of Networking

Connection: The foundation of any network is a connection. A connection could be a shared university, an interest in a particular field, or a mutual acquaintance. The more connections you have, the stronger your network.

Contact: A contact is a person you have met and would like to stay connected with. They could be a colleague, a client, or a mentor.

Mentor: A mentor is someone who guides you and provides advice as you navigate your career. They can offer support, answer questions and help you gain valuable insights into your desired field.

Alumni: Alumni are former students from a particular institution. Networking with alumni can be an effective way to make new connections in your field.

2. Tools of the Trade: Networking Strategies

Attend: Attending conferences, workshops, and industry events can be a great way to meet people interested in the same topic. Remember to bring business cards and be prepared to talk about your current work and interests.

Join: Joining professional organizations and interest groups can give you access to a like-minded community. You can attend regular meetings, participate in online forums, and connect with people who have similar goals.

Offer: Offering to help others can be a powerful networking tool. By volunteering your expertise or offering to introduce others to people in your network, you demonstrate your commitment to building and maintaining relationships.

3. Paths to Professionalism: Networking Stages

Emerging: Emerging networks are for those just starting or re-entering the workforce. These networks often have mentorship and training programs to help you build your skills and connections.

Established: Established networks are for those with more experience. These networks offer opportunities for career advancement, leadership roles, and industry-specific knowledge exchange.

Executive: Executive networks are for those at the highest level of their careers. These networks often provide opportunities for collaboration, philanthropy, and industry advocacy.

4. Gaining Altitude: Networking Tactics

Keys: Keys to the city ceremonies are events celebrating and honoring business and community leaders. Attending these events can give you access to influential people and opportunities.

Board: Serving on a board is a great way to build your leadership skills and gain visibility within your industry. Consider volunteering for a local non-profit or professional organization.

Lunch: Lunches, whether hosted or attended, can be an effective networking strategy. They provide a more intimate setting to connect and build relationships.

Committee: Serving on a committee is another way to build your network. These groups often have diverse perspectives and expertise, providing ample opportunities for collaboration and learning.

In conclusion, networking is the backbone of professional relationships. But like any language, it requires learning and practice to understand and communicate effectively. By familiarizing yourself with the basics and gaining valuable skills, you can build a strong and far-reaching network that will serve you well throughout your career. Remember, the goal is to form meaningful connections that can lead to growth, learning, and success. So go out there and start networking!

How Climate Change Influences Infectious Diseases


Climate change is not just a topic associated with rising global temperatures and melting ice caps. It has a profound impact on the spread and outbreak of infectious diseases, affecting human health and wellbeing. As the world grapples with the Covid-19 pandemic, it is essential to recognize the intricate link between climate change and infectious diseases, and how it threatens global health security. Warmth and Water: The Perfect Brew for Disease Transmission As temperatures rise, mosquitoes, ticks, and other biting and blood-feeding insects become more active, increasing the spread of vector-borne diseases like malaria, dengue fever, and Lyme disease. Warmth also allows these mosquitoes to adapt and spread to new areas, making them more potent carriers of disease. In many tropical regions, warmer temperatures extend the breeding season for mosquitoes, hosting more hatchings and enabling the spread of disease. When bodies of water become warmer, the ecosystems that rely on them also begin to shift. This temperature increase accelerates the life cycle of many waterborne pathogens, resulting in increased outbreaks of diseases such as cholera, typhoid, and amoebic dysentery. As the ice and snow melts with retreating glaciers, waterborne disease risk rises in communities vulnerable to floods. Changes in water chemistry too can present challenges as water becomes contaminated with pathogens, as well.

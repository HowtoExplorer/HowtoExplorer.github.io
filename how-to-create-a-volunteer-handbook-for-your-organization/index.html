How to Create a Volunteer Handbook for Your Organization


A volunteer handbook serves as one of the most foundational tools for any organization that relies on the dedication and service of volunteers. It functions as a comprehensive resource, setting expectations, providing guidance, and fostering alignment between your organization’s mission and the values of your volunteers. Designing one involves thoughtful planning and attention to detail, but the outcome is an indispensable resource that enhances volunteer recruitment, retention, and overall engagement. In this guide, we’ll walk you through the key elements of creating a volunteer handbook tailored to your organization’s needs.

Why Your Organization Needs a Volunteer Handbook

A well-crafted volunteer handbook benefits both your organization and your volunteers. By setting clear expectations and providing necessary information, it establishes a solid foundation for successful collaboration. Consider these reasons why a volunteer handbook is essential:

  1. Consistent Communication: It ensures all volunteers receive the same information about policies, procedures, and organizational culture.
  2. Legal Protection: It helps protect the organization by clarifying roles and expectations while addressing liability issues.
  3. Volunteer Empowerment: By equipping volunteers with detailed guidance, they gain confidence in their roles.
  4. Streamlining Orientation: It minimizes time spent on repetitive onboarding tasks by providing standard documentation.
  5. Alignment with Mission: By presenting your organization’s mission, goals, and values upfront, volunteers can better understand the purpose of their work.

Planning Your Volunteer Handbook

Creating a handbook demands careful thought and collaboration among stakeholders. Follow these initial steps to build an effective document:

1. Understand Your Audience

Before drafting your handbook, think about the wide range of volunteers who will read it. Are they students, retirees, or professionals? Are they committing to short-term projects or long-term service? Identify their unique needs and tailor the tone and structure of the handbook to make it approachable and relevant.

2. Collaborate with Key Stakeholders

Involve program managers, HR personnel, and legal advisors in the development process. Their perspectives will help ensure the handbook reflects internal procedures while covering any legal or safety concerns.

3. Define the Handbook’s Scope

Determine what information is most critical for volunteers to know. Strike a balance between offering comprehensive guidance and keeping the content concise enough to encourage reading.

Structure and Content of a Volunteer Handbook

A clear structure is key to crafting an accessible and user-friendly handbook. Below are essential sections you should include, along with suggested content for each:

1. Welcome Letter

Open your handbook with a heartfelt welcome letter from your executive director, volunteer manager, or board chair. This personal touch can make volunteers feel valued from the start. Use this space to express gratitude, introduce the mission, and emphasize the importance of volunteers to the organization’s success.

2. Mission, Vision, and Values

Explain WHY your organization exists, WHAT it aims to achieve, and HOW it upholds its values. By aligning volunteer roles with your organization’s mission, you provide meaningful context to their contributions.

3. Overview of Volunteer Roles

Clarify roles and responsibilities so volunteers understand what is expected of them. Provide descriptions of common tasks, time commitments, and potential program-specific opportunities. Help volunteers visualize their impact by correlating roles with organizational goals.

4. Policies and Procedures

This section might be the most comprehensive, as it ensures consistency in how volunteers conduct themselves. Include policies on:

  • Code of conduct
  • Dress codes (if applicable)
  • Attendance and punctuality
  • Anti-harassment and discrimination
  • Social media use while volunteering

5. Onboarding Process

Walk new volunteers through the onboarding journey, providing any resources or instructions they’ll need for their first shift. Mention any required training programs, paperwork, or background checks.

6. Safety and Emergency Protocols

Volunteer safety should be a top priority. Offer clear instructions on handling emergencies, reporting incidents, and accessing first-aid resources. Be sure to include contact information for key personnel in case of emergencies.

7. Communication Guidelines

Good communication fosters a strong sense of community among volunteers and staff. Indicate how volunteers can share feedback, report concerns, or stay updated on events and opportunities. Suggest preferred methods of communication, such as email or organizational newsletters.

8. Volunteer Benefits

Though volunteering is unpaid, many organizations provide perks to thank their team. List any available benefits, such as free event tickets, networking opportunities, or skills training.

9. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Anticipate common questions from new volunteers, such as:

  • “What happens if I can’t make a scheduled shift?”
  • “Am I required to wear specific gear?”
  • “How often will I receive volunteer updates?” Providing these answers in advance will save time while addressing common concerns.

10. Acknowledgment Form

Conclude the handbook with a section requiring volunteers to acknowledge they’ve read and understood the information provided. Include a signature line or checkbox for digital versions of the handbook.

Writing Tips for an Engaging Volunteer Handbook

Now that you know what to include, here are a few techniques to ensure your handbook is clear, engaging, and aligned with your organization’s voice:

  1. Use Plain Language: Avoid technical jargon or legalese; write in a conversational tone that’s easy to follow.
  2. Be Concise: While the handbook must be thorough, avoid overwhelming readers with excessive details. Focus on what’s most important.
  3. Visual Elements: Although this post focuses on text, consider using bullet points, numbered lists, and section headers when formatting the handbook to improve readability.
  4. Test with New Volunteers: Share the handbook with a few recent recruits to gather their feedback on clarity and usability.

Making the Handbook Accessible

Accessibility is crucial for ensuring every volunteer can utilize the handbook effectively. Consider the following to make your document inclusive:

  • Distribute digital and printed copies for those who prefer different formats.
  • Use simple fonts and high-contrast colors for readability.
  • Ensure all content complies with accessibility standards for individuals with disabilities.

Keeping Your Volunteer Handbook Up-to-Date

An outdated handbook does more harm than good. Make it a priority to review the document annually, or whenever significant policies or procedures change. Solicit feedback from volunteers about what additional information they’d find valuable. Update digital versions promptly and clearly communicate changes to ensure everyone is on the same page.

Conclusion

A thoughtful and well-organized volunteer handbook is a cornerstone of successful volunteer management. It not only equips your team with guidance but shows them your organization values their contributions. With careful planning, clear communication, and regular updates, your handbook can strengthen relationships with volunteers and play an integral role in your organization’s mission.

Taking the time to create a volunteer handbook is an investment that pays dividends not just for the volunteers who join your efforts, but for the organization as a whole.

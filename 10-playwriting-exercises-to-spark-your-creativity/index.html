10 Playwriting Exercises to Spark Your Creativity


As a playwright, you're well aware of the daunting task of developing compelling characters, weaving intricate plotlines, and crafting dialogue that comes alive on stage. But before these elements can be pieced together, you must first ignite the spark within yourself - a spark that fuels your creativity and propels your writing forward. And what better way to do so than with a series of playwriting exercises designed to get your imagination racing? Exercise 1: Sense Memory In this exercise, you'll delve into the world of sensory experience, putting yourself in the shoes of a character who's just undergone a pivotal moment. Write a descriptive piece that captures the sensation of walking into a dimly lit room, only to be enveloped in a faint scent of citrus. Note the intricate details: the way the scent settles on your skin, the faint tickle in the back of your throat, and the musky undertones that linger long after the initial hit. This exercise encourages sensory exploration, allowing you to tap into the emotions and surroundings of your characters. By honoring the authenticity of their experiences, you'll breathe life into your scenes and leave your audience with a lasting impression. Exercise 2: Story Through Objects Objects hold the power to evoke emotions, transport us to memories long past, and create a sense of continuity between disparate moments in time. Choose a personal item, such as a cherished heirloom or a scarred, worn book, and record its story through a series of fragmented scenes. Each fragment should be a snippet from a different time period, revealing the item's multi-faceted history and the roles it's played in your characters' lives. This exercise not only helps develop your character's connections to specific objects but also trains you to contextualize the objects relative to the events that unfold around them. Exercise 3: The L-Word Exercise This exercise focuses on unlocking the inner workings of your characters' psyches through the power of language. Take a random phrase and explore various emotional underpinnings associated with it. For instance, consider the phrase "Loser" - is it a label hurled as a tease or an insult directed at someone consumed by self-doubt? Play with the word's many meanings and assign it to various scenarios, ultimately crafting a fully fleshed-out scene driven by the fluid dynamics between character and setting. This exercise reminds you that words carry an immense responsibility in crafting the emotional fabric of your scenes - allowing you to delve deeper into the darkest as well as the lightest corners of your characters. Exercise 4: Dialogue without Body Language For this exercise, remove your characters' non-verbal cues from the scene. Break down the conversation into a straightforward exchange of words and phrases, stripping away every gesture, movement, and peculiarity that speaks volumes beyond the spoken word. This reduced conversation will suddenly render palpable the subtleties of your characters' personalities and enable you to build additional insight into their backstories. The absence of non-verbal cues helps emphasize the raw, unfiltered sincerity of words unsullied by context and emotional suggestion. Exercise 5: Story through Song Lyrics Song lyrics are an underutilized creative tool for playwrights looking to reinvigorate their characters and create narrative pockets around complex topics. Choose a song that speaks to a moment in your character's life, interpreting it through dialogue or non-verbal storytelling to convey the power and the depth of this pivotal emotional event. This exercise uses external pop culture to inspire your characters and expose dimensions within the dramatic trajectory of both internal monologues and exchanges between individuals. Exercise 6: The Absence of a Character Imagine you're developing a script for a 3-4 character scene when you suddenly realize that a crucial role is missing. Who would the vanished character be and how would they weave their presence into the scene through the actions, fears, and remarks of the remaining players? This thought experiment forces you to consider the nuances in any character dynamics and the possible way one unseen element may entirely reshape an otherwise familiar or predictable outcome. This exercise trains you to capture the power and the dynamic that even a silent presence can impart upon your scenes. Exercise 7: Building Conflict from 5 Minutes After the Character's Birth Create a hypothetical scenario: five minutes after the main character takes their first breath, what conflicts are brewing, shaping the paths they will take in the future? With every newborn comes a dynamic of tension, surprise and unexplored needs - in this specific brief and distinct instant capture their newborn self trapped between unknown responsibilities. This exercise sparks reflection on what makes for authentic storytelling: it reveals essential power shifts leading away from simplistic, overly simplistic origins stories. You will re-associate and analyze patterns in storytelling for narrative possibilities when assessing the central problem experienced by your characters in stage plays. Exercise 8: Alternating Between Realism and Fantasy Imagine a world where realistic elements sit alongside imaginative and fantastical elements, one mirroring reality in an uncanny play of contrasting perspectives. Take a mundane situation and embellish it with truly far-fetched elements so as to capture the eccentricities inherent within the reality itself. The interplay between realism and fantasy will teach you to connect both truthfully, the complexity and power hidden when trying to seize each separate artistic realm to compose just the right ambiance. Exercise 9: Finding the Key Scene in an Absent Story Choose a story where a pivotal scene never makes it into the larger narrative - either due to missteps in development or a creative choice. Use this abandoned scene to launch an exploration into the discarded backstory, personality development, inner conflicts and back-and-forths missing between both historical and timeless narratives in search of missing historical or critical junctures which unlock key patterns. By integrating these forgotten moments into your overall work, you'll begin to uncover new layers of meaning hidden within your script, strengthening the essential bonds between fragmented perspectives. Exercise 10: Collage Making Stimulate the right side of your brain and summon creativity through collage - gather disparate, abstract forms, bold shapes, colorful papers, and fragments to encapsulate, draw out a collage expressing raw emotional expression that elicits vivid insight on a character's perspective on the story. Describe the connection to how this world would mirror the person's emotional ups and downs as a complement - perhaps even contrast to your script's narrative. The expression you derive from fragmented elements may showcase emotions you previously thought you knew without evoking clarity or re-experiencing patterns one discovered from your story and narrative, reinforcing the power connected within the imagery associated with our emotional reflection. By embracing the unpredictable power of these playwriting exercises, you'll cultivate an arsenal of innovative ideas to keep your creativity percolating and ensure your storytelling stays relevant in today's ever-changing theatrical landscape. Don't be afraid to explore new ground and tap into the depths of your characters - for it's only from such bold experimentation that masterpieces rise from the ashes of traditional plots.

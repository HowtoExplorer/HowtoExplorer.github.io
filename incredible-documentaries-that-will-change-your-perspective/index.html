Incredible Documentaries That Will Change Your Perspective


Documentaries have a unique power to open our eyes to new ideas, cultures, and perspectives. They can challenge our beliefs, spark empathy, and inspire change. If you’re looking to expand your worldview and gain a deeper understanding of the world around you, here are some incredible documentaries that will change your perspective.

1. “13th”

Ava DuVernay’s “13th” is a powerful exploration of the intersection of race, justice, and mass incarceration in the United States. The documentary takes its title from the 13th Amendment to the Constitution, which abolished slavery but left a loophole that has been exploited to disproportionately imprison Black people. Through interviews, archival footage, and thought-provoking analysis, “13th” sheds light on the systemic issues within the criminal justice system and prompts viewers to reconsider their understanding of race and inequality.

2. “Blackfish”

“Blackfish” is a gripping documentary that delves into the controversial captivity of killer whales and the tragic consequences for both the animals and their human trainers. The film exposes the dark side of the entertainment industry and raises important questions about animal welfare, corporate responsibility, and the ethics of keeping wild animals in captivity. “Blackfish” will challenge your perceptions of marine parks and the treatment of animals in captivity.

3. “Won’t You Be My Neighbor?”

For a heartwarming and uplifting experience, look no further than “Won’t You Be My Neighbor?” This documentary celebrates the life and legacy of Fred Rogers, the beloved host of the children’s television program “Mister Rogers’ Neighborhood.” Through interviews and archival footage, the film reveals the profound impact of Rogers’ compassionate approach to children’s television and his unwavering belief in the value of kindness and empathy. “Won’t You Be My Neighbor?” is a moving portrayal of the transformative power of empathy and human connection.

4. “The Act of Killing”

“The Act of Killing” is a chilling and surreal exploration of the Indonesian mass killings of 1965-1966, as seen through the eyes of the perpetrators. Director Joshua Oppenheimer challenges former death squad leaders to reenact their crimes in the style of Hollywood films, resulting in a haunting portrayal of the banality of evil and the lasting trauma of the killings. This documentary offers a harrowing look at the human capacity for cruelty and the legacy of violence.

5. “13th”

Ava DuVernay’s “13th” is a powerful exploration of the intersection of race, justice, and mass incarceration in the United States. The documentary takes its title from the 13th Amendment to the Constitution, which abolished slavery but left a loophole that has been exploited to disproportionately imprison Black people. Through interviews, archival footage, and thought-provoking analysis, “13th” sheds light on the systemic issues within the criminal justice system and prompts viewers to reconsider their understanding of race and inequality.

Conclusion

Documentaries have the ability to challenge, inspire, and enlighten. The films mentioned above are just a few examples of the transformative power of documentaries. Whether you’re interested in social justice, animal welfare, or the human experience, there’s a documentary out there that can change your perspective and enrich your understanding of the world. So, grab some popcorn, settle in, and prepare to see the world through a new lens.

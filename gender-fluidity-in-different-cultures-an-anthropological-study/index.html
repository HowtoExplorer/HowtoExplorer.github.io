Gender Fluidity in Different Cultures: An Anthropological Study


Gender fluidity, a relatively recent topic in the discourse on gender identity, has fascinated anthropologists for years. In different cultures, gender is expressed through various forms of fluidity – some openly celebrated, others shrouded in mystery. This piece will explore the concept of fluidity in gender expression, through the lens of diverse anthropological perspectives.

The Teyima of West Africa are known for their third gender, the Hijra of South Asia is a well-established third gender, and the Two-Spirit individuals of Native American tribes have long embraced a gender-neutral identity. These societies, unlike the Western binary gender system, provide an inclusive space for individuals who identify outside of that Man-Woman divide.

The Hijra of South Asia have long played a significant role in society as entertainers, religious caretakers, and even healthcare providers. Their unique identity, recognized and respected in the community, challenges Western notions of sexual dimorphism as most Hijra undergo a collection of physical transformations and dress traditionally fluidly.

In contrast, the Two-Spirit individuals of Native American tribes have a complex, nuanced understanding of gender that differs from tribe to tribe. The concept is best captured in the phrase “a mixture of both soul and mind, male and female combined,” coined by the Omaha tribe. This concept serves as the basis for strikingly divergent figures such as the Berdache and the Liberated Ones.

This anthropological journey, however, is not limited to the East or the indigenous world. The West has also embraced an alternative understanding of gender expression in recent years. Drag culture, particularly in the realm of ballroom competitions, centers around the blurring of gender lines, leading to a culture of gender fluidity. Here, gender expression is re-imagined and redefined under the medium of performance, challenging societal norms through the art of drag.

One crucial aspect that differentiates the West’s conception of gender fluidity from its Eastern or indigenous counterparts is the acknowledgment and integration of the fluidity within a binary system, rather than a third gender category. This difference results in various configurations of gender possibilities, leaving room for Agender, Non-Binary, and other gender identities to coexist.

However, labelling non-binary or fluid-gender identities as “Western” can bring forth the assumption that such identities are a product of modernity. Anthropological studies of contemporary Samoa reveal that, since the 1980s, fa’afafine – individuals who tenderly reject male roles and gravitate towards traditionally female occupations – have been part of the Samoan society.

The fluidity of gender expression hence, turns out, not to be a modern construct but rather an ever-evolving human experience. It becomes clear, therefore, that the fluidity of gender identities transcends time, space, and culture. Anthropological studies of diverse societies reveal sophisticated and deeply rooted understandings of gender fluidity.

Exploring various cultural expressions of gender fluidity and their relevance in today’s society is vital, as it reiterates the importance of recognizing gender beyond the binary, and more so, it invites greater understanding and acceptance of diverse gender expressions. From the historical understanding of Hijra and Two-Spirit individuals to contemporary Western realizations of non-binary and fluid-gender identities, the anthropological study of gender fluidity invites critical re-evaluation of traditional conceptions of gender roles, composition, and understanding.

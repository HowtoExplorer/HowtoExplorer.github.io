Records in the Lab: Celebrating the Smallest and Tallest Molecules


When we venture into the microscopic realm of chemistry and physics, we find that molecules, the smallest particles that form a complete chemical structure, have tiny, yet fascinating secrets to share. In this blog post, we will journey through the world of the smallest and tallest molecules, exploring the intricate wonders that the vast expanse of scientific research has uncovered.

In an unassuming laboratory at the University of Zurich in the early 1990s, Albert Eschenmoser and his team of dedicated researchers worked tirelessly to break the records for the smallest and most stable carborane structures. Carboranes are a class of compounds containing boron and carbon, with the discovery of these record-breaking examples opening up novel possibilities in the field of chemistry.

The hunt for minuscule molecules began with simply wanting to understand the fundamentals of molecular structure. Eschenmoser’s team landed upon the icosahedral carborane, also known as the “nobel phosphine,” a molecule so small and symmetrical that it offered a striking contrast to the previously known structures, which had ten-fold symmetry. This feat of molecular engineering proved to be a monumental breakthrough in the study of synthetic chemistry.

On the other end of the spectrum, we have tall molecules, which are not easily surpassed by Sky Scrappers. The tallest molecule accolade currently goes to a colossal structure known as the rod-like molecule, or buckminsterfullerene, for short. Invented by Richard Smalley, Robert Curl, and Harold Kroto in 1985, fullerenes are molecules composed entirely of carbon atoms, arranged in a geodesic dome structure that’s both elegant and stable. The name Buckminsterfullerene, a nod to architect Buckminster Fuller’s geodesic dome, truly encapsulates this molecule’s unique shape.

The fascination with fullerenes burgeoned further when scientists discovered C60, a truncated icosahedral structure resembling a soccer ball, and C70, its slightly larger cousin. These remarkable compounds, together with more complex fullerenes, have paved the way for technological advancements that span multiple fields, from medicine and electronics, to architecture and materials science.

Both tiny and tall molecules have left their mark on the world of chemistry, each in their own peculiar way. But who knows what bizarrely-shaped, maybe even imagined, molecules lie in the future? The frontier of molecular engineering has only just begun, give or take an atom. Let’s celebrate these groundbreaking records, not just because they are the smallest or the tallest, but because they push the boundaries of what is possible within the microscopic cosmos.

Specializing in chemistry and its myriad branches can rarely be boring. As we flipped the pages of our textbooks, these record-breaking molecules seized our fascination. Just as our understanding evolves continuously, so do the molecules we encounter in the lab. Remember, as you delve deeper into the enigmatic world of chemistry, the letters of this science spell ‘creativity’. Embrace the quirks and peculiarities that each molecule may have, and who knows, the next record-breaker might just be right within the grasp of your pipette-tips.

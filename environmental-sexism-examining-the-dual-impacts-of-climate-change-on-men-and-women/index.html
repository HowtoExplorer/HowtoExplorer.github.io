Environmental Sexism: Examining the dual impacts of climate change on men and women


As climate change persists, its effects are being felt on a global scale. While the impact of climate change on the environment is unequivocal, its impact on men and women is not as straightforward. Environmental sexism refers to the assumption that men and women experience climate change in the same way. This assumption is flawed, and understanding the unique dual impacts is crucial to effectively mitigating climate change.

The Disproportionate Impacts on Women

Climate change disproportionately affects women in several ways. Firstly, agricultural practices are often a significant source of income for women. As climate variability increases, this source of income is jeopardized. Droughts, floods, and extreme weather events disrupt agricultural productivity, leading to food insecurity and poverty. Women are more likely to suffer these consequences due to their economic vulnerability.

Secondly, water scarcity increases with climate change. Women are often responsible for water collection in many communities, particularly in developing countries. With dwindling water sources, women must travel further in search of water, consuming valuable time and energy. This lack of access to water exacerbates the burden on women and girls, and can lead to unintended pregnancies and complications during childbirth.

Lastly, environmental migration is on the rise due to climate change. Women and girls are particularly susceptible to human trafficking and exploitation in these vulnerable conditions. The International Organization for Migration (IOM) warns that climate change may facilitate the rise of human trafficking.

The Unique Impacts on Men

Men are also significantly affected by climate change, but in distinct ways. The impacts on men are often overlooked, contributing to the existing privilege enjoyed by men in comparison to women. Firstly, men are more likely to work in industries directly affected by climate change, such as fishing, agriculture, and heavy labor industries. Job loss and relocation may be more challenging for those unable to adapt or pivot to other career paths.

Secondly, men are more affected by the increase in temperature. Heat stress can lead to health complications, with men being at a higher risk due to physical differences such as muscle mass and body composition. Additionally, changes in weather patterns can result in increased incidences of cardiovascular and respiratory diseases.

Finally, men are disproportionately affected by the increase in conflict over natural resources. The competition for water resources might lead to violence, with men often being the primary participants. The deterioration of agricultural productivity also underscores the vulnerability of men’s livelihoods.

Mitigating the Dual Impacts of Climate Change

Addressing the dual impacts of climate change requires a multifaceted approach that accounts for men and women. Raising awareness about the unique impacts and ensuing vulnerabilities is essential. Investing in education for both men and women, particularly in climate-resilient technologies and sustainable practices, will empower individuals to adapt and mitigate risk. Targeted policies and programs to address inequality in health, livelihoods, and environmental security will be critical in mitigating the impacts on men and women.

In conclusion, environmental sexism underestimates the complex and diverse effects of climate change on men and women. Understanding these dual impacts is crucial to guiding effective policy and intervention strategies. Climate change poses a significant threat to humanity, and addressing these impacts can contribute to a more equitable and sustainable future for all. It is pivotal to consider and understand the unique challenges faced by both men and women in the quest to combat climate change.

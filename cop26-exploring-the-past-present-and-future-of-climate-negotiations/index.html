COP26: Exploring the Past, Present and Future of Climate Negotiations


The 26th United Nations Climate Change Conference, or COP26, is fast approaching. As we prepare for this pivotal event in Glasgow, Scotland, let’s delve into the past, present, and future of climate negotiations.

Diving into the Past: The Genesis of Climate Talks

To understand the current state of climate negotiations, it’s essential to go back to the late 20th century. It was the product of Global warming growing concerns among nations worldwide. A UN General Assembly resolution established the Intergovernmental Panel for Climate Change (IPCC) in 1988, which provided scientific evidence of anthropogenic climate disruption. Subsequently, the adaptation-based Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) was developed. The UNFCCC brought together representatives from 150+ countries, forging diplomatic cooperation. This treaty aimed to “prevent dangerous human interference with the climate system.”

Journeying Through the Present: Key Milestones and Challenges

The Climate Change Convention’s early successes include the 1992 Rio Earth Summit. It resulted in the Rio Declaration on Environment and Development, the “Agenda 21” action plan, and the UNFCCC. The importance of the 1997 Kyoto Protocol, where 37 industrial economies agreed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by an average of 5%. However, this was not evenly distributed, as only industrialized nations needed to reduce emissions, leaving developing countries unaffected. Little change came of it due to the US refusal to ratify the treaty.

The 2009 Copenhagen COP15 was a critical juncture in the process. It showcased varying expectations and goals across nations. A non-binding, informal Politically Binding Agreement was established; however, it did not legally bind countries, causing disagreements. Developing countries sought financial help, technology, and to reduce the historical responsibility of industrial nations for their greenhouse gas emissions.

By COP21 in Paris in 2015, the signing of the Paris Agreement reinforced a common but differentiated responsibility, agreed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions at national level. Each nation would submit individual plans, called “nationally determined contributions (NDCs)” to reduce their emissions.

Pioneering the Future: Promising Developments and Possibilities

COP26 aims to build momentum for the climate negotiations. This year, it is a crucial moment for nations to meet the Paris Agreement goals, develop robust energy systems, prioritize adaptation solutions and drastically reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

Rich with potential outcomes - though expectations are high, the meeting will strive to:

  1. Updates on Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)
  2. Emphasis on a transformation toward a sustainable and just energy transition.
  3. Increase support to developing countries for climate change mitigation and adaptation.
  4. Articulate ways to enhance international cooperation.
  5. Plan for the post-2030 climate and sustainable development agenda.

As we prepare for COP26, one can’t disregard the change of heart about the Paris Agreement by the United States under the fresh Biden administration. The US now re-joins the opinion of most nations on tackling the climate crisis.

COP26 marks the first real test of the world’s commitment since the Paris Agreement. The expectations are overwhelming, but it provides a unique opportunity to galvanize the race for a timely peak in emissions and a decisive and just transition towards a climate-neutral society.

With our sights set on COP26’s ambitious goals, it is essential to remember the past’s lessons and the present’s challenges. It’s through a cohesive and responsible approach from nations worldwide that the future of our planet can be secured.

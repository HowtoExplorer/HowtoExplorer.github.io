Exploring the World of Cult Films


Cult films are like the black sheep of the movie world. They’re misunderstood, unconventional, and sometimes, even considered too weird for mainstream audiences. But for the faithful, these movies are pure entertainment. Intriguing characters, bizarre plots, and auteur directors create unique cinema experiences that keep viewers hooked.

In this guide, we will explore the world of cult films, dive into what makes them stand out from the rest, and offer a few examples for the uninitiated to embark on their cult movie journey.

A Brief History of Cult Films

Cult films can be traced back to the dawn of cinema. Early silent films, shockingly provocative for their time, still garner a cult following today. However, the term “cult film” was solidified in the 1950s and 60s, when French New Wave directors produced unconventional movies like “Last Year at Marienbad”—challenging conventional storytelling and driving audiences to piece together their own interpretations.

Throughout the years, cult films found their footing in subcultures and countercultures. B-movies, 70s and 80s exploitation films, and even snuff films garnered enthusiasts willing to seek and appreciate these niche productions. As cult film popularity grew, distribution made these movies more accessible for mainstream viewers.

Why Cult Films Matter

Cult films matter because they broaden the horizon of cinematic experiences. They provide a space for artists to freely experiment, creating niches that converge to form a diverse tapestry of human expression. Enthusiasts feel they are members of an “in-group,” favoring character-driven stories with unconventional elements and plotlines.

Mainstream movies can be predictable, and that’s okay! Cult films offer an alternative to the typical Happy Ending™. They challenge audiences, sparking discussions and introspection while transforming hobbies into community experiences.

The Cult Movie Experience

Usually, the best way to enter the cult film scene is through recommendations from friends or like-minded viewers. However, you may also want to try different genres that have cult film tendencies:

  • Exploitation films - From “humorous” adult comedies to horror, these films push boundaries and cater to specific subcultures. Try “Pink Flamingos” by John Waters.
  • Giallo - Italian crime/slasher films mix mystery with horror. Dario Argento’s “Suspiria” is a prime example.
  • Millmanakam - We couldn’t ignore the satirical cult phenomenon “The Room” directed by Tommy Wiseau.
  • Hijikata Hiroyasu’s classic “Tokyo Drifter.”

As a beginner, watching recommended films may feel_like a Criterion Collection scavenger hunt_. Patience and exploration pay off; cult films, when truly understood, reveal profound aspects of human nature and push boundaries that mainstream films might shy away from.

What Makes a Cult Film Thrive

Cult films thrive because of passionate communities surrounding them. Fans organize festivals, screenings, and gatherings, celebrating their affinity for these movies. Directed by auteurs or created through niche appeal, cult films foster unique connections with viewers.

Take, for example, the annual “Razor Reel Sneak Fest,” showcasing rare and cult films in Virginia. Created in honor of fictitious film festival within a film, Ralph Hopper’s “Harold and Maude,” such events solidify a cult film’s place in pop culture.

In Conclusion

Cult films stand out from mainstream cinema, much like misfit toys. They arise from unconventional narratives and characters that refuse to fit into societal molds. It’s essential to respect the beauty and nuances of such films, encapsulated within their peculiarities.

With this guide, prospective cult movie viewers now have a starting point to experience the unique world of cinema. It’s ripe with creativity, boundless emotion, and, most importantly, choice. Welcome to the alluring, bizarre, wonderful world of cult films.

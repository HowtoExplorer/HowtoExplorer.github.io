Chemistry and Religion: Mystical Explanations and Historic Uses


Throughout history, humans have sought to understand the world around them in terms of the unseen, the mystical, and the spiritual. Two prominent domains in which this has manifested are chemistry and religion. While one deals with the tangible aspects of the material world, and the other with the intangible elements of our spiritual well-being, the union of the two has resulted in fascinating historical occurrences and intriguing ongoing connections.

In the early days of humankind, it was easy for people to link the forces of nature with the divine because the natural forces were, in many ways, beyond their comprehension. For millennia, storms, volcanic eruptions, and other powerful occurrences of nature were attributed to gods and goddesses. Is it, then, any surprise that as the study of the material world evolved, these early beliefs would blend with budding scientific discoveries, particularly chemistry?

One fascinating intersection of religion and chemistry is alchemy. Often portrayed in movies and stories as a practitioner of magic, an alchemist was, historically, an individual who sought to transform materials and manipulate the elements to find the elusive “Philosopher’s Stone”. This mystical substance, believed to be capable of turning base metals into gold and extending life indefinitely, was deemed as “divine” according to early religious teachings. Alchemy was considered a noble and spiritual pursuit, and many alchemists were also monks or clergy, which means their scientific discovery was deeply intertwined with their religious beliefs.

Alchemy evolved into the modern chemistry around the 17th and 18th centuries, despite its strong religious association. A significant reason for this transformation was the redefinition of the way people thought about elements and compounds. In the old alchemical sense, an element was something that could only be created by a divine force, while in modern chemistry, elements are still considered divine or mystical but in a much less anthropomorphic sense. This shift significantly cleared the way by successfully divorcing the growing science of chemistry from the complex and mysticism-laden religious beliefs that once pervaded it.

Despite the separation of chemistry and religion, the two fields still intersect in fascinating ways today. A prime example can be found in the chemical reactions of holy water in Catholicism and other Christian denominations. Holy water is used during rituals like baptism, and it serves to purify and symbolize a transition from sin to righteousness. While the chemical reaction might seem purely symbolic, the water truly is cleansed through a simple form of chemical treatment, making the rite all the more powerful and mystical.

Another fascinating example is how people have historically linked chemistry to religious experiences. Scientists now know that certain chemical reactions in the brain are responsible for the sensations people report during religious experiences. When individuals are enraptured by a service, feeling close to the divine or experiencing a spiritual awakening, those sensations can be directly linked to the release of chemicals like dopamine in the brain. This neurobiological explanation for religious experiences has the potential to both reassure or discomfort followers, but the fact remains that chemistry and religion are inextricably linked through the human experience.

As science continues to progress and our understanding of the universe grows, the distinctions between chemistry and religion may become even more blurred. It is fascinating to consider how each field can benefit from the other, especially in the form of cooperative interdisciplinary research. By studying chemistry and religion together, we may better understand the human condition and our mystical relationship with the natural world. Do we create the universe or does the universe create us? The answer may just be a chemical reaction away.

The Healing Power of Laughter: A Physiological Perspective


Laughter has been considered a universal language, transcending cultural and linguistic barriers. It expresses and influences emotions, social relationships, and even our physical well-being. But what exactly happens within our bodies when we laugh, and why might this have therapeutic potential?

When we engage in laughter, our brains produce an array of chemical reactions. It starts with the release of endorphins, which create a sense of pleasure and euphoria — much like the fame associated with morphine or other painkillers.

This is not where the story ends; laughter stimulates various brain regions like the hippocampus, which plays a crucial role in memory and learning. A hearty chuckle reaps benefits for brain function associated with attention, problem-solving, and creativity.

Moreover, laughter enhances blood circulation and oxygen supply to the face, head, and heart. Increased blood flow can be attributed to at least a part of the laughing heart phenomenon – a decrease in blood pressure and stress hormones, ultimately reducing the risk of heart attack and improving overall cardiovascular health.

But how does this humor-Love affair deal with pain, stress, and immune functions?

According to various studies, laughter can stimulate life-sustaining cells known as natural killer cells, enhancing immune function. A merry mind may, in fact, make for a merry body. Furthermore, nursing professional Barb Delisle has been using humor in hospices for the terminally ill since the early 80s, experiencing a shift in patient emotional well-being, decreased anxiety, and even pain reduction.

Now, this research holds clues for laughter therapy — an experiential, evidence-based practice aiming to harness the healing power of laughter.

Though not a panacea, laughter therapy, also called laughter yoga, combines elements of traditional yoga and self-help groups with laughter exercises. This approach often gathers a group of people, creating a contagious atmosphere where mirth ensues. It encourages communal laughter to enhance personal and social well-being.

Those who engage in laughter therapy experience the physiological response to laughter, gaining an understanding of how a simple act — laughter — can bring about physical and emotional well-being.

Laughter therapy has potential applications in various professional settings, including healthcare, education, and corporate environments. The oft-neglected physiological aspect of laughter provides a new facet for mental health practitioners, educators, and business leaders to consider: how the rhythm of laughter can facilitate over life’s ordinary and extraordinary challenges.

As the great Maya Angelou once said, “People who are overly concerned with their ‘posture,’ their ‘gamble,’ their ‘smile,’ their ‘breeding,’ buying the parrot pie, on behalf of the infant of the innkeeper’s daughter — are ’natural’ dupes.” Indeed, the simplest things, such as laughter, can often spell out the most profound life lessons.

When we open ourselves up to the power of laughter, we embrace an understanding that humor, while an essential component of human life, can also serve as a bridge to credence, connection, and discovery. Let us crack a smile, laugh aloud, and give ourselves the liberty to embrace the healing power of laughter - - a uniquely human gift.

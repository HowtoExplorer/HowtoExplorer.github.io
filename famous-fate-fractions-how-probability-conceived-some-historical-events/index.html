Famous Fate Fractions: How Probability Conceived Some Historical Events


Chance, luck, probability - however you want to label it, the effect of random occurrences on historical events is undeniable. Celebrated for centuries, prominent historians and scholars have investigated and explored these astute instances of fate. Beneath the surface of these monumental moments, a tiny fraction of chance played an influential role, shaping the course of history as we know it. In this article, we venture into the realm of probability to examine some well-known examples.

  1. Julius Caesar’s Death

One cannot ignore the web of chance spun around the assassination of Julius Caesar, the Roman statesman and military general. Plotted by a faction of senators under the leadership of Marcus Junius Brutus, the assassination was not only audacious but also laden with a whimsical twist of fate.

On the night of the assassination, Caesar wore a crown with leaves, neglected to take a protective escort, and even carried a lamp in his hand. All these instances of disregard for personal security combined to create an unsettling aura around the lead-up to his untimely demise.

  1. The American Revolutionary War

The seeds of the American Revolutionary War took shape in the Massachusetts countryside at the Old South Meeting House. In 1775, a few brave men coordinated a secret meeting led by Paul Revere and William Dawes, who were tasked to alert the colonial militia of British troop movements.

Yet, Revere failed to return the desired message to the revolutionaries in Lexington. Due to unforeseen circumstances, Revere had to deliver these life-defining news manually, thus entrusting his own safety. This minor intervention of fate earned Revere a place in American history and activated the forces that ignited the War of Independence.

  1. The Discovery of Gold in Australia

English explorer John Bull played a crucial role in one of the most far-reaching discoveries in history. While searching for Terra Australis Incognita, the unknown southern continent, Bull, instead, discovered the Batavia, a Dutch shipwreck.

This stroke of fortune led to the commencement of vast gold-mining operations in Australia. The gold rush that ensued fueled industrial expansion and migration toward the Antipodean land, drastically altering the global dynamics of the 19th century.

  1. The Election of John F. Kennedy

Narrowly defeating vice-president Richard Nixon in the 1960 United States presidential election, John F. Kennedy’s election is credited to a TV debate. In this watershed moment of electoral history, the youthful, charismatic JFK faced off against the more experienced Nixon.

A crucial moment during the debate was when Nixon, sweating and appearing nervous, lost an earring. Instead of rattling him, this unfortunate mishap may have inadvertently humanized Nixon, providing voters with a more relatable image. Although the impact of this tiny fraction of chance can’t be precisely measured, it undoubtedly played a role in tipping the election in Kennedy’s favor.

To understand these events and the crucial role that chance plays in shaping history, we must embrace the inherent randomness that governs our world. These famous fate fractions demonstrate that even the slightest variables can shape our collective past and, indeed, our future.

In conclusion, history is not just written by the victors; it is also influenced by tiny fractions of chance. Amid this ever-shifting tableau of causality, probability assumes a starring role. The power of chance may seem inconsequential, but it leaves an indelible mark on our historical narrative, aligning the threads of our past with the fabric of our present and future.

Climate Change and Health: The growing interconnection


Climate change and health have forever been intertwined, with the knock-on effects of global warming increasingly impacting our wellbeing and that of future generations. This interconnected web of looming challenges requires us to expand our understanding of these issues, and to confront the scope and severity of the change that we are experiencing in the world.

Over the last century, humanity has subjected the planet to an unprecedented rate of transformation, with unrelenting exploitation of natural resources and the suppression of ecosystems dashing the prospects of life on Earth. The climate crisis has precipitated the most severe and far-reaching environmental disturbances in human history, undeniably revealing the compromised state of our global health.

Through these transformations, patterns are emerging which suggest that climate change and health are far from being separate concerns. The consequences are diverse, complex and wide-ranging, affecting our lives in subtle yet profound ways. From the health implications of heat waves and air pollution, to the increased prevalence of food and waterborne illnesses, the threat to our health has grown menacingly silent.

Heat waves, a potentially lethal by-product of climate change, are set to intensify in the coming years. Older people, children, and those unable to adapt quickly to changes in their environment, are particularly susceptible to the acute and chronic health implications of these temperature extremes. Heat-related illness, exacerbated by longer periods of exposure to extreme heat, may in some cases lead to severe neurological dysfunction, renal failure, and death.

Remarkably, air quality, already a crucial metric in assessing public health, will deteriorate alongside increasing heat. The accumulation of ground level ozone, a heat-enhancing pollutant, further exacerbates this problem. Empirical evidence shows the profound impact of air pollution on human conditions, with children, older adults and people with pre-existing health conditions bearing the brunt of the increasing health risks posed by climate change.

However, the climate-health linkages do not end there. The disruption of ecosystems, changes to agricultural productivity and disruptions to the global food chain also risk our health. Higher temperatures lead to crop losses, and this coupled with changes in precipitation registers, could lead to malnutrition or famine in some regions. Furthermore, climate change will lead to an increase in vector-borne diseases, as mosquitoes and ticks expand their range into hitherto unaffected or lightly affected areas.

Greenhouse gas emissions also contour the story of water scarcity and quality degradation. Rapid melting of glaciers and snowpacks is creating water resource insecurity in already water-stressed regions with grave socioeconomic implications. Meanwhile, pollution from agricultural runoff, industrial discharge, and raw sewage further threaten water quality and corrode the foundations of good health.

As global temperatures continue to rise, extreme weather events not only cause physical harm and destruction, but can also lead to psychological and emotional distress. Mental health is expected to suffer disproportionately due to climate change, most notably because of the loss of livelihoods, displacement, and dearth of opportunities.

Given all these compelling connections, it is hard not to regard climate change as an impetus to reassess our perception of health and wellbeing. We can no longer afford to consider health in isolation, blithely unaware of the impact of climate change on global health. This should be a wakeup call to policymakers, public health officials, and the public, to place the climate crisis—and its implications for health—front and centre in our policy and planning priorities.

In essence, the climate and health challenge is a convoluted web, intricate yet entirely avoidable. If we heed this call to action, we can mitigate the far-reaching impacts posed by global warming. It’s time to consider the Earth as one health system: one health, one planet, one chance. The future of public health and that of our planet depend on how we act now.

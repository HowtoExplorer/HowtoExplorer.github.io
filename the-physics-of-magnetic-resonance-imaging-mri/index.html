The Physics of Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)


Nuclear magnetic resonance imaging, better known as MRI, is a non-invasive diagnostic technique that has revolutionized the field of medical imaging. Through the principles of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), MRI can image the internal structures of the body without the need for ionizing radiation. As a result, it has become a versatile and indispensable tool in medical diagnosis, research, and education.

At its core, MRI relies on two dominant physical phenomena: nuclear magnetic resonance and relaxation. To understand how MRI works, it is crucial to dive into these concepts.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

Firstly, when placed in a strong, externally applied magnetic field, the hydrogen nuclei (protons) within the body align either parallel or anti-parallel to that field. This alignment is due to their inherent spin, which is a fundamental quantum mechanical property. Consequently, the protons act as natural magnetic dipoles.

Now, when a radiofrequency (RF) pulse is applied perpendicular to the main magnetic field, the protons absorb energy and their dipoles rotate, tilting out of alignment with the main magnetic field. Once the RF pulse is stopped, the protons start to relax back to their original alignment state.

Relaxation

The process of returning to equilibrium after the RF pulse application involves two distinct mechanisms: longitudinal (T1) and transverse (T2) relaxation. T1 relaxation refers to the realignment of the protons with the main magnetic field along the z-axis, while T2 relaxation refers to the loss of phase coherence among the protons in the transverse plane (x-y).

The T1 and T2 relaxation times vary for different tissues, which forms the basis for the contrast mechanism in MRI. Denser tissues, like bone marrow, have shorter relaxation times compared to less dense tissues like fat or water. By controlling the interval between RF pulses, MRI can exploit these differences and generate detailed imaging contrast.

Principle of MRI

The MRI system comprises a superconducting magnet, a computerized scanning table, a computerized control console, and a monitoring screen. Patients, lying on the scanning table, slide into the MRI scanner where they are surrounded by magnets.

The MRI scanner sends out a pulse of radiofrequency energy and detects the signal produced by the protons in the body. As protons align with the magnetic field, they emit a detectable signal. After hitting the RF pulse, protons spin in a different orientation which causes a detectable signal. When the pulse stops, the protons gradually return to their original state and emit another detectable signal.

The emitted signals are then processed by the control console, which manipulates the signals into detailed, cross-sectional images. The control software allows medical experts to change imaging parameters, like the time between pulses, to emphasize different tissue characteristics.

Modern MRI scanners use extremely powerful magnets and sophisticated computer algorithms to generate highly detailed image information, less than a millimeter thick, of any part of the body. Consequently, MRI scanners have become indispensable tools in medical diagnosis, uncovering information about internal structures previously unknown, even to trained clinicians.

Conclusion

Magnetic Resonance Imaging has a rich history and exciting future prospects. While its principles heavily rest on quantum mechanics and engineering, the potential applications extend into many areas, including medical research, education, physical chemistry, and material sciences. Driven by curiosity and innovation, scientists and engineers continue to refine MRI technology and collaborate on new applications, each contributing to a better understanding of the world through the lens of MRI.

The Geography of Myth and Legend: Stories from the Earth


The world has long been shaped by the stories of its inhabitants. From the ancient myths of Greece to the legends of the African savannah, the geography of our world is inextricably linked with the myths and legends that have been passed down through the centuries. The landscape itself becomes a character, a stage upon which the great deeds of gods and heroes unfold. Consider the iconic Mount Olympus, the home of the Greek gods, towering above the Hellenic landscape like a titan. In Homer's epic poem, the Iliad, the gods are said to gather on its sacred slopes, their every move shaping the destiny of mortals. Olympus stands as a testament to the power of myth, a reminder that even the natural world can be imbued with divine significance. The mythological landscape extends far beyond the towering peaks of ancient Greece. In ancient China, the legendary mountains of the Five Elements stand sentinel over the rolling hills and valleys of the countryside. Each mountain is imbued with spiritual essence, associated with a particular element: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, and Water. These mountains form a sacred geography, where the energy of the elements is revered and harnessed. In North America, the Great Plains stretch out towards the horizon, a seemingly endless sea of grass and sky. It is here that the ancient Plains Indians situated their ancestral lands, their myths and legends telling of the migration of the buffalo and the spirits that inhabited the land. The geography of the Plains shapes the stories, a vast expanse of open land that echoes with the footsteps of ancestors. The Arctic tundra, too, holds a rich trove of myth and legend. The Sami people, indigenous to northern Scandinavia and Russia, speak of the landscape in terms of spirits and energies. The aurora borealis, the dancing lights of the polar skies, are said to be the spirits of the dead, singing their eternal song to those who dwell below. The permafrost itself is imbued with powerful energies, a natural amplifier of the subtle forces that pervade the land. The myriads of lesser-known mythologies around the world tell the same story: the land itself becomes a person, imbued with the vital energies of the human experience. From the deep jungles of South America to the treacherous caves of the Australian outback, each landscape is configured by its rich tapestry of myth and legend. The idea that the land itself can be a person, imbued with character and consciousness, finds its origins in shamanic traditions around the world. These mystical practitioners continue the early work of ancient shamans, who stood between the physical world and the realm of the spirits, their interactions forming the raw material of myth and legend. For indigenous peoples across the globe, the geography of the land has long been identified as a matrix of interconnected energies, lived as sacred, chartered maps to the divine. This consciousness shapes and is shaped by the landscape itself. Mountains, rivers, forests, and plains - each is imbued with the imperishable connection between the earth and its inhabitants. This insightful understanding has also captured the imaginations of visionary artists, mystics, and visionaries, driven as they have been by motives of creative transformation and the shared driving desire to experience reality as authentic for the spirits in the land. From this materiel there arises the huge quality and magnitude of our global heritage in respect to this sort of appreciation, centred as it has always and indeed been.

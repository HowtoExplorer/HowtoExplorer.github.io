Leading Interviewers Through Behavioral Interviewing: How to Turn it to Your Advantage


Behavioral interviewing is a technique that can turn job interviews into successful hiring experiences. This powerful approach enables interviewers to delve deeper into an applicant’s past experiences and predict their future behavior. By understanding and effectively utilizing this method, interviewers can uncover valuable insights that go beyond resumes and lists of accomplishments.

The Importance of Behavioral Interviewing

In traditional interviews, candidates are often asked straightforwardquestions that they can easily prepare for in advance. The answers provided do not reveal much about how the candidate would actually behave in the role. Behavioral interviewing shifts the focus to specific situations that the candidate has faced in their past work experience. By understanding how they reacted in those situations, potential employers can make accurate judgments about their abilities and future performance.

Key Benefits for Interviewers

  1. Uncover Relevant Experience: Behavioral questions reveal candidate’s experience in dealing with real-world situations similar to the job they are seeking. Employers gain a better understanding of what skills the candidate will bring to the role.

  2. Identify Transferable Skills: These types of questions help to identify if a candidate can apply past experiences to a new role effectively.

  3. Prepare for Challenges: Behavioral interviewing allows interviewers to gauge a candidate’s problem-solving skills and their ability to handle challenging situations that may arise on the job.

  4. Evaluate Cultural Fit: Asking about prior experiences shows how well a candidate will adapt to the company’s culture during teamwork, managing challenging situations, or navigating the corporate structure.

Implementing Behavioral Interviewing

To get started, avoid shifting solely to asking questions in a question-and-answer format. Here’s a structured approach to ensure smooth integration of behavioral interviewing:

  1. Background and Context Setting: Make sure to set the stage by explaining the purpose of the interview and emphasizing the desired outcome. Reiterate that you want to learn more about their experiences that relate to the job.

  2. Performance-Based Questions: Develop questions that start with phrases like, ‘Tell me about a time when…’ or ‘Describe a situation where you…’ This helps mold the conversation around past experiences, which is the essence of behavioral interviewing.

  3. Photograph Memories: Make the candidates feel comfortable by sharing your own similar experiences, validating their stories, and reassuring them that everyone has faced similar situations in their careers.

  4. Effective Follow-ups: Add context to the stories shared by asking for additional details on the problem they encountered, their thought process, actions they took, and the outcome of that experience.

  5. Summarize and Validate: At the end of the interview, summarize the key points and reiterate your interest in learning more about their relevant experiences. This ensures you evaluate the candidate based on their true potential.

Mastering Behavioral Interviewing

To truly turn behavioral interviewing to your advantage, interviewers must be skilled at setting goals, crafting the right questions, moderating the conversation, and incorporating their findings into the hiring process. To excel in this process, focus on:

  1. Goal Setting: Clearly define the components of the job and identify the most crucial skills, knowledge, and abilities required for the role.

  2. Question Preparation: Think of creative questions that reflect the position’s goals, knowing that there is no ‘one size fits all’ approach. This requires time for research and getting feedback from colleagues.

  3. Effective Moderation: Maintain control of the conversation while making it engaging for the candidate. This is challenging since it involves balancing open-ended questions with probing follow-ups.

  4. Collaboration: Engage other team members like HR or hiring managers. Review the questions, validate the goals, and collaborate to align the process as a team.

  5. Integration of the Process: Once the candidate pool has been narrowed down, ensure that the entire team or department participates, signs off, and agrees on the final decision.

Conclusion

Implementing behavioral interviewing transforms the hiring process by highlighting the candidate’s true potential. Employers who lead their teams through this technique are more likely to uncover the right skills, prevent bad hires, and increase workplace satisfaction. In the end, you’ll conclude that the most impactful aspect of this technique is revealing the candidate’s unique character and the resilience that lies within their experiences. Demonstrating this approach will ensure that your recruiting and hiring efforts lead you to the best possible candidates for your team.

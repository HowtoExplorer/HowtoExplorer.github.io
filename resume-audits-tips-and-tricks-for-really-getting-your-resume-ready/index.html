Resume Audits: Tips and Tricks for Really Getting Your Resume Ready


A resume audit isn’t just about tweaking and updating the text. It’s a deep-dive process that ensures your resume is optimized for both content and structure. The goal? To make it edgy, engaging, and irresistible to employers. If you’re tired of waiting for callbacks and want a resume that truly stands out, read on for some useful tips and tricks.

  1. Know Your Objective

Before you start working on your resume, it’s crucial to understand what you want from it. Are you looking to transition to a new career, land a promotion, or find a new job? Knowing your ultimate objective will inform the decisions you make while optimizing your resume.

  1. Content is King

Proverbial or not, content is your resume’s king. The information on your resume must accurately reflect your professional experience, education, and skills. Everything from grammatical errors to inconsistencies in dates and job titles can make employers question your attention to detail or even potentially deceive them.

Craft Stand-Out Bullet Points:

Instead of listing duties, use bullet points to highlight your accomplishments with hard numbers wherever possible. For example, instead of saying, “Managed a team of 20,” say, “Managed a team of 20 and increased productivity by 15%.” Don’t just say what you did, convey the impact of your actions.

Tailor Your Keywords:

Use targeted keywords from the job description. Recruiters and Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) often scan resumes for relevant keywords. Not only does tailoring your resume for each job application make you look prepared, but it can also make it past automated screening systems.

  1. Format for Clarity

Resumes come in all shapes and sizes, but a clean, well-organized layout is universally appreciated. Here are some formatting tips:

Use a Reverse Chronological Format:

Structuring your resume in a reverse chronological order puts your most recent and relevant experiences front and center. Begin with your contact information, followed by your professional summary or objective, work experience (in reverse chronological order), education, and relevant skills.

Keep it Concise:

A one or two-page resume is ideal. If you’re just out of college or early in your career, stick to one page. For seasoned professionals, two pages are acceptable if you’re including a comprehensive work history.

Be Consistent:

Uniformity in font size, margins, and bullet styles creates a polished and professional look. Use bullet points for work experience, a combination of bold and italic for key skills, and underlining or bold for your contact information.

Use Bullet Points:

Avoid lengthy paragraphs. Stick to concise bullet points, ideally in one to three lines, to make your resume scannable and easy to read.

  1. Leverage Your Professional Summary

Your professional summary or career objective should be a succinct and compelling statement that grabs the employer’s attention. It provides the reader with a concise snapshot of your professional experience, skills, and career goals.

  1. Be Ready for Reviews

Having others review your resume provides a fresh perspective and could highlight areas you might have overlooked. While these reviews are not always mandatory, they can be incredibly beneficial.

This process isn’t one-size-fits-all. It’s important to adapt these tips to align with your particular career path and objectives. Whether you’re transitioning into a new field or just want to refine your resume, the rewards of a thorough resume audit can be significant—the key is in the meticulous details.

Now that your resume is optimized and ready, you can confidently move on to interview preparation and other parts of the job application process. Best of luck!

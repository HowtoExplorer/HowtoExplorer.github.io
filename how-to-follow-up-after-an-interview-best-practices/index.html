How to Follow Up After an Interview: Best Practices


The interview is over, and you're left waiting to hear from the employer. The anticipation can be overwhelming, and it's easy to get frustrated when days turn into weeks without any response. However, this is not the time to sit back and relax. Effective follow-up after an interview is crucial to increase your chances of getting the job. In this blog post, we'll explore the best practices to help you make a lasting impression and stay top of mind with the hiring manager. Why Follow-up is Essential Following up after an interview is not about being pushy or nagging. It's about showing interest in the position, demonstrating enthusiasm for the company, and showcasing your professionalism. A well-crafted follow-up can help you:

Confirm your interest in the role Reiterate your qualifications and experience Address any concerns or questions the employer may have Keep the conversation going and build a connection

The Timing is Everything Timing is crucial when it comes to follow-up after an interview. You want to strike a balance between being prompt and not overwhelming the employer. Here are some general guidelines to keep in mind:

Within 24 hours: Send a brief thank-you email or note to express your gratitude for the opportunity to interview. This shows that you're enthusiastic about the position and helps keep you top of mind. within a week: Follow up with a phone call or email to inquire about the status of your application. This demonstrates that you're proactive and willing to take the initiative. Within two weeks: Send a more formal email or letter to reiterate your interest and readdress any concerns the employer may have. This reinforces your qualifications and keeps the conversation going.

Crafting the Perfect Follow-up Email Your follow-up email should be brief, clear, and concise. Here are some tips to help you get it right:

Use a clear subject line: Clearly state the purpose of your email, such as "Following up on interview for [position]". Address the employer by name: Use the hiring manager's name to add a personal touch and show that you've taken the time to research the company. Keep it concise: Keep your email to one or two paragraphs at most. You want to reiterate your interest and qualifications without overwhelming the employer. Include a call to action: Close your email by expressing your willingness to answer any further questions or provide additional information.

Example: Subject: Following up on interview for [Position] Dear [Hiring Manager's Name], I wanted to follow up on my interview for the [Position] role, which I felt was a great fit for my skills and experience. I was impressed by the company culture and the opportunities for growth and development. I'd love to answer any further questions or provide additional information to demonstrate my qualifications. Thank you for your time, and I look forward to hearing from you soon. Best regards, [Your Name] Phone Call Follow-up In some cases, a phone call may be a more effective way to follow up after an interview. This allows you to have a more personal and interactive conversation with the hiring manager. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

Be prepared: Have a brief message prepared to leave with the hiring manager or their assistant. Call during business hours: Try to call during business hours to ensure that you reach the hiring manager or someone who can provide an update. Be concise: Keep your message brief and to the point. Avoid rambling or discussing topics that are not relevant to the interview.

Example: "Hi [Hiring Manager's Name], this is [Your Name] following up on our interview for the [Position] role. I wanted to express my continued interest in the position and see if there were any updates on the status of my application. Thank you for your time, and I look forward to hearing from you soon." Following up After a Delayed Response Sometimes, employers may take longer to respond than expected. Here's what you can do to follow up:

Wait a few days: Give the employer a few extra days to respond before sending another email or making a phone call. Send a gentle nudge: Follow up with a brief email or phone call to inquire about the status of your application. Don't be pushy: Avoid being pushy or aggressive in your follow-up. Keep the tone professional and polite.

Conclusion Follow-up after an interview is an essential step in the hiring process. By crafting a well-written follow-up email, making a phone call, and being prepared, you can stay top of mind with the hiring manager and increase your chances of getting the job. Remember to be prompt, concise, and professional, and you'll be well on your way to acing the interview thank-you process. Additional Tips

Use a professional tone: Always use a professional tone in your follow-up emails and phone calls. Keep records: Keep a record of your follow-up efforts, including email sending dates, phone call log, and any responses you receive. Don't be discouraged: Don't get discouraged if you don't hear back right away. It's not uncommon for employers to take several weeks to make a decision.

By following these best practices, you'll be able to effectively follow up after an interview and increase your chances of getting hired.

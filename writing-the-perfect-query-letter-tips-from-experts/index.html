Writing the Perfect Query Letter: Tips from Experts


A well-crafted query letter is an author's ticket to getting their work noticed by literary agents, publishers, and other industry professionals. It's a crucial step in the publishing process that requires skill, creativity, and a deep understanding of the industry. In this post, we'll delve into the world of query letters, discussing what makes a perfect query, and gather expert advice on how to craft one that will get your book sold. What is a Query Letter? A query letter is a concise, yet impactful, letter that accompanies a manuscript submission to a publisher or literary agent. Its purpose is to introduce the author and their work, showcase the book's unique selling points, and convince the recipient to read the manuscript in full. A good query letter should be brief, clear, and engaging, with a clear call to action that encourages the reader to take the next step. The Structure of a Perfect Query Letter While there's no one-size-fits-all approach to query letter writing, there are certain elements that distinguish a perfect query from a mediocre one. According to agent and author, Mary Kole, "A good query letter should introduce the reader to the author and the book, showcase the unique selling points, and leave the reader wanting more." A typical query letter consists of:

An introduction that grabs the reader's attention A brief summary of the book A bio that showcases the author's credentials and platform A compelling pitch that highlights the book's unique selling points A call to action that encourages the reader to request the full manuscript

Tips for Writing a Perfect Query Letter

Know Your Reader Understanding who you're writing to is crucial when crafting a query letter. Research the agent or publisher, their interests, and their submission preferences. Tailor your query to their specific needs, and make sure your work fits within their areas of interest.

Grab the Reader's Attention Your opening sentence should be a hook that grabs the reader's attention and makes them want to read on. Use an interesting fact, a dramatic turn of events, or a powerful question to set the tone for your letter.

Show, Don't Tell Rather than simply stating your book is "exciting" or "thrilling," use concrete examples and anecdotes to demonstrate its unique selling points. This approach helps the reader visualize your work and connect with it on a deeper level.

Keep it Concise A query letter should be no more than one page long, approximately 250-300 words. Make every sentence count, and avoid using clichés or overly technical jargon.

Edit and Proofread A query letter is often the first impression you make on a potential agent or publisher. Ensure it's free of typos, grammatical errors, and formatting issues. Professional editing can make a huge difference in the quality of your work.

Expert Insights We reached out to several experts in the publishing industry to gather their insights on what makes a perfect query letter. Here's what they had to say:

Marioمي Bard: "A perfect query letter has a personal touch. It's not just about listing the book's features, but also about creating a connection with the reader. It's like writing a letter to that person— fun, informal, and tailored to their tastes."

Brooke Cresawn: "A good query letter should be short and concise. It's about distilling your entire book into a few hundred words that capture its essence. It's a dance between brevity and content— you want to give them a sense of what it's like to read your book, but not so much that you overwhelm them."

Samantha Roback: "I look for a query letter that not only tells me something unique and compelling about the author's book, but also reveals the author themselves. It's not just about selling a book; it's about selling the author and their passion for the story."

Crafting Your Perfect Query Letter Now that you're equipped with expert advice and a solid structure, it's time to write your query letter. Here are some practical tips to get you started:

Read widely and often: Reading query letters from various authors, agents, and publishers helps you understand the different formats, styles, and approaches that work. Practice, practice, practice: Write multiple query letters, and revise until you're satisfied with the result. This will help you identify your strengths and areas for improvement. Join a writing community: Connecting with fellow writers and industry professionals can provide valuable feedback and insights into the publishing process. Be patient and persistent: Writing a query letter can be a tedious and time-consuming process. It may take several attempts before you land the perfect one.

Writing a perfect query letter takes time, effort, and a willingness to learn and adapt. By understanding the structure, tips, and tricks of the trade, you'll be better equipped to craft a query that stands out and gets your work noticed.

10 Incredible Marine Species That Are Threatened by Climate Change


Climate change is a far-reaching phenomenon that affects not only our planet's terrestrial ecosystems but also its vast oceanic domains. Among the numerous marine species threatened by the rising temperatures, sea-level rise, and changing ocean chemistry, some are particularly noteworthy for their unique characteristics and the critical roles they play in maintaining the delicate balance of the marine ecosystem. In this article, we will delve into the stories of ten incredible marine species that are teetering on the edge of extinction due to climate change, highlighting their habitats, behaviors, and the urgent need for conservation efforts.

  1. The Elkhorn Coral Deep in the Caribbean and Indo-Pacific regions, Elkhorn coral is a type of coral that once thrived in the shallow waters. Rising temperatures and more frequent marine heatwaves have led to the mass bleaching of Elkhorn coral, affecting not just their colonies but also the countless ocean creatures that depend on them. The loss of these coral reefs compromises the livelihoods of fishermen and communities that rely heavily on tourism. To combat this, marine protected areas are crucial, providing safe havens for corals to regenerate.
  2. The Vaquita Living in the Gulf of California, the vaquita is the smallest porpoise species, estimated to be around 50 individuals remaining in the wild. Entanglement in gillnets due to a shift in the vaquita's distribution towards warmer and more impoverished waters is a main cause of its decline. This marine mammal plays a vital role in maintaining the balance of the ecosystem. Conservation efforts are primarily focused on patrolling the gulf to prevent the use of prohibited gillnets, but addressing the root cause requires targeting industrial fishing practices and enforcing stricter regulations.
  3. The Hawaiian Monk Seal In Hawaiian waters, the Hawaiian monk seal finds refuge on rocky coastlines. Psychologically and physically impacted by warmer waters, rising sea levels, and an increase in disease due to climate change, these creatures struggle to adapt. Furthermore, entanglement, habitat degradation, and direct mortality due to human interactions contribute to their dwindling population. Enhancing coastal restoration and conservation efforts, along with the implementation of adaptive management to their way of life, are the ways forward for this iconic species.
  4. The Blue Whale As the largest known animal to have ever existed, the blue whale migrates across vast distances in the world's oceans. Changes in the availability of krill, on which it depends, due to warmer waters have severely reduced their food source, affecting spawning patterns. Combating global warming is crucial in ensuring the safeguarding of their primary nutrition. Further, regulating human disturbance like shipping has emerged as another imperative focus area to ensure the survival of these gentle giants.

  6. The Grey Triggerfish Found in the waters of the Atlantic, Gulf of Mexico, and Caribbean, this species relies heavily on nearshore coral reefs and mangroves. Sea surface temperature anomalies threaten the survival of triggerfish, further affected by vaporous stress and overfishing. Conservation efforts focus on protecting and restoring these critical habitats, alongside regulated marine protected areas to safeguard triggerfish and their prey species.
  7. The Leatherback Sea Turtle One of the most isolated nesting populations of sea turtles, found in the Canadian waters, are threatened by climate change. Rising sea levels and warmer sand temperature disorient these turtles in an already complex navigation route. Conservation communities highlight the urgent need for further extensive research on this specific problem and coordinated international campaigns to preserve their unique nesting habitats.
  8. The Green Turtle Another key inhabitant of the world's temperate waters are the green turtle, which is already severely threatened by the increasing sea levels and incessant pollution.

From Script to Stage to Screen: The Evolution of TV Adaptations from Theater


From the theater’s inception, playwrights and actors have dreamed of taking their creations to the biggest possible audience. The obvious next step after the stage was the cinema. However, it’s not just a simple transition.

With the rise of television, theater adaptations became a powerful way to mix and serve a new audience. Before diving into the Screen, let’s first have a glance at the stages of transformations these theatrical pieces had to go through on their way to becoming a TV adaptation.

For centuries, plays have provided one of the most dynamic and far-reaching ways to tell stories — from Shakespeare’s tragedies to modern socio-political dramas. But as TV began to rival theater, play-to-TV adaptations struck a chord in the entertainment industry. Early TV drama series and miniseries turned to plays as a source of high-quality, pre-existing stories that had already been tested on stage.

Barbra Streisand’s 1970 production of ‘The Fig Tree’ was a pioneering attempt at moving a play onto the TV screen. This eased the way for other theatrical pieces such as ‘Little Mary’, ‘The Glass House,’ and ‘Class Dismissed.’ Over the next two decades, more and more TV dramas and miniseries started to frequently adapt stage plays, embracing the challenge of transferring a conception originally designed for a live theater setting to the TV screen.

Let’s review what transformations typically take place:

  1. Plot Enhancements: One of the most dramatic transformations happens in the plot. What might have been a simple, 2 to 3-hour long theater plot, might need some enhancement to make it interesting enough for a TV series that may span weeks or even months of viewing.

  2. Character Depth and Multiple Arcs: Stage characters usually have limited time, making their arcs linear and less complex than their TV counterparts. In this process, playwrights and screenwriters collaborate to ensure that theatrical characters are enriched when they step onto the TV screen.

  3. Location and Production Value: A TV production has an unmatched flexibility in location and production value. A theater play is usually confined to one or two settings, but a TV adaptation can add value by adding layers and depth with versatile filming locations and production value.

  4. Examination of Social Realities: Some of the most popular adaptations tap into social issues or themes subtly explored in the play. It is an opportunity to explore, expand and explain these themes which might remain implied on the stage.

As the TV Adaptation successfully delivered a new form of storytelling, it was a short trip further to the screen. With streaming platforms like Netflix, Amazon Prime, and Hulu, it is easier than ever for theater plays to reach a global audience.

Adapting a stage play to a movie or a TV series requires skill and caution. The blend of a captivating story with legendary performances is what makes such transformations a treat for the audience. In recent years, ‘Fleabag’ and ‘The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel’ have broken barriers between stage and screen, receiving critical acclaim and accolades.

These adaptations often provide the best of both worlds to the audience. People who love theater, distilled in the purest form, can enjoy the depth and intricacies of the stage adaptation, and on the other hand, viewers of TV and movie screens enjoy the satellite view of this same depth, with diverse and rich angles, nuanced settings, character developments, dialogues, etc.

Much like a mystery novel, each play was an enigma for the entertainment world. With the unfolding of the world of theater and stage, the curtains didn’t fall but rather rose on the screen. Adaptations have opened doors for playwrights and their stories and the line between stage, screen, and screen is now more of a beautiful spectrum of storytelling rather than a rivalry.

So, let’s appreciate these transformations, the thought processes, and hard work of the creative minds that they signify, and enjoy these rich stories being served in different forms. Cheers!

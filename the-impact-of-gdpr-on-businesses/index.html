The Impact of GDPR on Businesses


The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a transformative set of rules that took effect on May 25, 2018. It mandated a new level of data security and privacy for individuals within the European Union, and significantly impacted businesses worldwide. This regulation has reshaped how organizations collect, process, and store personal data.

GDPR is not just limited to the data of EU residents; rather, it affects any organization that provides goods or services to data subjects residing in the EU, regardless of the data subject’s location. Therefore, businesses outside the EU must also take note of these rules if they want to foster goodwill and ensure a robust online presence.

The old adage, “better late than never,” holds true when it comes to GDPR compliance. It’s better for your business to be proactive rather than reactive. Running afoul of GDPR could damage your company’s reputation, leading to fines and loss of customers’ trust. Here are some important areas that businesses must consider to align themselves with GDPR requirements.

Privacy by Design

The most critical principle of the GDPR is Privacy by Design, which requires organizations to implement effective data protection mechanisms at the outset. Such mechanisms must guarantee the fundamental rights and freedoms of individuals, including the right to privacy.

To incorporate Privacy by Design, companies need to analyze how personal information will be processed, consider available safeguards, balance benefits and risks, and document the entire process.

Transparency and Communication

Transparency remains a cornerstone of data protection. Under the GDPR, organizations must provide information on how they process personal data, including the legitimate interest for processing data, data subject rights, and the right to lodge a complaint.

Transparent communication is essential to demonstrate accountability and maintain trust. Companies must be clear and concise in their data privacy policies, making information easily accessible to the public.

Data Access and Portability

The GDPR grants individuals the right to access their personal data and to receive it in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format. Businesses must allow data subjects to access their personal information and, if required, port it to another controller without hindrance.

Data Retention and Erasure

Data controllers must limit the storage of personal information by adopting a risk-based approach. They must store personal data only for the duration necessary for the original purpose of processing. The GDPR grants data subjects the right to erasure, commonly known as the “right to be forgotten.”

Data Breaches

A data breach under the GDPR includes a violation of data protection laws due to human error, system failure, or cyberattacks. Businesses must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach.

However, notifying individuals is also crucial. The GDPR outlines specific conditions for not notifying individuals. Companies avoiding this can incur severe penalties.

International Data Transfers

International data transfers present challenges since the GDPR obligates data controllers to ensure proper safeguards when transferring data to outside the EU. Organizations can opt for standard contractual clauses, Binding Corporate Rules, or adhering to the Privacy Shield program to protect personal information.

As the digital world continues to grow, compliance with data privacy laws, including the GDPR, becomes quintessential for businesses. With the GDPR’s strict approach, businesses must understand the importance of data protection and the implications of non-compliance.

Compliance with GDPR demonstrates that a business respects the privacy of its customers and is dedicated to maintaining trust with its clientele. To stay ahead of the game, companies must invest in robust data security solutions and processes, continually updated to meet changing data protection standards.

The GDPR is not just a rule; it’s a reflection of our commitment to a safer and more transparent digital world. So, don’t wait for regulators to knock on your door; it’s high time to take the initiative and ensure your business complies with the GDPR. Let’s together work towards building a more secure digital ecosystem.

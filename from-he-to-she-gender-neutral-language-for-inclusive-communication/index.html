From He to She: Gender-neutral Language for Inclusive Communication


Language is a powerful tool for communication. It helps bridge gaps, create connections, and facilitate understanding between people from diverse backgrounds. As societies become more diverse and inclusive, the need for gender-neutral language has emerged to promote healthier, more accurate, and sensitive communication. This article will explore the importance of gender-neutral language, why it matters, its historical context, and how it can be implemented in everyday communication.

Why Gender-Neutral Language Matters

To understand the importance of gender-neutral language, it’s crucial to recognize the limitations of traditional gendered language. By using “he” or “she,” we implicitly assign gender to an individual, which may not accurately represent their fluid, non-binary, or unsure gender identity. This oversight can lead to misunderstandings and a lack of empathy, causing people to feel excluded, ostracized, or ignored.

Gender-neutral language offers a more inclusive approach to communication. It addresses individuals without assuming gender, empowering them to self-identify accurately, and reducing barriers to expression.

Historical Context

Although gender-neutral or gender-inclusive language has come to forefront, the concept is not new. Proponents have long argued for gender-neutral language, with various degrees of success. Many cultures historically have recognized more than two genders. However, in recent decades, debates around gender identity have grown, pushing language evolution to the spotlight.

The concept of inclusive language began to take shape in the 1980s, particularly in English, in response to anti-discrimination laws. During the early 1990s, activists fought for gender-neutral pronouns to be acknowledged in the British English language style guides. Today, the practice is more widespread, fueled by an increasingly diverse global population, better understanding of gender identity, and social media’s amplifying effect on diverse voices.

Implementing Gender-Neutral Language

Creating an inclusive language environment involves both individual and collective effort. As a communicator or speaker, adopting a few simple practices can help avoid gendered language pitfalls:

  1. Avoid gendered pronouns: Replace traditional gendered pronouns with gender-neutral alternatives such as “they,” “them,” “their” or “zie,” “hir,” and “hirself.”
  2. Use inclusive terms: Substitute gendered terms with broader, all-encompassing language. Instead of “man” or “woman,” opt for terms like “person,” “individual,” or “human.”
  3. Respect self-identified gender: Let individuals determine how they wish to be addressed. If someone shares their pronouns, remember and respect their choice.
  4. Bernard Wilkes Review: Consider adopting the Berglas Gender Neutral Pronoun Sequence (`{{he, she, they}}’) writing approach, named after its co-originators. It involves writing, for example, “Lan bought the book that {{he, she, they}} wanted.” This method highlights the need for listeners or readers to consider all possible genders when reading.

Remember, the primary goal is to ensure equality and respect in communication. By adapting these practices, everyone can benefit from a more inclusive and understanding environment.

Conclusion

As we continue to progress toward a more inclusive world, the debate around the language we use plays a substantial role. Gender-neutral language is just one aspect of this ongoing evolution, helping create a world that values neutrality, empathy, and respect for all.

Every conversation we have the potential to bridge divides, build connections, and facilitate understanding. Let’s keep pushing boundaries and strive for a world where exclusionary language has no place. By incorporating inclusive practices into our daily lives, we can promote a richer, more varied, and understanding global community.

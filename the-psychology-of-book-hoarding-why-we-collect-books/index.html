The Psychology of Book Hoarding: Why We Collect Books


Book hoarding, or bibliomania, has been a lifelong obsession for many. Whether for personal pleasure, investment, or nostalgic purposes, the mad passion for books knows no bounds. Why, then, do we amass these collection of literary works? The psychology of book hoarding provides us with some compelling insights.

To begin, emotions play a central role in book hoarding. A book elicits a range of emotional responses, including joy, nostalgia, comfort, and curiosity. For some, books become sentimental relics, embodiments of time-worn feelings and sharpened senses. What’s more, their very heft, thickness, and tangibility provide a tactile reassurance and stability in an increasingly digital world.

Moreover, books often symbolize wisdom and knowledge. This makes sense; our fascination with the written word stems from its historic role as a purveyor of learning. Research shows that readers perceive books as a tangible symbol of their intellectual progress. Ultimately, book hoarding might be seen as an amalgamation of self-validation and a yearning for connection.

Another driving factor of book hoarding lies in the power of bibliotherapy. Jackie Buxton, the former manager of the Cheshire Set Bookshop, has coined the term ‘idenitity library.’ These are the books that feed into our innate identity, our self-perceived understanding of who we are. For some, this may mean a specific set of spiritual, scientific, or philosophical books, the accumulation of which solidifies our viewpoint or deepens our understanding.

In a broader context, book hoarding is also connected with human identity formative years. During adolescence and early adulthood, the first books people acquire often result in enduring feelings of attachment. These books are often treasured due to sentimentality. In that vein, they create intense emotional associations that can persist for life.

Social belonging is another explanation for book hoarding. Books placed in a community setting, shared among other readers, evoke discussion, connection, and camaraderie. We use these books to establish rapport, create deep-rooted interests, and experience the joy of collective intellectual curiosity.

Lastly, books provide an opportunity for escapism. They represent different experiences and worlds that are unimaginable in everyday life. They propel us into emotions, places, and times not otherwise accessible. The escapism inherent in books can function as a form of therapy, far surpassing their simple entertainment value.

The psychology of book hoarding displays the deep intertwining between personal emotions, societal expectations, and human desire for connection. No matter the vein, books serve as anchors in life - providing solace, knowledge, and opportunity. Hoarding books, ultimately, is more than simply storing physical possessions. It is an archival manifestation of our human experience.

In conclusion, book hoarding encapsulates our raw connection to the world, our curiosity, our self-validation, and our love for humanity. Understanding the psychology of book hoarding opens up a realm of possibility for personal growth, intellectual virtue, and the journey of self-discovery. It also helps to redefine our fascination with physical books in an increasingly digital world.

Just as every book tells a story, book hoarding reveals facets of our human experience. Every collection of books is a testament to the individual’s journey of understanding and the tangible reminder of our place in the world.

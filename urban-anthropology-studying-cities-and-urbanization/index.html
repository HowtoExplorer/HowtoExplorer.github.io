Urban Anthropology: Studying Cities and Urbanization


The modern world has undergone a significant transformation due to urbanization. The shift from rural to urban areas is a multifaceted and dynamic process, involving various aspects such as culture, social relations, economics, and politics. Urban anthropology is a discipline that studies these changes and processes, delving into the complex social, cultural, and economic life of cities. In this article, we’ll examine the fascinating realm of urban anthropology, exploring its roots, methodologies, and the profound insights it provides about urban life today.

Urban anthropology started in the late 19th and early 20th centuries as anthropologists began to expand their traditional focus on small-scale societies to encompass urban settings. As early urban anthropologists like Robert E. Park observed, cities had grown to become captivating social and cultural laboratories that fostered unique forms of human interaction, from alienation to creativity. Studying cities became a key way to understand human societies’ complexities and changes in the context of an increasingly globalized world.

Urban anthropologists use various methodologies to study cities and urbanization. Fieldwork remains an essential methodological tool in urban anthropology. Unlike classical anthropological fieldwork, which may take place within isolated communities, urban fieldwork often involves studying various sites, from specific neighborhoods to entire metropolises. Moreover, urban anthropologists often use participant observation techniques, immersing themselves in urban environments to better understand the social dynamics at play.

Urban anthropologists also analyze various forms of urban culture, such as art, music, and architecture, to understand the ways in which people create and recreate their social worlds in cities. By examining these cultural practices, urban anthropologists illuminate how urbanites navigate and resist the constraints of modern urban life.

Beyond these qualitative methodologies, urban anthropology has recently embraced quantitative approaches to study spatial patterns, networks, and data such as urban geodesign or urban big data analytics. These innovations have opened new ways to analyze urban phenomena, such as the spread of infectious diseases, the evolution of urban infrastructure, and the dynamics of social networks.

One essential aspect of urban anthropology is the study of urban social relations. Cities are places where people from different backgrounds, cultures, and ethnicities meet, creating new social bonds and structures. Urban anthropologists research the formation and transformation of urban communities, the emergence and functioning of subcultures, and how people establish social networks in the urban context. By doing so, they highlight both the opportunities and challenges of living in diverse and densely populated urban environments.

Urban anthropology also focuses on the study of urban economies and their implications for people’s lives. Economic activities shape the city’s geographies and affect its residents’ well-being. Urban anthropologists examine how economic processes such as gentrification, the informal economy, and socio-economic inequalities shape urban environments and the lives of city dwellers.

Urbanization has far-reaching implications for the planet, from resource consumption to climate change. Urban anthropologists contribute to this public discourse by investigating the consequences of cities’ rapid growth and development. These studies provide valuable insights for urban planning and policy making, helping governments and organizations to create more sustainable and equitable cities.

In conclusion, urban anthropology is a vast and ever-evolving field that is essential to understanding today’s urban reality. By studying cities and urbanization, we get more than an in-depth glance into the lives of people in cities; we gain valuable insights into the complex tapestry of human society and the way we relate to each other, our environment, and the built world we inhabit. As cities continue to grow and develop, studying urban anthropology remains an increasingly crucial tool for navigating modern urbanism.

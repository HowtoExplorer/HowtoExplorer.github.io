The Myth of the Outcast: Must-Read Books Featuring Outcasts and Misfits


At the heart of human stories, you’ll find the universal themes that connect us all – love, family, friendship, and identity among others. But there’s one theme that has captured our imaginations for centuries and resonates with each and every one of us: the marginalized individual, the outcast, the misfit. Why do we find their stories so compelling? For one, they are the embodiment of our dream to be accepted for who we truly are. These peculiar characters allow us to explore different dimensions of our existence – the darker, hidden corners that often leave us feeling out of place.

Books have been a great source of inspiration for shedding light on the lives of outcasts. They help us discover modern-day Heracless, joining them in their triumph over adversity and self-discovery. Don’t believe me? Here are some books featuring outcasts and misfits, some forgotten and others remembered, that you absolutely must read:

  1. “The Outsiders” by S.E. Hinton

Released in 1967, The Outsiders is an almost autobiographical tale by 16-year-old S.E Hinton about the struggle between two rival gangs in Tulsa, Oklahoma. The story revolves around the Greasers, an unprivileged and misunderstood group, collectively outcast by society. As they fight their rivals, the Socs, it questions the very notions of class, wealth, and the struggles faced by those who fall outside society’s role.

  1. “The Ugly Duckling” by Hans Christian Andersen

This beautifully written tale, by Hans Christian Andersen, tells of a young duckling that grows up not belonging to any group, rejected by the farmside inhabitants and fend ducks. As his journey unfolds, he undergoes many trials and tribulations. The ending though, is one of triumph, transformation, and most importantly, acceptance. An unforgettable story of an outcast becoming a swan.

  1. “The Bell Jar” by Sylvia Plath

Before she was the fabulous writer she is remembered as, Sylvia Plath struggled with mental health issues and the expectations society placed on women. In “The Bell Jar”, Plath takes us on a deeply personal journey of Esther Greenwood, a talented young woman who feels trapped and out of place in her society. Her thoughts and emotions resonate profoundly and provide a profound lens into the invisibility and loneliness of outcasts.

  1. “Gargantua and Pantagruel” by François Rabelais

Gargantua and Pantagruel takes the essence of chivalry and fantasy and molds it into a coming-of-age story for an outcast giant. François Rabelais humorously chronicles the endless misadventures of Gargantua and his son, Pantagruel, as they navigate life’s many challenges. Beyond its boundless imagination, “Gargantua and Pantagruel” also gives a critique of social norms, church dogma, and prejudices of that era, making it relevant even today.

  1. “Frankenstein” by Mary Shelley

“Frankenstein” explores the concept of an outcast not just within society but within oneself. Created as a Romantic novel, Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein delves into Victor Frankenstein’s obsessive quest for knowledge, which ends in the birth of his creation, that is subsequently, dismissed by society. Frankenstein’s tale highlights the dark side of ambition, moral responsibility, and the outcast’s struggle to find belonging.

  1. “The Book Thief” by Markus Zusak

Set in Germany during the second World War, “The Book Thief” is a story that combines elements of tragedy, hope, and triumph with the common thread being an outcast – an orphan girl Liesel Meminger. Through the beautiful power of words and books, Liesel changes lives in her tumultuous world becoming a metaphor for the importance of hope and human kindness.

  1. “Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone” by J.K. Rowling

If you’ve read it, you know the first book in the Harry Potter series has a vast narrative beyond its magic and fantastical creatures. The series is partly about a boy’s journey from an outcast in a muggle family to a revered wizard and eventually a leader of his magical community. Each book features a deeper understanding of the challenges faced by marginalized individuals – a reminder that embracing one’s differences empowers us to challenge the world’s norms.

Each of these books showcases the very human aspect of being an outcast. We laughed, cried, and empathized along with our heroes, magnifying the connection we feel for these immaterial characters. Celebrate the myths of outcasts through these beautifully written books and don’t forget the rich lessons they have to offer. With conviction and sometimes brilliance, these rebels showcase that it’s not always where you start, but how you reshape the world, that truly matters.

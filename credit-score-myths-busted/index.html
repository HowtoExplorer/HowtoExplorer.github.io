Credit Score Myths: Busted


When it comes to personal finance, credit scores often play a significant role. They can affect your ability to get a loan, secure a credit card, or even rent an apartment. However, there are many myths surrounding credit scores that can lead to confusion and misinformation. In this blog post, we’ll debunk some of the most common credit score myths to help you understand how credit scores really work.

Myth #1: Checking Your Credit Score Lowers It

One of the most persistent myths about credit scores is that checking your score will lower it. In reality, there are two types of credit inquiries: soft inquiries and hard inquiries. Soft inquiries, such as when you check your own credit score or when a potential employer does a background check, do not affect your credit score. On the other hand, hard inquiries, which occur when you apply for credit, can have a small impact on your score. It’s important to regularly monitor your credit score to stay informed about your financial health.

Myth #2: Closing Old Accounts Improves Your Credit Score

Some people believe that closing old accounts can improve their credit score. However, the length of your credit history is an essential factor in determining your credit score. Closing old accounts can actually shorten your credit history, which may negatively impact your score. Additionally, closing accounts can also affect your credit utilization ratio, another important factor in credit scoring. It’s often better to keep old accounts open, especially if they have a positive payment history.

Myth #3: You Need to Carry a Balance on Your Credit Card to Build Credit

Contrary to popular belief, carrying a balance on your credit card does not help you build credit faster. In fact, carrying a balance can result in paying unnecessary interest and fees. To build good credit, it’s more important to make on-time payments and keep your credit utilization low. Using your credit card responsibly and paying off the balance in full each month demonstrates good financial habits and can positively impact your credit score.

Myth #4: Income Affects Your Credit Score

Your income is not directly factored into your credit score. While lenders may consider your income when deciding whether to approve you for a loan, it does not impact your credit score. Your credit score is based on your credit history, payment behavior, credit utilization, length of credit history, and other factors related to your borrowing and repayment habits. Regardless of your income, maintaining a healthy credit history is crucial for a good credit score.

Myth #5: Closing Accounts Removes Them from Your Credit Report

Closing a credit account does not erase it from your credit report. Closed accounts can still appear on your report for several years, depending on the type of account and the credit reporting agency. The age of closed accounts is factored into your credit history, so they can continue to influence your credit score even after they’re closed. It’s important to review your credit report regularly to ensure that it accurately reflects your credit history.

Myth #6: You Only Have One Credit Score

There are actually multiple credit scoring models used by different lenders and financial institutions. The most well-known credit scoring model is FICO, but there are also other models such as VantageScore. Each model may generate a slightly different credit score based on the information available to them. Additionally, different credit bureaus may have varying information, leading to differences in credit scores. It’s essential to be aware that you may have multiple credit scores and to focus on maintaining a healthy credit profile overall.

Conclusion

Understanding the truth behind common credit score myths is essential for making informed financial decisions. By debunking these myths, you can take control of your credit health and work towards improving your credit score. Remember that responsible credit management, including making on-time payments, keeping credit utilization low, and maintaining a diverse credit history, is key to a healthy credit score. Stay informed, monitor your credit regularly, and don’t fall for the myths that can lead to unnecessary financial stress.

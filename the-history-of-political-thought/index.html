The History of Political Thought


Political thought, the foundation of political theory, has indeed come a long way since its inception. Throughout history, philosophers and thinkers have grappled with the concepts of state and government, power and authority, and liberty and justice. This essay delves into the evolution of political thought, journeying through the significant milestones that have shaped our understanding of this critical aspect of society.

Ancient Political Thought

Our journey begins with the Ancient Greeks, who laid the groundwork for Western political philosophy. Key figures like Plato and his student, Aristotle, made significant contributions during this era. Plato, in his work “The Republic,” proposed an ideal form of government, advocating for philosopher kings to rule justly. Aristotle’s “Politics” presented a naturalistic approach to understanding state and government, emphasizing the significance of considering both the individual and the community in political discussions.

Medieval Political Thought

The Middle Ages were shaped by Christianity, and political thinkers were mostly religious figures. St. Augustine and St. Thomas Aquinas were two such influential individuals. Augustine’s “The City of God” stresses the importance of divine guidance in ruling and the distinction between the earthly city, where human laws govern, and the heavenly city, where eternal truths prevail. Aquinas built upon this foundation by integrating Aristotelian thought into Christian principles, which expanded the understanding of natural law, a concept intrinsic to political theory.

Early Modern Political Thought

The shift towards modernity brought us groundbreaking thinkers like Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, and Jean-Jacques Rousseau. Hobbes’ “Leviathan” is an essential work that emphasizes the necessity of a strong central government to maintain peace, order, and security amidst a chaotic and selfish human nature. Locke’s “Two Treatises of Government” introduces the concept of a social contract and the importance of individual rights and limited government. Rousseau’s “The Social Contract” highlights the collective nature of political association and urges citizens to actively participate in crafting the laws that govern their lives.

19th Century Political Thought

The advent of the Industrial Revolution saw political thinkers addressing the rapidly changing social, economic, and political landscape. Utilitarianism, with key proponents like Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, became a dominant political philosophy of the time. The focus here lay on maximizing the greatest good for the greatest number, often from the perspective of efficiency and utility.

20th Century Political Thought

Twentieth-century political thought encompasses various pluralist, critical theory, and postmodern movements that questioned or built upon the previous centuries’ ideas. Political scientists like Harold Laski and Hannah Arendt contributed to the development of political philosophy in the context of modernity’s complex political landscape, highlighting issues surrounding consent, power, and the role of states and peoples in political life.

Contemporary Political Thought

Today, we are witnessing an ever-evolving understanding of political thought, with issues like multiculturalism, globalism, environmentalism, and postcolonial theory influencing the discourse. The ongoing debates surrounding the roles of the state, global institutions, and the individual in the political landscape remain an essential part of our understanding of politics.

As we reflect upon the historical evolution of political thought, we cannot help but acknowledge its relevance in the present. Our understanding of state and government, power and authority, and liberty and justice has evolved tremendously over time, shaping the policies and decisions we make today. Understanding our past is crucial as we continue to mold political thought to adapt to the rapidly changing world we live in. Through the consideration of various perspectives and viewpoints, we can craft a political understanding that best serves the needs and aspirations of our communities.

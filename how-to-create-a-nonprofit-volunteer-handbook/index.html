How to Create a Nonprofit Volunteer Handbook


A well-crafted volunteer handbook can be the foundation for an effective and sustainable nonprofit program. This essential document is not just a resource—it sets the tone for your organization’s expectations, culture, and processes, helping volunteers feel aligned, informed, and valued. Whether you’re starting from scratch or looking to refine your existing document, creating a comprehensive volunteer handbook ensures your nonprofit operates smoothly, fosters positive relationships, and supports its mission with clarity.

Why a Volunteer Handbook Matters

A volunteer handbook is more than a set of guidelines; it’s a communication tool that benefits both your organization and your volunteers. For nonprofits, this document ensures consistency, professionalism, and clarity. Volunteers, on the other hand, gain a clear understanding of expectations, responsibilities, and how they contribute to the organization’s goals. Without it, misunderstandings and misalignments can occur, leading to decreased volunteer morale or operational inefficiencies.

By covering the essential aspects of your nonprofit, such as policies, procedures, and values, your handbook helps volunteers feel welcomed, reduces onboarding challenges, and ultimately strengthens the relationship between your organization and its team. Now let’s explore how to craft a volunteer handbook that resonates with your nonprofit’s unique mission.

Step 1: Define the Purpose of Your Handbook

Before diving into writing your handbook, take time to define its purpose. Consider these key questions:

  1. What do volunteers need to know to succeed? Your handbook should walk a fine line between being comprehensive and accessible. Focus on the elements that directly impact volunteer engagement and performance.
  2. How does your nonprofit operate? Include specific policies, operational guidelines, and an overview of your goals.
  3. What is your desired tone? Lively? Compassionate? Formal? The tone should reflect your nonprofit’s culture and values.

Using these guiding questions, you can build a framework that emphasizes clarity, relevance, and user-friendliness.

Step 2: Include a Warm Welcome and Mission Statement

First impressions count, and your handbook’s opening pages are no exception. Start with a warm welcome to show your appreciation for volunteers, emphasizing how their contributions play a pivotal role in advancing your nonprofit’s mission. A heartfelt message from your organization’s leadership can add a personal touch, making volunteers feel valued.

Afterward, include your nonprofit’s mission statement. This section conveys your purpose and goals, inspiring volunteers as they read. Aligning expectations starts here—help your volunteers see the bigger picture and their vital role within it.

Step 3: Outline Your Organization’s Core Values

After introducing your mission, share key organizational values that guide your nonprofit’s work. Think of this section as the ethical and cultural compass for your team. Discuss principles such as integrity, inclusivity, sustainability, or community engagement. When volunteers understand your values, they’re better equipped to align their actions and attitudes with the organizational culture.

For instance, if cultivating diversity and inclusion is a central value, explain how you implement it within your programs. Be actionable and specific to encourage volunteers to embody those same principles in their work.

Step 4: Define Volunteer Roles and Expectations

Volunteers thrive when they know precisely what is expected of them. Use this section to outline their specific roles, tasks, and required commitments. Break it down into the following:

  • Job Descriptions: Include comprehensive role descriptions for each volunteer position. Whether it’s event assistance, administrative support, or community outreach, define the scope of each role clearly.

  • Schedule Commitments: Outline shift assignments, time commitments, and attendance policies to ensure volunteers can manage their involvement effectively without feeling overwhelmed.

  • Behavioral Expectations: Specify your organization’s code of conduct. Lay out workplace etiquette, communication norms, and behavioral standards to maintain a respectful and harmonious environment.

Including specific yet accessible details assures prospective and existing volunteers of what lies ahead and avoids potential confusion.

Step 5: Provide an Overview of Operational Policies

Introduce volunteers to the key operational policies that govern your nonprofit. These policies might not seem engaging at first glance, but they’re fundamental for ensuring safety, legal compliance, and consistency. Use plain language and avoid confusion by structuring policies into categories. Essential topics to cover may include:

  • Confidentiality: Outline what kind of organizational information must remain private, such as donor details or sensitive program data.
  • Health and Safety: Explain protocols related to emergency procedures, volunteer safety, or training for operating equipment (if applicable).
  • Inclusion and Nondiscrimination: Provide your policies ensuring equal opportunities and a welcoming environment for volunteers of diverse backgrounds.
  • Media Permissions: Clarify how photos, videos, and other media involving volunteers may be used.
  • Grievance Procedures: Outline the process for reporting concerns, whether about harassment, workplace conditions, or other issues.

Clearly conveying these policies in your handbook protects both the organization and its volunteers while fostering a sense of transparency.

Step 6: Describe the Volunteer Onboarding Process

The onboarding process is often a volunteer’s first direct interaction with your organizational systems. Use the handbook to outline the full journey from application to activation. Key steps to include in this section are:

  1. Application Details: Explain how prospective volunteers can apply, including any required paperwork or online forms.
  2. Initial Screening: If background checks or interviews are involved, clarify this process and its timeframe.
  3. Orientation Sessions: Share what volunteers can expect during orientation, such as training programs or meeting leadership.
  4. Ongoing Training and Development: Explain how skills-building opportunities such as workshops or seminars will support their roles.

Providing a roadmap for onboarding is invaluable in calming nerves and boosting engagement from day one.

Step 7: Emphasize Recognition and Rewards

Volunteers dedicate their time and energy because they care deeply about your mission, but recognition goes a long way in maintaining their enthusiasm. Use your handbook to describe your organization’s approach to celebrating and appreciating volunteer contributions. Examples include:

  • Hosting appreciation events
  • Featuring volunteers in newsletters or on social media
  • Providing certificates, awards, or small tokens of gratitude
  • Offering developmental perks, such as access to networking opportunities or free courses

Recognizing the value volunteers bring reaffirms their importance to the nonprofit, ensuring they remain motivated and connected.

Step 8: Offer a Communication Guide

Clear communication is the backbone of effective collaboration, and your volunteer handbook should reflect that. Include sections on when, where, and how volunteers can get in touch with organizational leadership or staff. Key points to address include:

  • Key Contacts: List staff members or designated coordinators assigned to handle volunteer inquiries.
  • Tools and Platforms: Highlight any apps, messaging platforms, or systems volunteers will use to stay connected.
  • Feedback Mechanisms: Provide information on how volunteers can share their thoughts, whether through surveys, meetings, or suggestion boxes.

By fostering an environment of open communication, you’ll strengthen trust and build enduring relationships.

Step 9: Regular Updates and Revisions

The needs of your nonprofit and the scope of your programs may evolve over time, so your volunteer handbook should be a living document. Commit to reviewing and updating it annually to ensure it reflects current policies, procedures, and goals.

For example, if you launch a new volunteer program or adopt a significant policy change, your handbook should be updated accordingly. Include a version control section that records revision dates for transparency.

Step 10: Make It Accessible and Engaging

Finally, consider how your handbook will be distributed and received. Use language that is conversational yet professional. Organize content with headings, bullet points, and short paragraphs for easy readability.

Distribute your handbook digitally via email or an online portal, and consider providing printed copies for in-person orientations. Accessibility ensures that volunteers can revisit the document whenever they need clarification.

Closing Thoughts

Creating a nonprofit volunteer handbook is an investment of time and effort, but it’s a foundational step that pays dividends. This document empowers your team, sets the stage for professional and respectful relationships, and supports your mission in countless ways.

By crafting a handbook rooted in clarity, inspiration, and practicality, you’ll create a resource that aligns with your vision, engages your volunteers, and ensures sustained success for your nonprofit. Take the time today to start—or refresh—this vital tool, and watch your volunteer program flourish.

The Effects of Climate Change on Oceans and Fisheries


Climate change, an encompassing term for global warming and its impacts, is an unfolding disaster with far-reaching consequences on our planet. Chief among these consequences are the profound effects on the oceans, the life-supporting source of the planet. Oceans absorb around 30% of the carbon dioxide emissions caused by human activities and about 90% of the excess heat. The seas are undergoing a steady process of chemical changes that in turn affect marine life and ecosystems, precipitating a domino effect on fisheries around the globe.

One of the most visible effects of climate change on oceans is the increase in sea surface temperatures. This, in turn, impacts various oceanic livelihoods. Warmer waters increase stratification, a layering of water temperatures that destabilizes the ocean. This hinders the mixing of vital nutrients, like oxygen and plankton, to deeper levels, leading to areas of ocean devoid of life.

This warming of the ocean globally is already affecting marine food chains. Coral bleaching, where the vibrant colors of corals fade due to stress from heat, has escalated due to steadily warmer waters. Also, the upper layers of the ocean are becoming less dense, creating an area of water about 10-20 meters thick that is inhospitably warm for anything but the smallest and hardiest of sea life. This has resulted in a reduction of phytoplankton, an essential source of food for larger sea-life.

Plankton form the basis of the marine food chain. If phytoplankton populations diminish, this has significant ramifications for the entire food chain, including commercially important fish species such as cod and haddock. Already there have been reports of alterations in fish distribution and changes in fish migration patterns, affecting major fishing industries worldwide.

The impacts of climate change on ocean currents are equally worrying. Changes to the ocean circulation, such as the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Current, can disrupt the weather patterns worldwide, affecting rainfall, monsoons, as well as ocean productivity. Disruptions in ocean currents could potentially lead to ‘regime shifts’ where marine ecosystems vastly differ from their historic norms.

Another significant impact of ocean warming is the development of ocean acidification. The oceans are absorbing massive amounts of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. This triggers a chemical reaction that increases acidity, a problem evidenced by the loss of calcium carbonate in seawater. As a result, it becomes difficult for marine organisms that build shells and skeletons using calcium carbonate, such as mollusks and corals, to maintain their life-supporting structures.

Currently, over four billion people rely on seafood as their primary source of protein, with the global fish-derived food market at a whopping $150 billion annually. Fishing industries are, therefore, of paramount importance. Unsurprisingly, climate change’s adverse effects on ocean ecosystems could destabilize entire countries’ economies critically reliant on fisheries for food security and financial surplus.

The time for action against climate change effects on oceans and fisheries is now. Tentative efforts are being made globally to address climate change challenges, yet more extensive, and immediate action is required. The corals are already turning bleached, and the lines of fish are not where they used to be. The time for transition is at hand, and we must grasp the opportunity to ensure our oceans’ health and our fisheries stay viable for generations to come. Let’s not overlook the value of a hearty plate of fish, one of the last unexplored food frontiers, as we work to safeguard this crucial global resource.

Understanding Your Rights with Gift Cards


Gift cards have become a popular means of gift-giving and reward-redemption in today's digital age. However, despite their convenience, many consumers are unaware of their legal rights and protections when it comes to these seemingly basic cards. A closer look at the laws and regulations governing gift cards reveals a complex and often unclear landscape. The Fine Print: Gift Card Laws and Regulations In the United States, gift card laws and regulations are largely governed by the Card Act of 2009, also known as the Credit Card Accountability Responsibility and Disclosure, or CARD, Act. This legislation aimed to protect consumers from the growing trend of unscrupulous gift card practices. One of the key provisions of the CARD Act is the requirement that gift card issuers clearly disclose fee terms, expiration dates, and any other terms or conditions prior to sale. However, this requirement does not necessarily mean that consumers are aware of these details. Expired and Unused: The Hidden Dangers of Expiration Dates Gift cards often come with expiration dates or dormancy fees that can be triggered when the card remains unused or inactivated for a certain period. These dates and fees can catch consumers off guard, leaving them with little recourse but to accept the loss. In reality, a gift card is only a gift card until a blackout expiration date takes effect. Consumers who are not aware of the expiration date may unknowingly accept a card as a gift and later find that the gift card is no longer valid by the time they try to use it. Online retailers relying on gift cards to encourage repeat sales often neglect to mention these time-sensitive details, perfecting a spin to trick customers. Hidden Fees: The Sneakiest Form of Gift Card Desperation Dormancy fees, the fees associated with an inactive gift card, can be triggered by simply not using the card for a calendar period. These fees are commonly classified as inactivity fees. Gift card holders might unknowingly incur fees by losing track of their balances or finding themselves unable to make a purchase before the card falls below a purchase eligibility threshold.

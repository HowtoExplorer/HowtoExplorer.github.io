The Concept of Beauty in Philosophy


Beauty, as a concept, has intrigued philosophers for centuries. It encompasses an emotion, a quality, and an aesthetic appreciation. We all have our own interpretations of beauty, which varies from one person to another. But, what does it truly mean, and how has the philosophy of beauty evolved over time? In this blog post, we will explore these questions and more, painting an image of beauty as attested by some notable philosophers.

Beauty as Universal and Subjective

Aristotle, one of the most significant ancient philosophers, argued that beauty is ‘universal.’ He posited that beautiful objects share intrinsic qualities that make them universally appealing. Similar to how a perfect apple is universally delightful, elegant art is universally beautiful. This viewpoint asserts that beauty is, to some extent, a universal concept. However, even with this premise, allowances should be made for individual preferences.

In contrast, philosopher Charles Fourier maintained that beauty is ‘subjective.’ He defined beauty as what causes pleasure in our senses. What brings joy and delight to one might not mean the same for another; thus, making beauty subjective.

Ethics of Beauty

Following the definition of beauty as a universally appealing and subjective quality, the philosophical relationship between beauty and morality emerged. Immanuel Kant, in his “Critique of the Judgment,” explored this aspect. Kant claimed that a beautiful object’s moral worth lies not in its content but in its capacity to evoke an empathetic response. He held that aesthetically valuable works promote moral, emotional, and spiritual ideals, both in the creator and the viewer.

The Role of Taste

Taste, a critical component in appreciating beauty, has been an area of substantial focus in aesthetics. David Hume asserted that taste should not be dictated by rules. Taste is subjective, and it is. Still, John Keats believed that if we look long enough, we will come closer to understanding the eternal truth, which bounds our human understanding of beauty.

Beauty as Life’s Purpose

Lastly, Nietzsche‘s perspectivism on beauty sought to challenge our traditional notions of it. He believed that beauty does not exist, rather that you interpret it through your perspective. Life is the aesthetic experience; we give life meaning by appreciating beauty. Nietzsche’s Nietzschian individualism makes the essence of beauty subjective, ultimately implying that beauty iterates life’s purpose.

In conclusion, the definition and purpose of beauty are not universally agreed upon. However, philosophers such as Aristotle and Kant insist on a deeper meaning and common understanding of beauty. Conversely, Fourier and Nietzsche allow for subjectivity and personal perspectives to dictate beauty. Ultimately, the concept of beauty remains open to interpretation. The philosophical discourse on beauty can help us better comprehend its intricate nature, relating directly to human nature.

Asking questions, contemplating answers and seeking to understand our world, this is the essence of philosophy. It can help us visualize the beautiful concepts we cannot see but can imagine. The pursuit of understanding the concept of beauty encapsulates the enthusiasm of a unique quest, exploring diverse viewpoints, and nurturing intellectual curiosity. This quest resembles the joy we find in the art itself. Our journey toward interpreting the ineffable concept of beauty, whether led by Kant, Nietzsche, or another philosopher, is our passage through intellectual curiosity.

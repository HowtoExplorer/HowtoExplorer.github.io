The Importance of Trees: Their role in combating climate change and enhancing wellbeing


One common denominator in the natural world is the prevalence of trees. They line our streets, grow in our backyards, and constitute the bulk of our forests. Trees provide oxygen, timber, fruits, and shade. Yet, their most distinguished role is in mitigating climate change and promoting our overall wellbeing. Let’s delve deeper into this unsung hero of the natural world.

Trees, Climate Change, and the Carbon Connection

Our planet isn’t just experiencing weather changes; these changes are all part of the “climate” - which refers to the long-term average weather patterns of a region. Human activities, like burning fossil fuels, releasing pollutants, deforestation, and land-use changes, are causing climate change. These activities have detrimental impacts on weather patterns, air quality, ecosystems, and human health.

It’s well-known that trees absorb carbon dioxide (CO2), a potent greenhouse gas contributing to the warming of our planet. Through a process called photosynthesis, trees capture CO2 from the atmosphere and convert it into oxygen and biomass. In this way, trees are carbon sinks - places where carbon dioxide is stored.

By knitting a substantial portion of the Earth’s carbon into their woody frames, trees act as carbon sponges that literally absorb and store a significant portion of the excess carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Trees sequester approximately 1.5 billion tons of carbon annually, a considerable figure indeed.

Deforestation exacerbates climate change. Wiping out forests means sweeping away nature’s chief carbon sinks. As a result, 20% of global carbon emissions arise from land-use change, with deforestation accounting for two-thirds of these emissions. Consequently, forest preservation and afforestation, or planting new trees, are crucial to combating climate change.

Trees and Wellbeing

The benefits of trees are far from limited to the atmospheric arena. In enhancing wellbeing, trees affect mental and physical health. They provide the backdrops for our parks and gardens and the settings for our homes, in essence, creating spaces where life happens.

Japanese urban planners coined the term “Shirakaba Village” to evoke a community where trees are the essence of the landscape. With “shirakaba” meaning white birch in Japanese, the term describes the sylvan ideal in which streetsides are lined with trees. The philosophy informs urban planning, landscape architecture, and garden design in Japan, making it a beautiful example of how trees promote wellbeing.

Trees create additional benefits by purifying the air. They release oxygen, stabilize soil, and provide habitats for myriad species. Their shade benefits humans by reducing the Urban Heat Island Effect (UHI) - a phenomenon in which cities are hotter than their rural surroundings. Occurring as the paste urban environments are densely populated, leading to increased land surface absorption of heat, adding to the temperature rise.

By their tranquil ambiance, trees reduce stress and anxiety, leading to improved mental health. Patients in hospitals in general recuperate faster when they can see trees. Furthermore, trees provide ballast to communities, giving a sense of place and belonging.

Conclusion

Undeniably, trees have intrinsic importance. Beyond adding aesthetics to our daily lives, they play a priceless role in the global struggle against climate change and the urban wellbeing movement. Deforestation has no winners - it raises global temperatures, disrupts ecosystems, and exacerbates climate change.

Planting trees and conserving woodlands should be a principal concern. Let’s raise a voice in the fight against the enemy of our time - climate change - and draw up a plan to ensure we don’t lose this natural ally.

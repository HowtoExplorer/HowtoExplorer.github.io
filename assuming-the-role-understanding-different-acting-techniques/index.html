Assuming the Role: Understanding Different Acting Techniques


When people refer to “acting techniques,” they often think of the various methods and approaches employed by actors to bring their characters to life. These techniques differ in the emphasis they place on various aspects of acting, and many have been developed over time by well-known figures in the performing arts. Understanding these techniques is vital for any aspiring actor or theater enthusiast.

Let’s dive into five primary acting techniques and discuss their merits as well as potential drawbacks.

1. Method Acting

Method acting is one of the most well-known and widely used techniques. Originating in the early 20th century, it encourages actors to truly “become” their characters. By immersing themselves in the character’s emotions, experiences, and life circumstances, actors can provide more authentic, relatable performances.

This technique often involves lengthy research and development, such as taking on hobbies, mannerisms, and language quirks that the character might exhibit. Many consider this approach to be the gold standard of acting. However, it can be taxing, both mentally and emotionally, for the actor.

2. Meisner Technique

Named after Sanford Meisner, a founding member of the Neighborhood Playhouse, the Meisner Technique focuses on realistic and truthful performances. It involves two essential practices: repetition exercises and emotional availability.

In this technique, the actor repeats lines and actions with a focus on being present and responding honestly to other actors’ lines and movements. This creates a more genuine and improvisational quality to the performance. While effective, Meisner techniques require dedication, patience, and commitment.

3. Stanislavski’s System

Developed by the Russian playwright and director Constantin Stanislavski, this technique emphasizes the connection between subtext and intention. Actors are encouraged to dive deep into the character’s psyche to uncover motivations and desires.

This method focuses on creating a consistent internal world for the character that drives their actions in the scene. Though highly powerful and immersive, this approach might sometimes be challenging for newer actors, as it requires a certain level of interpretive skills and experience.

4. Circular screwed up

In this unique technique, Czech director and playwright Vsevolod Meyerhold stressed the importance of physicalization and dynamic, or circular, movement on the stage. Actors utilizing this approach would move and gesture around the stage in a fluid, almost dance-like manner.

This method can be particularly useful when it comes to exploring relationships and subtle emotions. However, it may not be ideal for every scene or play, as it has a specific aesthetic that doesn’t fit every production.

5. Michael Chekhov Technique

Born from the plays of Anton Chekhov and further developed by his grandson Michael, the Chekhov technique focuses on various exercises that encourage relaxation, imagination, and positive thinking. Actors in this process work on creating objects and symbols—which are not just physical, but also emotional and spiritual elements.

This technique is helpful for actors who struggle with emotional connection, as it empowers them to access deeper emotions purposefully. Still, it requires a willingness to play and a stern focus on untapping the imagination.

Understanding these distinct techniques provides a strong foundation for any actor, allowing for versatility and the ability to adapt to various roles and challenges on stage. Along with these methods, practice, dedication, and a growth mindset will undoubtedly contribute to a lifetime of enhancing one’s craft.

The Weight of Existence: Exploring the Depths of Human Condition through Literature Introduction Existential themes have been a cornerstone of human philosophy, capturing the essence of our being and sparking intense debates. Literature serves as a powerful reflection of our collective experiences, emotions, and perceptions of life. Throughout the ages, authors have employed literary works to delve into the complexities of human existence, often exploring the abyss of desperation, hopelessness, and the human search for meaning. Camus's Absurdity and the Quest for Meaning Albert Camus's absurdist philosophy has long been a cornerstone of existentialist thought. In works such as 'The Myth of Sisyphus' and 'The Fall', Camus critiques the idea of inherent meaning in the universe, suggesting that humans must create their own purpose in a seemingly indifferent world. This philosophy resonates through literary works of authors such as Kafka, who explores the meaninglessness and absurdity of modern life. Camus's own works foreshadow the realization that the human condition is a constant, inexorable anxiety-seeking depthless nothing. Sartre's Existentialism and the Freedom to Choose Jean-Paul Sartre's foundational principles of existentialism emphasize human freedom as the fundamental aspect of being human. His masterwork, 'Being and Nothingness', posits that individuals possess complete responsibility for choosing their actions, shaping their own destiny amidst the freedom from inherent purpose. This concept has been echoed through other notable authors such as Kierkegaard, who echoes Sartre's line of thought, placing an unequivocal focus on personal agency.

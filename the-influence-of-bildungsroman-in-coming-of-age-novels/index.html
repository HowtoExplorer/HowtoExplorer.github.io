The Influence of Bildungsroman in Coming-of-Age Novels


Coming-of-age novels have been a staple of literary canon for centuries, captivating readers with stories of self-discovery, growth, and transformation. One of the most significant and enduring influences on this genre is the concept of Bildungsroman, a literary term coined by German critic Johann Wolfgang von Goethe in the 18th century. In this blog post, we will delve into the world of Bildungsroman and explore its profound impact on coming-of-age novels.

The Concept of Bildungsroman

Bildungsroman, literally meaning “novel of formation” or “education novel,” refers to a genre of literature that focuses on the psychological and emotional development of the protagonist as they navigate the challenges of growing up. The term is often used interchangeably with “coming-of-age novel,” although the latter tends to emphasize the physical and social aspects of adolescence, whereas Bildungsroman emphasizes the internal, psychological transformation.

The Influence of Bildungsroman

The influence of Bildungsroman on coming-of-age novels is profound and far-reaching. By exploring the inner world of the protagonist, Bildungsroman allows authors to create rich, nuanced characters that readers can relate to and empathize with. This focus on the internal struggle also enables authors to tackle complex themes and issues, such as identity, morality, and social class, in a more subtle and effective manner.

One of the most significant ways in which Bildungsroman has influenced coming-of-age novels is through its emphasis on the protagonist’s journey of self-discovery. In Bildungsroman novels, the protagonist is often depicted as struggling to find their place in the world, grappling with questions of identity, morality, and purpose. This journey of self-discovery is typically marked by moments of crisis, revelation, and transformation, as the protagonist emerges with a newfound understanding of themselves and their place in the world.

Examples of Bildungsroman in Coming-of-Age Novels

Many coming-of-age novels have been influenced by the concept of Bildungsroman, and some have even been classified as Bildungsroman novels in their own right. One of the most famous examples is Goethe’s own novel, “The Apprenticeship of Wilhelm Meister,” which is often considered the first Bildungsroman novel. Other notable examples include Jane Austen’s “Emma,” Charles Dickens’ “Great Expectations,” and J.D. Salinger’s “The Catcher in the Rye.”

More recent examples of Bildungsroman in coming-of-age novels include John Green’s “The Fault in Our Stars,” Rainbow Rowell’s “Eleanor & Park,” and Celeste Ng’s “Everything I Never Told You.” These novels all share a common thread of exploring the internal struggles and transformation of the protagonist, and are a testament to the enduring influence of Bildungsroman on the coming-of-age genre.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the influence of Bildungsroman on coming-of-age novels is profound and far-reaching. By exploring the internal world of the protagonist, Bildungsroman allows authors to create rich, nuanced characters that readers can relate to and empathize with. The emphasis on the protagonist’s journey of self-discovery also enables authors to tackle complex themes and issues in a more subtle and effective manner. Whether in classic novels like “The Apprenticeship of Wilhelm Meister” or more recent examples like “The Fault in Our Stars,” the influence of Bildungsroman is a testament to its enduring impact on the coming-of-age genre.

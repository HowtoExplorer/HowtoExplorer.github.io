The Philosophy of Education: What Should We Teach?


Education has been an essential part of human society for centuries, shaping individuals, communities, and civilizations. But the fundamental question remains: what should we teach? The philosophy of education delves into this question, examining the principles, objectives, and content of what is taught in schools, universities, and other educational institutions. At its core, the philosophy of education seeks to understand the purpose of education and how best to foster the intellectual, moral, and social development of students.

In this post, we explore the various schools of thought within the philosophy of education, the goals we should strive to achieve in education, and the importance of content selection in shaping the minds of future generations. The goal is not only to provide insight into the purpose of education but also to highlight the factors that should guide decisions about what we teach in classrooms today.

The Purpose of Education: Why Do We Educate?

Before considering what to teach, it is crucial to understand why we educate. The purpose of education has been debated for millennia, with different philosophical traditions offering various perspectives. Historically, education has been used to transmit knowledge, cultivate virtue, and prepare individuals for participation in civic life. But in today’s diverse and rapidly changing world, the purpose of education has evolved.

  1. Transmission of Knowledge: One traditional view of education emphasizes the importance of transmitting knowledge. This knowledge can be about the natural world, human history, mathematics, literature, and the arts. Students learn the accumulated wisdom of generations, and education serves to perpetuate culture, tradition, and expertise.

  2. Development of Critical Thinking: Modern perspectives on education often focus on fostering critical thinking and intellectual independence. Instead of simply absorbing knowledge, students are encouraged to question, analyze, and interpret information. This approach is grounded in the belief that education should cultivate individuals who can think for themselves, solve problems, and navigate the complexities of life.

  3. Moral and Character Development: Another key purpose of education is moral and character development. Education is seen as a means of shaping ethical individuals who contribute positively to society. This perspective places emphasis on teaching virtues such as honesty, empathy, responsibility, and respect for others.

  4. Preparation for Civic Engagement: Education is also viewed as a tool for preparing individuals to participate fully in democratic society. A well-rounded education should equip students with the knowledge and skills necessary to engage in political, social, and cultural activities. This includes understanding their rights and responsibilities, appreciating diversity, and working collaboratively with others.

Philosophical Approaches to Education

Several philosophical traditions have emerged throughout history to address the question of what should be taught. These traditions provide frameworks for educational theory and practice, influencing curriculum design, teaching methods, and the role of teachers in the classroom.

1. Idealism

Idealism, often associated with philosophers like Plato and Immanuel Kant, emphasizes the importance of abstract thinking, ideals, and moral development. In education, idealism advocates for teaching students the timeless truths of philosophy, art, and literature. The idealist educator seeks to cultivate the intellectual and moral faculties of students, encouraging them to reflect on higher principles and the meaning of life.

In practice, idealism suggests that students should be taught not just facts, but also the ethical and philosophical dimensions of human existence. This can involve exploring fundamental questions about justice, beauty, and virtue, encouraging students to engage deeply with ideas that have shaped human civilization.

2. Realism

Realism, as championed by philosophers like Aristotle, takes a more pragmatic approach to education. Realists believe that education should focus on teaching students about the real world—the observable, tangible world around them. In the classroom, this translates to a focus on subjects like science, mathematics, history, and language, with an emphasis on objectivity and empirical knowledge.

Realism suggests that what we teach should be grounded in the physical world and should help students develop practical skills. Realists argue that education should prepare individuals to understand and navigate the complexities of the real world, equipping them with the knowledge necessary to succeed in life.

3. Pragmatism

Pragmatism, associated with philosophers such as John Dewey, emphasizes the importance of experience in the learning process. Pragmatists believe that education should be dynamic and responsive to the needs of students, focusing on problem-solving, critical thinking, and hands-on learning. Rather than being taught abstract theories, students should learn by engaging with real-world problems and experimenting with solutions.

In the context of education, pragmatism calls for a curriculum that is flexible and adaptable. The goal is not only to impart knowledge but also to foster the skills necessary for students to adapt to a constantly changing world. This includes developing the ability to think critically, collaborate with others, and approach challenges with creativity and resilience.

4. Existentialism

Existentialism in education, as advocated by thinkers like Jean-Paul Sartre and Martin Heidegger, emphasizes individuality and personal freedom. According to existentialist philosophy, education should help students explore their own identities, question societal norms, and define their own meaning in life. Rather than conforming to preset ideas or rigid curricula, existentialist education encourages students to find their own paths and take responsibility for their own learning.

Existentialism in education stresses the importance of student choice and autonomy, allowing individuals to shape their own educational journey. In practice, this may mean offering students a range of options for courses, allowing them to pursue their unique interests and passions while also addressing universal existential concerns such as the search for purpose, identity, and freedom.

What Should We Teach? Content Considerations

Once we understand the broader purpose of education and the philosophical traditions that inform it, we can begin to address the practical question: what should we teach? There are several key considerations when selecting content for education, from foundational knowledge to contemporary issues.

1. Core Knowledge and Basic Skills

A foundational aspect of any education system is the teaching of core knowledge and basic skills. These are the fundamental building blocks that students need to succeed in life, such as literacy, numeracy, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills. In many educational systems, core subjects such as mathematics, science, language arts, and social studies form the foundation of the curriculum.

The content of these core subjects should provide students with the necessary knowledge and skills to navigate the world around them. Mathematics, for example, equips students with the ability to think logically, solve problems, and understand the principles that govern the physical world. Language arts help students develop communication skills, enabling them to express ideas clearly and engage with others effectively.

2. Character and Moral Education

While academic subjects are crucial, there is also an increasing focus on character and moral education. In an age of global challenges and ethical dilemmas, teaching students about empathy, responsibility, and integrity is essential. Many schools incorporate social-emotional learning (SEL) into their curricula, helping students develop self-awareness, social awareness, and relationship skills.

Moral education encourages students to reflect on their actions and the impact they have on others. It also promotes the understanding of ethical principles and the importance of contributing to society. Character education is an integral part of developing well-rounded individuals who are not only knowledgeable but also responsible and compassionate citizens.

3. Global Awareness and Diversity

In an interconnected world, it is essential for students to understand global issues and appreciate cultural diversity. Education should expose students to different perspectives, teaching them about the world’s diverse cultures, histories, and belief systems. This fosters respect for others and prepares students to engage in a globalized society.

Global awareness also involves teaching students about contemporary issues such as climate change, inequality, and human rights. It is essential to educate the next generation about the pressing challenges facing humanity and equip them with the knowledge and skills to address these challenges.

4. Technology and Innovation

In today’s digital age, education must also prepare students for a world driven by technology and innovation. From coding and artificial intelligence to digital literacy and online communication, students need to be equipped with the skills to navigate the digital landscape. Technology education can foster creativity, problem-solving, and collaboration, helping students stay competitive in an ever-evolving job market.

Moreover, technology has the potential to transform education itself. The integration of digital tools and online resources can make learning more personalized, accessible, and interactive. By embracing technology, educators can create dynamic learning environments that engage students and prepare them for the future.

5. Critical Thinking and Problem-Solving

At the heart of education lies the development of critical thinking and problem-solving skills. The ability to analyze information, think critically, and solve complex problems is essential for success in the modern world. Educational systems should encourage students to question assumptions, evaluate evidence, and consider alternative viewpoints.

Teaching critical thinking helps students become informed decision-makers and active participants in their communities. It also empowers them to tackle complex issues, from personal dilemmas to global challenges, with creativity and confidence.

Conclusion: The Future of Education

The philosophy of education raises important questions about what we teach and why we teach it. As we look to the future, it is clear that education must evolve to meet the needs of a rapidly changing world. We must prioritize not only knowledge acquisition but also the development of critical thinking, creativity, and moral responsibility.

What we teach should reflect the complexities of the modern world, preparing students for active participation in society, a career, and lifelong learning. As education continues to adapt, the central goal remains the same: to empower individuals to reach their full potential and contribute meaningfully to the world around them.

In the end, education is not just about transmitting information—it is about shaping individuals who will think deeply, act responsibly, and engage thoughtfully with the world. As we continue to ask, “What should we teach?” we must also consider how we teach, recognizing that the methods and content of education have the power to shape the future.

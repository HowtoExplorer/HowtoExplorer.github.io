Conflict Resolution Styles: Finding What Works for You


Conflict is an inseparable part of human life. It can be unavoidable, but how we resolve it is a choice. Our approach to resolving disputes dictates the outcome, and our decision should be based on the specific situation and our personality. Understanding your conflict resolution style can help to improve your relationships and decision-making abilities. This guide will shed light on the most common styles, and help you identify the one that works best for you.

There are primarily five conflict resolution styles: avoiding, accommodating, competing, collaborating, and compromise. Read on to understand each style and identify which one suits you the best.

1. Avoiding

Avoiding is the simplest form of conflict resolution. If you’re someone who tends to sidestep confrontation at all costs, this is your style. This approach is suitable for situations where conflict is unnecessary or unimportant, or when the conflict is consistently negative.

However, the downside of avoiding is that it prevents you from resolving crucial issues that require attention. It may lead to further resentment and misunderstandings.

2. Accommodating

Accommodating is the art of surrendering your needs and desires to maintain peace. This style is ideal when you value relationships over objectivity. If you’re someone who tends to prioritize harmony over everything else, this is likely your style.

However, always putting others’ needs first can lead to feeling taken for granted and may create dependency.

3. Competing

Competing is the style where you assert your needs and wants without any compromise. An individual with this style tends to be assertive and focused on their needs at the expense of others.

However, constant competition may harm your relationships, given its zero-sum nature. It assumes there’s a fixed sum of rewards and one’s gain is another’s loss.

4. Collaborating

Collaborating is not just about resolving a conflict but going beyond it to create stronger, more profound relationships. This approach emphasizes finding solutions that benefit everyone involved. It works best in situations where a cooperative solution is possible and mutually beneficial.

However, collaborating might not work in every conflict since it demands adequate time and effort to find common ground.

5. Compromise

Compromise is a middle-ground that satisfies everyone’s needs partially. If you value consensus and fairness in disputes, you’re likely a compromiser. This approach often stems from a belief that finding a middle-ground is the best way to maintain relationships and achieve some level of satisfaction.

However, focusing solely on compromise might not resolve the underlying issues.

Discovering your style can provide a starting point for improving your conflict management skills. Knowing your approach may help you:

  • Understand the limits of each style
  • Adapt your style based on the situation
  • Develop more effective strategies in conflict resolution
  • Strengthen your relationships

At the end of the day, being aware of our responses and adapting helps to employ conflict resolution effectively. It is essential to move away from a one-size-fits-all approach to conflict resolution and realize that different situations call for different styles. Your journey towards mastering conflict resolution will undoubtedly contribute to your personal and professional growth.

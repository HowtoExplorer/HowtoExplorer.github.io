How to Ask for Feedback After an Interview


Asking for feedback after an interview is a crucial step in the job interview process. It can be a make-or-break moment that can either boost your confidence or reinforce your insecurities. While it may seem daunting, asking for feedback can be done effectively and efficiently. In this post, we'll guide you through the process of asking for feedback, its benefits, and what to expect during and after the conversation. Why Ask for Feedback After an Interview? Asking for feedback is a valuable opportunity to understand your strengths and weaknesses, as perceived by the interviewer. It can provide you with insights into how you performed, what you did well, and what you can improve on. This information can be used to refine your interview skills, making you a more prepared and effective candidate in the long run. Additionally, it shows that you're interested in the position and willing to learn, making you a more attractive candidate. Timing is Everything When to ask for feedback is an essential aspect to consider. Ideally, ask for feedback within a few days after the interview. This timeframe ensures that the interviewer has a clear memory of the conversation and that you can still make changes before further interviews or the onboarding process. Be sure to send a follow-up email or make a phone call to request feedback. The tone of the message should be polite and concise, showing appreciation for the interviewer's time and willingness to improve. Crafting the Perfect Request for Feedback Crafting a clear and confident ask for feedback demonstrates your preparedness and professionalism. Consider using phrases like:

"I wanted to thank you again for taking the time to speak with me and I'd appreciate any feedback you can provide on my performance during the interview." "I'd be grateful if you could share any thoughts on how I could improve or what I could do differently in future interviews." "I'm interested in the opportunity and want to ensure I can meet the requirements; any feedback you have would be invaluable to me."

Receiving Feedback When the interviewer responds with feedback, remain open-minded and take it constructively. It's essential to understand that their perspective may differ from yours, but it doesn't discount your abilities as a candidate. There are two types of feedback you should expect to receive:

Informative feedback: Points out your strengths and highlights areas you're successfully performing in, providing insight into how you can excel. Actionable feedback: The interviewer will typically share specific instances of when you excelled or struggled with a particular aspect of the conversation.

Importance of Silence Whether the feedback is positive or negative, maintain silence and listen carefully. Avoid arguing or justifying your actions. Instead, acknowledge the feedback and express gratitude for the time spent providing honest insights. This not only appreciates the interviewer's input but also allows you to thoughtfully process and internalize their feedback. Putting Feedback into Practice Feedback serves as an actionable insight. After receiving feedback, create a plan to improve. If there were areas where you performed well, consider doing more of the same while examining what specific qualities or behaviors made a positive impact. On the other hand, consider the challenges you faced in areas where you struggled. Identify the source of these struggles and brainstorm potential solutions to tackle them the next time you encounter similar situations. Preparing for Future Interviews Feedback helps polish your interviewing skills, enabling you to tackle each opportunity with renewed confidence and poise. Avoid worrying about whether to be liked or approved instead, focus on positively reframing critical feedback to improve your interviewing technique. With these inputs and resources, the days are behind and your focused, compared timed approaches will distinguish you as a professional job seeker.

The Connection Between Sustainability and Mental Health


As the world grapples with climate change, environmental degradation, and social inequality, it's easy to get lost in the complexities of sustainability and the measures we can take to address these pressing issues. However, there's an often-overlooked consequence of our collective actions: the impact on our mental health. Research suggests that the pressures of sustainability can have a profound effect on our mental wellbeing. Living with anxiety about the future, feeling overwhelmed by the sheer volume of environmental problems, and witnessing the destruction of our planet can contribute to a range of negative mental health outcomes, including depression, anxiety disorders, and even suicidal thoughts. This phenomenon is not new; one of the pioneers in the field of mental health, Susan Clark, wrote extensively in the early 2000s about the relationship between human psychology and our environment. She argued that our perception of the natural world greatly affects our happiness and mental well-being. Walking the Green Tightrope While embracing a more sustainable lifestyle can be exceptionally gratifying, doing so in conjunction with societal expectations can induce feelings of dread or dismay. This could manifest in thoughts such as: "If I recycle, will I still contribute to pollution?" or "Is buying locally sourced organic food an equivalent expenditure of my salary compared to other countries?" Doubts like these reside in the struggle between stepping up the sustainability pace while enduring societal reciprocativeness. Fostering empathy in society, in regard to those of us who seek greater environmental awareness, can aid in bridging this sustainability gap. Addressing the power structure underlying global temperatures can moderate these negative correlations with psychological stress. This perspective attempts to dispel myths implying that mental healthlessness is intrinsic to sustainability. Rather, we can commence applying evidence-based tactics — learning mindfulness practices, appreciating the healing power of nature, much like hiking in lush natural sceneries. Ways to Curb Mental Health Toll of Sustainability Efforts There are numerous psychological shortcomings associated with sustainability counseling. One prospective method underscores we promote knowledge on interconnected world events; another approaches mental wellness through widened inter-relational appreciation and acquiescence, enabling incumbent consumers of society (individuals, businesses, institutions) meet their dreams peaceably.

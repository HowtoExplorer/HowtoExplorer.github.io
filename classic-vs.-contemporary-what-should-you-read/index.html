Classic vs. Contemporary: What Should You Read?


In the vast world of literature, the debate between classic and contemporary works often ignites passionate discussions among readers. Each category offers unique insights into the human experience, yet they do so through different lenses. Whether you’re a seasoned bibliophile or just beginning your reading journey, understanding the merits of both classic and contemporary literature can help you make informed choices about what to read next.

Defining the Classics

Classics are the literary works that have stood the test of time, often presenting universal themes and truths that resonate across generations. These are the books that are frequently included in school curricula and considered essential reading for anyone seeking a deeper understanding of literature and culture. Works by authors such as Jane Austen, Charles Dickens, Leo Tolstoy, and Mark Twain have become staples of the literary canon.

The Timeless Themes of Classic Literature

One of the defining characteristics of classic literature is its exploration of timeless themes. Love, betrayal, morality, and the quest for identity are just a few of the profound subjects that classic authors tackle. For example, in “Pride and Prejudice,” Jane Austen delves into the intricacies of social class and romantic entanglements, themes that remain relevant today.

Additionally, classics often offer a window into the historical context of their time. Reading works like “Moby-Dick” or “The Great Gatsby” not only provides insight into the author’s perspective but also into the societal norms and issues of the era. This historical richness can enhance your understanding of contemporary society as you draw parallels between the past and present.

The Language of the Classics

Another aspect that sets classic literature apart is its distinctive language. Many classic works employ a formal and elaborate style that reflects the time in which they were written. This can be both a challenge and a reward for modern readers. While the language may require extra effort to comprehend, it often results in a deeper appreciation for the artistry of the prose.

Moreover, classics can enrich your vocabulary and enhance your understanding of literary devices, as authors like Shakespeare and Dickens masterfully employed metaphors, similes, and symbolism. Engaging with these texts can sharpen your analytical skills and deepen your appreciation for the craft of writing.

The Allure of Contemporary Literature

On the other end of the spectrum lies contemporary literature, which encompasses works written in the late 20th century to the present day. Contemporary authors like Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, Haruki Murakami, and Zadie Smith explore modern themes, often reflecting the complexities of contemporary life, identity, and culture.

Relatability and Relevance

One of the primary appeals of contemporary literature is its relatability. Modern readers often find themselves drawn to characters and situations that mirror their own experiences. The themes of mental health, social justice, and the impact of technology are prevalent in contemporary works, making them resonate with a new generation of readers.

Books like “The Kite Runner” by Khaled Hosseini or “Little Fires Everywhere” by Celeste Ng tackle current social issues, providing insight into the human condition in today’s context. Readers may find solace or understanding in the characters’ struggles, fostering a sense of connection that can be difficult to achieve with older texts.

Evolving Literary Styles

Contemporary literature also showcases a diverse range of styles and genres, from magical realism to experimental narratives. This evolution reflects the changing landscape of society and the myriad voices that contribute to the literary conversation. Authors today often break traditional narrative structures, playing with form and style in innovative ways.

For example, works like “House of Leaves” by Mark Z. Danielewski challenge readers with their unconventional formats and layered storytelling. This experimentation can be thrilling for readers seeking fresh perspectives and new literary experiences.

The Case for Reading Both

As you navigate the literary landscape, consider the benefits of incorporating both classic and contemporary works into your reading list. Each offers unique advantages that can enrich your understanding of literature and the world around you.

Bridging the Gap Between Eras

Reading classics can provide a foundation for understanding contemporary literature. Many modern authors draw inspiration from their predecessors, weaving elements of classic literature into their narratives. By familiarizing yourself with the classics, you may uncover references and allusions in contemporary works that enhance your reading experience.

For example, understanding the themes of existentialism in Dostoevsky’s “Crime and Punishment” can deepen your appreciation for modern novels that explore similar themes, such as “The Stranger” by Albert Camus. This intertextuality allows readers to see the connections between different eras and the evolution of literary thought.

Diverse Perspectives

Incorporating both classic and contemporary literature into your reading can expose you to a wider array of perspectives. Classics often reflect the dominant cultural narratives of their time, which can be limited in scope. Contemporary literature, on the other hand, frequently highlights marginalized voices and diverse experiences that have historically been overlooked.

By engaging with a variety of texts, you cultivate empathy and broaden your worldview. This diversity in reading can enhance your understanding of complex social issues and foster a sense of community with readers from different backgrounds.

Making Your Choice

When deciding between classic and contemporary literature, consider your personal preferences and reading goals. Are you looking for a deep dive into historical themes, or do you seek relatable characters navigating modern dilemmas? Both categories offer valuable insights, and the choice ultimately depends on what resonates with you at this moment in your literary journey.

Tips for Balancing Your Reading

  1. Create a Reading List: Curate a list that includes both classic and contemporary works. This approach allows you to alternate between the two, keeping your reading experience fresh and engaging.

  2. Join a Book Club: Participating in a book club can expose you to titles you might not have chosen on your own, encouraging a balanced reading diet that includes both categories.

  3. Set Reading Goals: Challenge yourself to read a certain number of classics and contemporary works each month. This can help maintain a healthy balance and ensure you’re exploring both literary worlds.

  4. Follow Literary Trends: Stay informed about current literary trends and new releases while also revisiting classic texts. This way, you can appreciate how contemporary writers are influenced by their predecessors.

Conclusion

The debate between classic and contemporary literature is not one of superiority but of preference and purpose. Each genre offers its own set of rewards, and together they create a rich tapestry of literary experience. As you embark on your reading journey, embrace the diversity of voices and themes that both classic and contemporary literature provide.

Whether you find yourself enchanted by the eloquence of Jane Austen or captivated by the raw honesty of a contemporary voice, remember that every book has the power to shape your understanding of the world. So pick up that classic novel or dive into a contemporary bestseller—what matters most is the journey of discovery that lies within the pages. Happy reading!

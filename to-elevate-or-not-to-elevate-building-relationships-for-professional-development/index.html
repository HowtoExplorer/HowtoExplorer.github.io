To Elevate or Not to Elevate: Building Relationships for Professional Development


Building relationships is a crucial aspect of professional growth – it can open doors to new opportunities, foster trust, and aid in navigating the complex maze of the professional world. However, these relationships must be built thoughtfully and strategically. The question remains, is it advantageous to cultivate connections with people higher in the corporate pyramid, or should we focus more on leveling and connecting with peers and those lower in the same hierarchy? In this blog post, we will explore the nuances of building relationships at various hierarchical levels to elevate your professional development.

Advantages of Building Relationships with Higher-Ups

  1. Mentorship Opportunities: Higher-ups possess experience, wisdom, and skills that have led them to their current positions. By building relationships with them, you open the door to mentorship and guidance, helping you to navigate the professional world more effectively.

  2. Strategic Support: A key advantage of building relationships with senior leaders is the potential strategic support they can provide. They can vouch for your abilities, become your sponsors, and help you gain access to challenging projects that further your development.

  3. Visibility and Exposure: Connecting with higher-tier professionals can aid in increasing your visibility within the organization. They can advocate for your ideas, recommend you for promotions or transfers, and ultimately play a crucial role in getting your work noticed.

Disadvantages of Building Relationships with Higher-Ups

  1. Overdependence: When the focus is solely on building relationships with those in authority, there is a risk of becoming overly dependent on them. This can hinder your ability to generate your own opportunities and ideas, leading to a lack of initiative and wrongly secluded reliance on others for professional development.

  2. Time and Effort: The energy and resources required to maintain relationships with those in the upper echelon of the company may not be sustainable for everyone. The higher you go, the more limited access to them becomes, and the effort required to build and maintain a relationship with them often surges exponentially.

Advantages of Building Relationships with Peers

  1. Collaboration and Networking: Building relationships with peers allows for the exchange of ideas and collaboration opportunities, enriching your professional development through shared knowledge and experiences. These relationships can help in creating an extensive and diverse professional network.

  2. Exposure to Different Perspectives: By collaborating with peers, you get to understand different perspectives and approaches to problems, helping you grow into a more well-rounded professional.

Disadvantages of Building Relationships with Peers

  1. Possible Conflicts of Interest: When relying solely on peer relationships, there is an increased likelihood of encountering conflicts of interest, as everyone wants to elevate themselves professionally. This may overshadow collaboration and, in some cases, even harm your own professional development.

  2. Limited Influence: While relationships with peers can foster a collaborative environment, they may not give you the same level of access to high-level decision-making and opportunities as connections with those in higher roles.

Striking a Balance

To optimize professional growth, it’s essential to build relationships across all levels of the organization while keeping the following points in mind:

  1. Authenticity: Focus on building authentic and meaningful connections instead of solely seeking to benefit from these relationships.

  2. Self-reliance: While others can aid in your professional development, it’s essential to remain self-reliant. Acquire new skills, grow your knowledge, and generate your own opportunities to ensure that your professional development is not purely dependent on your relationships.

  3. Diverse Relationships: Develop a diverse network, allowing you to benefit from various perspectives and opportunities.

In conclusion, though building relationships with higher-ups and peers can both contribute significantly to professional development, striking a balance between the two is vital. By nurturing authentic relationships at all levels, maintaining self-reliance, and consistently learning and growing, you can elevate your professional development to new heights. Just remember – it’s not about “to elevate or not to elevate” but about building meaningful connections in a strategic and balanced manner.

5 Myths About Consumer Rights busted - The Truth


Everyone has a responsibility to protect consumer rights and ensure fair business practices. This means that we should be able to rely on the goods we purchase, safely use services without worry, and have a reachable avenue to voice our concerns when faced with subpar treatment. However, with so much information floating around the internet, myths can occur, causing false beliefs and misunderstandings about our rights. This blog post will dispel five common myths concerning consumer rights while providing the truth behind them.

Myth 1: Once you bought an item, it’s yours outright

Truth: When a buyer purchases an item, they own it. However, in some situations, consumer rights might still apply. For example, if an item is discovered to be counterfeit or lost essential components during transportation, the buyer has the legal right to refuse delivery of the item and return their money. This is known as the Implied Warranty of Merchantability.

Myth 2: Online transactions are riskier than in-person purchases

Truth: While it may feel less secure, online transactions generally offer better protection. Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, consumers have a 30-day window to return unwanted or faulty online purchases. This also applies to digital content, with buyers entitled to a full refund regardless of use, as long as they haven’t breached the copyright terms.

Myth 3: You have no say in matters concerning data privacy and personal information

Truth: Your data is your right, and you can request the removal or correction of your data from firms when necessary. The Data Protection Act 2018 grants these rights, particularly when personal information is being processed, ensuring higher standards for data privacy. Furthermore, companies must act promptly with the revision or removal of individual data when requested by the person who owns it.

Myth 4: Going to court is the only option when resolving disputes

Truth: Usually, consumers don’t need to head to court to resolve disputes. Alternative dispute resolution (ADR) methods exist, such as mediation or arbitration, offering quicker and less expensive solutions to many consumer issues. Most websites and services providing a service or product have dedicated consumer rights teams to handle complaints, with some even providing online complaint forms for quicker response times.

Myth 5: Consumers have limited rights with digital purchases

Truth: Actually, digital content is well-protected under consumer law. The Consumer Rights Act 2015 explicitly covers digital content, arranging similar rights to physical goods in cases of faulty products, digital content that differs significantly from the description, or unsatisfactory performance quality. Users could ask for a repair, replacement, or a full refund, depending on the degree of non-conformity with the contract.

It’s crucial always to be well-informed when engaging in any form of commerce. By knowing these truths, you can protect yourself and make better, well-informed decisions. Always remember that consumer rights are there to uphold equality in the seller-consumer dynamic and aim to maintain trust in businesses and transactions within our society.

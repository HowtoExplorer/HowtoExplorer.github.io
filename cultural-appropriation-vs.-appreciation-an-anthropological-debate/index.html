Cultural Appropriation vs. Appreciation: An Anthropological Debate


Cultural appropriation is a complex and sensitive topic that has sparked debates for decades. As anthropologists and sociologists delve deeper into the understanding of cultures, cultures themselves continue to change and evolve. Cultures are not static; they are ever-growing, involving the continuous shaping and reshaping of beliefs, values, and norms. The boundaries between cultural appropriation and appreciation are often blurred. This post seeks to clarify and delve into this contentious anthropological debate.

To begin, let’s break down the key terms. Cultural appropriation refers to the adoption of elements of one culture by members of another culture. It ranges from borrowing cultural elements such as cuisine, dress, and traditions to fully incorporating them into another culture without context or understanding. Cultural appreciation, on the other hand, involves learning and cherishing the rich heritage of a culture, preferably your own, or that of others, in a mutually agreed and honorable manner.

It’s easy to think that cultural appreciation always stays on the right side, whereas cultural appropriation is inherently wrong. However, this view is far from being accurate. Cultural appropriation can be both beneficial and harm-inducing. The appropriation of elements of a culture can lead to the fostering of understanding, cultural diplomacy, and exchanges. It can lead to the cross-pollination of creativity and ideas, leading to innovative discoveries and ground-breaking inventions.

On the other hand, cultural appropriation has driven resentment and anger when it perpetuates negative and stereotyped representations, exploiting the weaker state of minority cultures. Excessive or irresponsible cultural borrowing often leads to misrepresentation and trivialization of cultures. For instance, using a Native American Tribe’s totem poles as a mere fashion statement without an understanding of the Tribal history and beliefs is deeply problematic.

Most debates on cultural appropriation revolve around the ownership of culture. Whose culture is it? Who has the right to appropriate it? This brings us to the topic of intellectual property rights and the broader debate about cultural heritage. Every culture has its elements unique to its identity, and this identity is built upon thousands of years of traditions, rituals, food, dress, customs, music, and more. The idea of intellectual property rights for cultural heritage raises an interesting question: should culture be privatized?

Some argue that culture should be open-source. They believe that culture is meant to be shared, adapted, and changed. Culture is not a fixed entity; it is a fluid that evolves with society, and blending is an integral part of cultural evolution. They argue that it is impossible and counterproductive to attempt to rigidly categorize or protect every element of a culture and impose a barrier to prevent others from engaging with it.

Others argue that because cultures are the collective identities and knowledge of people, they should be protected and controlled just like patents or trademarks. This could prevent appropriation and exploitation, but it also limits access and understanding.

As the anthropology debate continues on the lines between cultural appreciation and appropriation, it is vital to tread with respect and understanding. It is essential to learn from one another while being vigilant against exploitation, and the key lies in open dialogue and constructive discourse. An essential takeaway from this debate is that every culture is valuable and should be treated with equal respect and care.

In conclusion, cultural appropriation and cultural appreciation are two vastly different approaches to sharing and borrowing from one culture to another. While appreciation fosters understanding and diplomatic bridges, excessive cultural appropriation or lack of sensitivity may lead to the erosion and exploitation of minority cultures. Balancing cultural exchange and respect for cultural heritage requires mutual understanding, open dialogue, and a continuously evolving understanding of cultural landscapes.

Credit Score Fluctuations: Understanding the Reasons


Credit scores are a crucial aspect of financial health, determining our ability to secure loans and credit cards. Despite its significance, many people remain confused about why their scores fluctuate. In this post, we’ll dive into the various reasons why your credit score might change and how to navigate these fluctuations.

Reasons for Credit Score Changes

Several factors can lead to fluctuations in your credit score. While maintaining a good credit score requires consistent effort, understanding these factors can help you steer your credit score in the right direction. Let’s explore the common reasons for changes:

1. Late Payments

Late payments can significantly impact your credit score. Payment history makes up around 35% of your credit score. If you consistently miss payments, it reflects poorly on your creditworthiness.

2. Credit Utilization Ratio

Your credit utilization ratio (CUR) - the percentage of your total available credit that you’re using - also plays a significant role in credit score calculations. Keeping your CUR below 30% is ideal since it shows lenders that you’re responsibly managing your credit.

3. Hard Inquiries

Every time you apply for new credit, it results in a hard inquiry on your credit report. While one or two hard inquiries won’t significantly affect your score, multiple applications within a short period can negatively impact your score.

4. Soft Inquiries

Soft inquiries, like those from potential employers or credit card companies offering pre-approved credit, do not affect your credit score and are mainly for personal use. Nonetheless, it’s essential to monitor them and ensure that they don’t turn into hard inquiries.

5. Credit Mix

Having a mix of credit, including credit cards, student loans, mortgages, and auto loans, can positively impact your credit score. Maintaining a responsible balance between revolving debt (credit cards) and installment debt (loans) is crucial.

6. RecUPERating from Past Mistakes

It might take time for negative items on your credit report to fade away, especially if you’ve made mistakes in the past. Late payments, charge-offs, collections, or public records such as bankruptcies can take up to seven years to clear from your credit report.

7. Rechecking Your Credit Report

Monitoring your credit report can help you identify discrepancies and fraudulent activities in your credit report. However, each time you check your credit report, it counts as a soft inquiry - which does not affect your score, unless you’re checking your report frequently within a short period.

Managing Fluctuations

Keeping a close watch on your credit report is essential to ensure that any fluctuations in your credit score are tracked and rectified, if necessary. Regularly monitoring your credit report allows you to detect fraudulent activities or errors on your report and take quick action to address any issues.

Moreover, maintaining good financial hygiene practices can help minimize unexpected score variations:

  1. Pay bills on time.
  2. Keep an eye on your credit utilization ratio.
  3. Limit applying for new credit unless necessary.
  4. Manage a mix of credit responsibly.
  5. Avoid bankruptcy or engaging in predatory lending.
  6. Monitor your credit report regularly.

Be patient and committed to your financial health. Making responsible credit decisions takes time, but the payoff is worth it. Remember that improving your credit score doesn’t happen overnight, but practicing good financial habits will set you on the path to a solid credit score.

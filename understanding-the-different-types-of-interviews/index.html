Understanding the Different Types of Interviews


When it comes to interviewing for a new job or as a journalist, the words "interview" and "conversation" are often used interchangeably. However, the truth is that there are several types of interviews, each with its own unique characteristics, goals, and best practices. Panel Interviews A panel interview, also known as a panel discussion or group interview, takes place when multiple interviewers sit with a single candidate. This type of interview involves a series of questions and discussions with each panel member, allowing the candidate to showcase their skills and experience to a diverse group of professionals. In a panel interview, the candidate may be asked to engage with each panel member individually, or participate in a group discussion. This format is commonly used in academia, in the medical field, and in certain corporate environments where the candidate needs to interact with multiple stakeholders. Behavioral Interviews Behavioral interviews, also known as competency-based interviews, focus on the candidate's past experiences and behaviors as a way to predict their future performance. This type of interview is based on the idea that past behavior is a reliable indicator of future performance. In a behavioral interview, the candidate is typically presented with a scenario or question and asked to provide specific examples from their past experiences. The candidate's response should demonstrate how they handled the situation, what they learned from it, and how they would apply those skills in the future. Scripted Interviews A scripted interview, also known as a standardized interview, is a type of interview where the candidate is presented with a series of prepared questions or scenarios. The interviewer may read from a script or use a template to guide the conversation. Scripted interviews are often used in academic or research settings, where the interviewer is trying to collect specific data or information. In these situations, the interviewer may ask very direct and technical questions to elicit precise responses from the candidate. Unstructured Interviews Unstructured interviews, also known as conversational interviews, involve a free-flowing, unscripted conversation between the interviewer and candidate. This type of interview may not have a specific agenda or set of questions, but rather allows the interviewer to explore the candidate's thoughts and experiences in a more natural and spontaneous way. Unstructured interviews are often used in fields such as social sciences, where the interviewer may be trying to gather rich, qualitative data from the candidate. This type of interview allows the interviewer to explore topics and themes that may not have been anticipated ahead of time. Telephone Interviews Telephone interviews, also known as phone screenings, take place over the phone, where the candidate is asked to participate in what can be seen as a more formal conversation. This type of interview is often used as an initial screening process, where the interviewer may be trying to gauge the candidate's knowledge, experience, and fit for the role. Telephone interviews can be challenging for the candidate, who may not have the opportunity to ask follow-up questions or respond to previous questions in as much depth. However, they can also provide a more convenient and flexible option for both the interviewer and candidate. Video Interviews Video interviews, also known as virtual interviews or digital interviews, involve using digital tools, such as video conferencing software, to conduct the interview. This type of interview has become increasingly popular, especially in today's remote work environment. Video interviews can offer more flexibility and convenience for both the interviewer and candidate, while also having its own set of challenges. However, when done correctly, video interviews can allow for a more immersive and engaging experience for all parties involved. Assessment Centers Assessment centers are structured interview processes, designed to test the candidate's technical, behavioral, and psychological skills. This type of interview often involves multiple interviewers, assessment exercises, and other evaluation tools to gauge the candidate's fit for the role. Assessment centers are often used in large corporations and organizations, where the candidate is being considered for a senior leadership position or a complex technology role. These assessment centers are specifically designed to assess the candidate's skills, knowledge, and fit for the organization. Practical Examinations Practical examinations are assessment exercises that test the candidate's technical, creative, or practical skills. This type of interview may involve hands-on activities, projects, or presentations where the candidate is asked to showcase their skills and abilities. Practical examinations are often used in fields such as programming, engineering, or design, where the candidate needs to demonstrate their skills and expertise in a practical setting. Wrap-up In conclusion, there are several types of interviews, each with its own unique characteristics, goals, and best practices. Whether it's a panel interview, behavioral interview, or practical examination, it's essential to understand the type of interview you're facing and prepare accordingly. By understanding the different types of interviews, you can better prepare for the conversation and showcase your skills and experiences to the interviewer. Remember to research the company and position, come prepared with examples and stories, and be open and enthusiastic about the opportunity. The next time you face an interview, remember to ask questions, be yourself, and showcase your skills and experiences to the best of your ability. Good luck!

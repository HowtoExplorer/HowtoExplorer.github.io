The Art of Writing Dialogue: Tips from Great Authors


Effective dialogue is a crucial aspect of writing a novel. It brings characters to life, reveals their personalities, and advances the plot. Yet, many writers struggle to craft dialogue that sounds natural and authentic. The good news is that great authors have shared their tips and tricks for writing compelling dialogue. Let's dive into the world of great writers and explore their advice. Mastering Subtext: The Art of Suggestion Author Joyce Carol Oates often emphasizes the importance of subtext in dialogue. Subtext refers to the underlying emotions, ideas, or motivations that are not explicitly stated. Oates suggests that subtext should be woven throughout a character's dialogue, providing a richer understanding of their thoughts and feelings. For example: "I'm fine," said Emily, trying to hide her frustration. "No, you're not," replied her sister. "You're upset because Mom and Dad don't understand you." This exchange suggests that Emily is feeling misunderstood and frustrated, but her sister is astute enough to pick up on her emotions. This subtle exchange allows the reader to infer Emily's true feelings, creating a more nuanced character. Dialogue Tags: A Matter of Form Another important aspect of dialogue is the use of tags. A dialogue tag is a word or phrase that accompany dialogue to indicate who is speaking. However, many authors believe that dialogue tags can be overused and detract from the conversation. Author Ernest Hemingway's minimalist approach to writing suggests that dialogue tags should be limited and only used when necessary. For instance, rather than using "said" or "asked" repeatedly, a writer can use action beats or the natural flow of the conversation to indicate who is speaking. Consider this example: "Don't worry about it, it's just a car accident," said Jack. Emily rolled her eyes. "You're one to talk. You're always driving like a maniac!" In this exchange, the dialogue tag is used judiciously, allowing the reader to infer the conversation's progression. Avoiding On-Page Echoes: The Unnatural Flow of Conversation Sometimes, writers make the mistake of using a character's exact words in a subsequent conversation. This is known as an on-page echo, and it can make the dialogue feel stilted and unnatural. Author Neil Gaiman suggests that if a character says something profound, it's better to let them say it once, rather than repeating it ad nauseam. For example: "I have a message for the people of the world," declared the professor. "Be kind to one another, and always serve the greater good." His student scribbled furiously in her notebook. "That's amazing. I'll write it down. Be kind to one another, and always serve the greater good." The repetition of the professor's words comes across as awkward, taking away from the power of the original statement. Show, Don't Tell: Dialogue as Action Author George Orwell emphasizes the importance of using dialogue as a means of showing, rather than telling. This implies that a writer should trust the reader to infer a character's emotions and motivations from their actions and words, rather than spelling it out for them. Consider this example: "What's with the silly grin on your face?" asked Sarah, eyeing her friend's antics. "Don't you remember that I just won the lottery?" replied Tom, chuckling. The reader is allowed to infer that Tom's grin is due to winning the lottery, rather than being explicitly told. This creates a more immersive and engaging reading experience. Authenticity: Capturing the Sound and Cadence of Speech Author David Foster Wallace believed that dialogue should capture the nuances of everyday speech, rather than trying to recreate a perfect, polished version of the language. This involves experimenting with different dialects, idioms, and sentence structures to reflect the unique voice of each character. For example: "Dunno, hab init comin in doo Jan[y]'? he muttered under his breath." This snippet of conversation evokes the sounds and cadences of everyday speech, creating a more authentic and engaging character. Becoming Silent: The Power of Empty Space Finally, some great authors suggest that empty space can be just as powerful as the written word. Leaving out dialogue can create a sense of tension or anticipation, or even give the reader a chance to reflect on what's been said. Author Don DeLillo uses this technique in his novel "Underworld" to convey the impacts of a series of catastrophic events on the characters. At one point, a conversation between two characters is described, but their words are left unwritten, adding to the sense of loss and confusion. In this example, the absence of dialogue is used to create a powerful and evocative scene that leaves the reader to process the emotions and ideas. Conclusion Writing effective dialogue requires attention to detail and a willingness to experiment with different techniques and forms. By incorporating subtext, mastering dialogue tags, avoiding on-page echoes, using dialogue as action, capturing authenticity, and becoming silent, writers can create compelling dialogue that brings their characters to life and advances the plot. By studying great authors, writers can refine their craft and produce dialogue that resonates with readers.

Found in Translation: Must-Read Books from Around the World


Books serve as windows to the world, inviting us into the lives of people, cultures, and places we may never have the chance to visit in person. Translation allows us to experience these stories without having to leave our homes. It grants us the ability to wander through the imaginations of authors from various walks of life, broadening our understanding of the world and its inhabitants. In this blog post, we will dive into a collection of must-read books translated from different languages, each offering a unique glimpse into the essence of human existence. So, prepare to embark on an exciting literary journey!

Our first stop is “The Shadow of the Wind” by Spanish author Carlos Ruiz Zafón. This thrilling novel, translated by Lucia Graves, is set in post-war Barcelona. The story follows a young boy named Daniel who accidentally discovers a mysterious novel called “The Shadow of the Wind.” As he endeavors to learn more about the book and its enigmatic author, he uncovers a labyrinth of secrets, betrayals, and lost loves. The novel beautifully weaves together suspense, love, and loss, offering a vivid sense of the past and present-day Spain.

Next, let’s venture into the depths of Russian literature with “The Brothers Karamazov” by Fyodor Dostoevsky. This philosophical masterpiece, translated by Richard Pevear and Larissa Volokhonsky, explores themes of faith, morality, and familial relations. The narrative is dominated by the enigmatic personalities of its characters and the intricate web of deceit and revenge that ensnares them. This prolific work inspires readers to grapple with existential questions and reminds us of the complexity of the human psyche.

Jumping across continents, we land in Japan with “Norwegian Wood” by Haruki Murakami. Originally written in Japanese, this novel has been masterfully translated by Jay Rubin. The story is narrated by Toru Watanabe, a young college student who becomes entangled in a love triangle that spirals into unexpected depths. Murakami’s unique blend of postmodernism and folklore evokes a melancholic atmosphere that lingers long after the final page is turned. The novel delves into themes surrounding love, loss, and individual identity.

Our next book is “Midnight’s Children” by Salman Rushdie, an Indian-British author. This captivating novel, translated by Amit Chaudhuri, won the prestigious Booker Prize in 1981. Set during the time of India’s partition, the story follows Saleem Sinai, a boy born at the stroke of midnight between August 14th and 15th in 1947. Saleem discovers that he has special abilities connected to his being one of 1,001 children born in the same hour, and his life becomes intertwined with the history of India. The novel is a kaleidoscopic blend of history, magic, and bold storytelling.

We conclude our global journey with “The Algebra of Infinite Justice” by Arundhati Roy, also an Indian author. This collection of essays, translated by Biswadeep Banerjee and nutan Kumar Bachar, provides a fearless examination of contemporary Indian society. From the power struggles to the environmental devastation, Roy holds a mirror up to the challenges faced by her country and sheds light on the ongoing battles that continue to define India’s future. This collection offers profound insights into political inequality, corporate greed, and, most importantly, the resilience of the human spirit.

In conclusion, every page turned on these captivating translations offers a new perspective on the world. By delving into the works of authors from diverse cultures, lands, and experiences, we enrich our global awareness and, in turn, gain a deeper understanding of our shared humanity. The beauty of books lies in their ability to transcend language, culture, and borders, providing access to stories and ideas that may never otherwise reach our minds. So, let us continue to celebrate the magic of translation and uncover more narratives that illuminate the many worlds we inhabit.

Happy reading!

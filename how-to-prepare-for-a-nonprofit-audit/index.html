How to Prepare for a Nonprofit Audit


Preparing for a nonprofit audit can be a daunting but crucial task for any organization. Audits serve as an essential financial health check, ensuring compliance with laws and regulations while providing transparency to stakeholders. Proper preparation can help your nonprofit demonstrate accountability and trustworthiness. This guide will walk you through the essential steps to get your organization audit-ready. Understanding the Purpose of an Audit Before diving into preparation strategies, it's crucial to understand why an audit is necessary. Audits verify a nonprofit's financial statements and can assess the organization’s compliance with financial regulations and internal policies. They can help identify areas for improvement, enhance financial efficiency, and build trust among donors, stakeholders, and grantors. Steps to Prepare for a Nonprofit Audit

  1. Establish a Timeline The first step in preparing for an audit is establishing a timeline. Knowing when the audit will take place will help you create a checklist of necessary documents and tasks. Start by:

Setting a Date: Coordinate with your auditor to choose an appropriate time. Creating a Timeline: Break down the preparation tasks into manageable segments leading up to the audit date.

  1. Gather Financial Documents Financial documents are the backbone of any audit. An organized approach to gathering these documents is essential. Common documents required include:

Financial Statements: Balance sheets, income statements, and cash flow statements. Bank Statements: Ensure they align with your financial statements for the audit period. Tax Returns: Collect the most recent IRS Form 990 and any state tax filings. Revenue Documentation: This includes donation records, grant agreements, and fundraising event income. Expense Records: Maintain documentation for all expenses, including receipts and invoices for transparency.

  1. Organize Policies and Procedures An audit isn’t limited to just financials. Your nonprofit's internal policies and procedures play an integral role in ensuring compliance. Organize these documents and ensure they are current:

Bylaws: Review and confirm they are up to date, reflecting your organization’s governance. Financial Policies: Maintain a clear set of financial procedures, including how funds are handled and spent. Human Resource Policies: Ensure employment records are in order, including contracts, payroll records, and benefits documentation.

  1. Implement Internal Controls Establishing strong internal controls not only prepares your nonprofit for an audit but also combats fraud and errors. Consider:

Separation of Duties: Divide responsibilities among staff to minimize risks. For example, the person who handles cash should not also handle accounting records. Regular Reconciliations: Regularly reconcile bank statements with your accounting records to catch discrepancies early. Documentation of Processes: Ensure that all processes are documented so that staff knows how to adhere to procedures consistently.

  1. Conduct a Pre-Audit Conducting a self-audit or pre-audit can help identify areas that require attention before the official audit begins.

Review Financial Records: Go through financial statements and internal reports to catch errors or inconsistencies. Check Compliance: Ensure that your organization complies with federal, state, and local regulations. Assess Risks: Identify any potential risks that might arise during the formal audit and develop strategies to address them.

  1. Engage Your Board Involving your board members in the audit preparation process is crucial. Their oversight can enhance accountability and transparency.

Board Meetings: Discuss the audit process during board meetings, allowing members to ask questions and express concerns. Audit Committee: If your organization has an audit committee, engage them to provide oversight and support during preparation. Training: Offer training to board members about their roles during the audit process.

  1. Choose the Right Auditor Selecting the right auditor can make a significant difference in the smoothness of the audit. It's essential to choose someone with nonprofit auditing experience.

Research Available Auditors: Look for professionals or firms that specialize in nonprofit audits. Interviews: Schedule interviews to gauge their understanding of nonprofit regulations and financial reporting. References: Ask for references to get insight into their previous work with nonprofit organizations.

  1. Create an Audit Preparation Checklist Developing a comprehensive checklist will keep you organized and ensure nothing is overlooked. Organizing this checklist into sections can add clarity: Financial Documents

Financial statements Bank statements Tax forms (990, state filings)

Policies and Procedures

Bylaws Financial policies Employee handbooks

Internal Controls

Separation of duties Reconciliation procedures Internal audit self-assessment

  1. Communication is Key Effective communication with your auditor before and during the audit can ease the process. Be open and transparent:

Share Key Information: Provide your auditors with any initial findings from your pre-audit. Keep Lines Open: Maintain ongoing communication throughout the audit for questions or clarifications. Gather Feedback: Encourage auditors to provide feedback for ongoing improvements post-audit.

  1. Training Your Staff Ensure that your staff understands their role in the audit process. Providing training makes them aware of the importance of compliance and accuracy.

Workshops: Host workshops focusing on proper documentation and compliance practices. Role Clarity: Clearly outline each team member's responsibilities concerning financial documentation and reporting.

  1. Prepare for Questions Anticipating questions from your auditors can save time and reduce stress. Review possible questions based on past audits or common areas of probe, such as:

Revenue Sources: Be ready to explain how funds are raised and used. Expense Justifications: Have documentation on hand to justify significant expenses. Policies Followed: Prepare to discuss how policies are implemented in daily operations.

  1. Create a Post-Audit Plan Once the audit is complete, it’s essential to have a plan in place for addressing findings and implementing recommendations:

Review Report: Go over the audit report thoroughly for areas of concern or recommendations. Set Action Steps: Define clear action steps to address any issues raised in the audit report. Communicate Results: Share audit findings with the board and staff to promote transparency.

Conclusion Preparing for a nonprofit audit is not merely a task; it is an opportunity for growth, improvement, and increased accountability. By following these steps, your organization will be better equipped to navigate the audit process, resulting in a positive experience for all involved. With thorough preparation, open communication, and a commitment to excellence, your nonprofit can emerge from its audit more robust and more secure in its financial practices. Remember, an audit is not the end; it is a pathway to continual improvement and trust among your stakeholders.

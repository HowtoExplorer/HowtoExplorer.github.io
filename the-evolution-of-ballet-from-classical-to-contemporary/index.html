The Evolution of Ballet: From Classical to Contemporary


Ballet has been a cornerstone of Western classical dance for centuries, evolving from a traditional, formalized art form to a diverse and dynamic expression of creativity. Its history can be seen in the various styles that have emerged over time, shaping the face of this beautiful and elegant art form. The Classical Era 1600s Ballet originated in Italy in the 15th century, where it was known as "balletto," a name derived from the Italian word "balletto," meaning small dance. The first classical ballets were performed at the Italian court, where they served as entertainment for the nobility. As the art form spread across Europe, it became a staple of royal courts and social gatherings. Pierre Beauchamp's codification of ballet in the 17th century led to the development of the five basic positions of the feet, known as the "basic position of the feet." These five positions have remained largely the same to this day and are still taught to ballet dancers all over the world. One of the most famous ballets of this era is "Le Ballet Comique de la Reine," which premiered in 1581. This work is considered one of the earliest ballets in history and is the oldest surviving ballet with a known script. It is a significant milestone in the evolution of ballet, showcasing its early development into a distinct art form. The Romantic Era 1830s-1850s In the 19th century, ballet underwent significant changes. The Romantic period saw a focus on expressive and emotive performances, characterized by elaborate costumes and dramatic settings. This era was marked by a growing emphasis on individual expression and the presentation of fantasy and mythology. The Romantic era was also a time of innovation in ballet technique, particularly in the development of the grand jeté, a movement that became a hallmark of ballet's expressive and fluid form. During this period, ballet companies began to establish themselves in major cities, providing a platform for artists to showcase their talents. One of the most famous ballets of this era is "Giselle," which premiered in 1841. Set in a picturesque village, this ballet explores themes of love, loyalty, and sacrifice, reflecting the dramatic and romantic qualities of this era. The Neoclassical Era 1890s-1940s The 20th century saw a shift in ballet's focus, marked by a return to classical principles and cleaner, more refined aesthetics. The "ballet blanc" emerged as a dominant style, emphasizing purity of movement and clean lines. This approach is characterized by its white costumes and beautifully choreographed tutus. During this era, famous choreographers such as George Balanchine and Les Ballets Russes revolutionized ballet by introducing new and innovative techniques. The De Basil Ballet Russes company's production of "Petrushka," featuring leotards and tutus, was a defining moment in ballet's history, demonstrating the versatility and beauty of the art form. The Modern and Contemporary Era 1950s-Present In the mid-20th century, ballet began to fragment into various styles, styles such as neoclassical and contemporary. Techniques continued to evolve, incorporating new influences from various dance styles. International Ballet Finals emerged, including ballet duets like Alene Deorbes and Russell Fusion, solidifying ballet's status as an integral part of the world dance scene. Contemporary ballet, an innovative style that strays from traditional ballet technique, appears to be an ever-changing canvas. Choreographers continue to experiment with new techniques, merging ballet with other dance styles and unconventional methods. The Contemporary Scene Today, contemporary ballet is a reflection of the physical world we live in. Butoh, capoeira, and ballet fusion continue to influence and change the face of classical ballet. Contemporary ballets are created by combining different cultural influences, inviting nuance, emotiveness, and togetherness to audiences worldwide. Anywhere, from mental states expressed through movement to imaginative territories with LED lights and converted dance spaces, ballet continues to grow and seek more identities.

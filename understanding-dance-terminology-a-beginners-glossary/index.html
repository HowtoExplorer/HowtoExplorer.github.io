Understanding Dance Terminology: A Beginner's Glossary


As a beginner in the world of dance, you're probably aware that there's a plethora of jargon thrown around the studio, social media, and dance communities. From choreographic terms to choreographic style descriptions, understanding the dance terminology can be overwhelming. In this comprehensive guide, we'll break down the most commonly used words and phrases to familiarize you with the language of dance. Adagio

Adagio is a musical direction that translates to "in a slow, beautiful style" in Italian. In dance, it refers to a slow and dramatic movement, often used in classical ballet. However, adagio can also be applied to other styles of dance, such as contemporary and modern. When performed in an adagio movement, dancers focus on rendering elegance and control, exuding an ethereal quality. Alarmer

An alarmer is a violin or keyboard instrument typically used in dance classes to signal the start and end of classes. For instance, when the teacher rings the alarmer, students are attracted to the voice and follow the sound to the next class or location. Ameliorate

Ameliorate implies an improvement or betterment. In dance, it's used to describe the refinement of a movement or technique. When dancers ameliorate their pliés, they master the art of shifting their weight, balance, and posture to improve the quality of the movement. Arpeggio

An arpeggio is a broken chord on a musical keyboard. But what does it mean in dance? An arpeggio in dance is a series of movements or step combos that mimic the distillation of movement in a sequence. Essentially, an arpeggio dissects a single line or phrase into smaller components that show the transition from one movement to another with apparent fluidity and cohesiveness. Many world styles and techniques such as Disco and contemporary take cue from arpeggio movements. Cadenza

A cadenza is a solo section in music, typically written for a solo instrument. In dance, a cadenza can be a passage of music that requires a dancer to showcase their expressive qualities, often highlighting their musicality and technical skills. Contour

An element of movement through space, contour is an essential aspect of dance technique. Contour is the shape of the body as it moves through space, taking into account its size, proportion, and visual impact. Dancers use contour to create dynamic and expressive movements that convey emotion and tell a story. Fragile

Fragile refers to a movement quality that is delicate and easily disrupted. In dance, fragile movements require a high level of control and precision to execute correctly. Fragile movements often involve subtle changes in weight, tension, or release, and can be used to create a sense of vulnerability or fragility. Interlude

An interlude is a short pause or break in a dance, often marked by a change in music or tempo. Interludes can be used to create contrast, add drama, or provide a moment of relief in a sequence of movements. Principals

A principal dancer is a lead dancer who typically performs the most demanding and visible roles in a ballet. Principals are typically senior dancers with extensive training and experience. Pirouette

A pirouette is a turn on one leg, typically performed on the toe or heel. Pirouettes require strength, flexibility, and control, and are used to add dynamic movement and flow to a dance. Sauté

Sauté is a term used in both dance and cooking. In dance, sauté refers to a quick, energetic movement that involves rapid changes in direction and speed. Sauté movements are often used in ballet and contemporary dance to add energy and dynamism. Timbre

Timbre refers to the unique tone and quality of a sound. In dance, timbre can be used to describe the distinctive sound of a movement or step, such as the soft rustle of fabrics or the clatter of heels on the floor. Turn

A turn is a fundamental movement in dance that involves rotation of the body around a central axis. Turns are used to create dynamic movement, add fluidity, and convey emotions. By understanding these dance terms, you'll be better equipped to communicate with your dance partners, teachers, and fellow dancers. Remember, dance is a language, and speaking its vocabulary will help you become a more expressive and confident dancer. Happy dancing!

How to Develop a Nonprofit Crisis Communication Plan


In the world of nonprofits, effective communication is crucial, especially during a crisis. A well-structured crisis communication plan can help your organization navigate challenging situations, maintain trust with stakeholders, and protect your reputation. This guide will walk you through the essential steps to develop a comprehensive crisis communication plan tailored for your nonprofit.

Understanding the Importance of a Crisis Communication Plan

A crisis can strike at any moment, whether it’s a natural disaster, a financial scandal, or a public relations issue. For nonprofits, the stakes are particularly high. A crisis can jeopardize funding, damage relationships with donors, and undermine the mission of the organization. Therefore, having a crisis communication plan in place is not just a precaution; it’s a necessity.

Key Benefits of a Crisis Communication Plan

  1. Preparedness: A plan ensures that your organization is ready to respond quickly and effectively to any crisis.
  2. Consistency: It provides a framework for consistent messaging, which is vital in maintaining trust and credibility.
  3. Stakeholder Confidence: A well-executed plan can reassure stakeholders that your organization is capable of handling crises.
  4. Reputation Management: Timely and transparent communication can help mitigate damage to your organization’s reputation.

Step 1: Identify Potential Crises

The first step in developing a crisis communication plan is to identify the types of crises your nonprofit might face. This involves brainstorming potential scenarios that could impact your organization. Consider the following categories:

  • Natural Disasters: Floods, earthquakes, or other environmental crises that could disrupt operations.
  • Financial Issues: Budget cuts, loss of funding, or financial mismanagement.
  • Public Relations Issues: Negative media coverage, social media backlash, or controversies involving staff or board members.
  • Operational Crises: Issues related to service delivery, such as program failures or safety incidents.

Conducting a Risk Assessment

Once you have identified potential crises, conduct a risk assessment to evaluate the likelihood and impact of each scenario. This will help prioritize which crises require more detailed planning and resources.

Step 2: Establish a Crisis Communication Team

A crisis communication team is essential for effective response and management. This team should include key personnel from various departments, such as:

  • Executive Director: Responsible for overall decision-making and communication.
  • Communications Director: Manages messaging and media relations.
  • Program Managers: Provide insights into specific programs affected by the crisis.
  • Legal Counsel: Ensures compliance with legal requirements and mitigates liability.

Defining Roles and Responsibilities

Clearly define the roles and responsibilities of each team member. This will streamline communication and ensure that everyone knows their tasks during a crisis. Create a contact list with phone numbers and email addresses for quick access.

Step 3: Develop Key Messages

Crafting key messages is a critical component of your crisis communication plan. These messages should be clear, concise, and tailored to your audience. Consider the following elements when developing your key messages:

  • Acknowledge the Crisis: Recognize the situation and its impact on stakeholders.
  • Provide Facts: Share accurate information to dispel rumors and misinformation.
  • Express Empathy: Show understanding and compassion for those affected by the crisis.
  • Outline Next Steps: Communicate what actions your organization is taking to address the situation.

Tailoring Messages for Different Audiences

Different stakeholders may require different messages. Tailor your communication for various audiences, including:

  • Donors: Reassure them of your organization’s stability and commitment to the mission.
  • Volunteers: Keep them informed about any changes in operations or programs.
  • Clients/Beneficiaries: Provide updates on services and support available during the crisis.
  • Media: Prepare a press release with factual information and a designated spokesperson.

Step 4: Create a Communication Protocol

Establishing a communication protocol is essential for ensuring timely and effective dissemination of information. Consider the following elements:

Communication Channels

Identify the channels you will use to communicate during a crisis. These may include:

  • Email: For direct communication with stakeholders.
  • Social Media: For real-time updates and engagement with the public.
  • Website: A dedicated crisis page can provide ongoing updates and resources.
  • Press Releases: For formal communication with the media.

Approval Process

Define an approval process for messages to ensure that all communications are vetted and authorized before dissemination. This may involve a quick review by the crisis communication team or legal counsel, depending on the nature of the crisis.

Step 5: Develop a Crisis Communication Timeline

Timing is critical during a crisis. Develop a timeline that outlines when and how information will be communicated. Consider the following:

  • Immediate Response: Initial communication should occur within hours of the crisis to acknowledge the situation.
  • Ongoing Updates: Provide regular updates as new information becomes available.

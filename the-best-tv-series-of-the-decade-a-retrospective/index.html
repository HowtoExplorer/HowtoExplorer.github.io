The Best TV Series of the Decade: A Retrospective


As we step into the new decade, it’s time to reflect on the television series that have defined and transformed our viewing experiences from 2010 to 2020. This era has been marked by an explosion of creativity and innovation, with shows that have pushed boundaries and captivated audiences across various genres. From gripping dramas to hilarious comedies, the best TV series of the decade have left an indelible mark on popular culture. In this retrospective, we will explore some of the standout series that not only entertained us but also sparked conversations and challenged norms.

Breaking Bad (2008-2013)

While it technically began in 2008, the impact of Breaking Bad resonated throughout the decade, solidifying its place in the pantheon of great television. Created by Vince Gilligan, the series follows Walter White, a high school chemistry teacher turned methamphetamine manufacturer. With its intricate storytelling and compelling character arcs, Breaking Bad is a masterclass in character development. Bryan Cranston’s portrayal of Walter White showcases a transformation from a sympathetic protagonist to a morally ambiguous antihero. The show’s meticulous attention to detail and its exploration of themes like morality, family, and the consequences of one’s choices have made it a cultural touchstone.

Game of Thrones (2011-2019)

Based on George R.R. Martin’s book series A Song of Ice and Fire, Game of Thrones became a global phenomenon during its eight-season run. The fantasy drama captivated viewers with its intricate political plots, complex characters, and unexpected plot twists. The series’ ability to blur the lines between good and evil kept audiences on the edge of their seats. Iconic moments, such as the infamous Red Wedding and the rise of Daenerys Targaryen, became part of pop culture lexicon. Despite a polarizing final season, the show’s impact on television is undeniable, influencing countless series in the fantasy genre and beyond.

The Crown (2016-Present)

The Crown, created by Peter Morgan, offers a stunning portrayal of the reign of Queen Elizabeth II. With its lush cinematography and exceptional performances, particularly by Claire Foy and Olivia Colman, the series captures the complexities of monarchy and personal sacrifice. The show’s meticulous research and historical accuracy provide a fascinating glimpse into pivotal moments in British history. As viewers delve into the private lives of the royal family, The Crown raises questions about duty, power, and the personal cost of public service.

Stranger Things (2016-Present)

Netflix’s Stranger Things is a nostalgic homage to 1980s pop culture, blending horror, science fiction, and coming-of-age drama. Created by the Duffer Brothers, the series follows a group of kids in the fictional town of Hawkins, Indiana, as they encounter supernatural phenomena and government conspiracies. The show’s clever writing, memorable characters, and a killer soundtrack have made it a beloved favorite among audiences of all ages. With its perfect blend of suspense and humor, Stranger Things has not only entertained but also sparked a resurgence of interest in 80s nostalgia.

Fleabag (2016-2019)

Phoebe Waller-Bridge’s Fleabag revolutionized the comedy genre with its raw honesty and innovative storytelling. The series follows a young woman navigating life and love in London while grappling with grief and personal demons. Waller-Bridge’s sharp wit and fourth-wall-breaking narrative style create an intimate connection with viewers. The show’s exploration of themes like female identity, relationships, and mental health resonated deeply, earning critical acclaim and multiple awards. Fleabag is a testament to the power of personal storytelling and its ability to connect us all.

The Mandalorian (2019-Present)

As the first live-action series in the Star Wars franchise, The Mandalorian introduced a fresh narrative and aesthetic that captivated both die-hard fans and newcomers alike. Created by Jon Favreau, the series follows the journey of a lone bounty hunter in the outer reaches of the galaxy. Its unique blend of western motifs and sci-fi elements, combined with the iconic character of Baby Yoda, has made The Mandalorian a cultural sensation. The series not only expands the Star Wars universe but also showcases the potential of streaming platforms in delivering high-quality content.

The Handmaid’s Tale (2017-Present)

Based on Margaret Atwood’s dystopian novel, The Handmaid’s Tale explores themes of oppression, reproductive rights, and the fight for autonomy. Elisabeth Moss delivers a powerful performance as Offred, a woman living in a totalitarian society where women are stripped of their rights. The show’s haunting portrayal of a bleak future resonates with contemporary social issues, making it a poignant commentary on gender and power dynamics. Its critical acclaim and influence on feminist discourse highlight the importance of storytelling in addressing societal challenges.

Chernobyl (2019)

The Chernobyl miniseries stands out as a harrowing retelling of the 1986 nuclear disaster. Created by Craig Mazin, the series meticulously reconstructs the events leading up to and following the catastrophic explosion at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant. With its gripping narrative and haunting performances, particularly by Jared Harris and Stellan Skarsgård, the show delves into themes of truth, accountability, and the human cost of negligence. Chernobyl not only serves as a historical account but also as a cautionary tale about the consequences of power and deception.

Schitt’s Creek (2015-2020)

Schitt’s Creek, created by Eugene and Dan Levy, is a heartwarming comedy that chronicles the lives of the wealthy Rose family as they adapt to life in a small town after losing their fortune. The series masterfully blends humor with poignant moments, exploring themes of family, love, and personal growth. The character development, particularly that of David and Alexis Rose, resonated with viewers, leading to a devoted fanbase. With its inclusive representation and uplifting message, Schitt’s Creek has left a lasting legacy in the world of comedy.

Better Call Saul (2015-Present)

As a prequel to Breaking Bad, Better Call Saul explores the transformation of Jimmy McGill into the morally flexible lawyer Saul Goodman. Created by Vince Gilligan and Peter Gould, the series is a slow-burn character study that delves into themes of ambition, morality, and the gray areas of the law. Bob Odenkirk’s nuanced performance brings depth to a character initially seen as a comic relief. The show’s intricate storytelling and strong character development have garnered critical acclaim, making it a standout in the era of prestige television.

The Queen’s Gambit (2020)

Although it premiered at the tail end of the decade, The Queen’s Gambit quickly became a cultural phenomenon. Based on Walter Tevis’s novel, the limited series follows the life of Beth Harmon, a chess prodigy navigating her way through personal struggles and societal challenges in a male-dominated world. Anya Taylor-Joy’s captivating performance and the series’ stunning visuals brought chess into the limelight, inspiring a new generation of players. The show’s themes of resilience, addiction, and the pursuit of excellence struck a chord with audiences, solidifying its place among the best TV series of the decade.

Conclusion

The past decade has been a remarkable time for television, with a diverse array of series that have pushed boundaries and redefined storytelling. From the morally complex narratives of Breaking Bad and Better Call Saul to the cultural critiques of The Handmaid’s Tale and Fleabag, these shows have not only entertained but also provoked thought and discussion. The rise of streaming platforms has allowed for innovative storytelling and greater creative freedom, leading to a golden age of television.

As we move forward, it will be exciting to see how the landscape of TV continues to evolve, but the series of the past decade will undoubtedly remain influential for years to come. Whether through laughter, tears, or moments of reflection, these shows have left an unforgettable mark on our hearts and minds, reminding us of the power of storytelling in capturing the complexities of the human experience.

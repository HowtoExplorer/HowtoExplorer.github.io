Moral Philosophy: Examining Ethical Theories


Moral philosophy is the branch of philosophy that examines questions about right and wrong, good and evil, and the nature of morality. Its primary concern is to understand and define moral concepts, what makes an action right or wrong, and the norms and rules we follow in our everyday life. Throughout history, several ethical theories have been developed to address these questions, and in this post, we’ll explore some of the most prominent ones.

Consequentialism

Consequentialism is an umbrella term for a group of moral theories that determined the rightness or wrongness of an action based on its results or consequences. In simpler terms, a thing is right when it leads to positive consequences, and wrong when it leads to negative ones. There are two primary forms of consequentialism.

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism is a moral theory that explicitly determines rightness based on the outcome. Developed by figures like Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, it’s used to determine the most beneficial outcome for the greatest number of people. So, if an action brings happiness, wellbeing, or utility to the most people, then it’s considered moral.

Deontology

Deontology, on the other hand, places emphasis on duties and the principles that govern our actions. Immanuel Kant developed this idea, stipulating that the moral action is not about maximizing happiness, but about doing our ‘duty’ as per rules and principles. Regardless of the consequences, an action could be right if it adheres to certain principles or it’s our duty to adhere to certain principles or rules but it could also be wrong even if good results.

Virtue Theory

Virtue theory differs from consequentialism in that it focuses on the person, not the act or consequences. Instead of focusing on the single action, this moral theory places importance on the character of the person performing the action. Aristotle was a key proponent of this theory, emphasizing becoming an excellent human being (`eudaimonia’) as the ultimate goal. According to philosophers like Aristotle, virtues such as courage, wisdom, and temperance are what make us truly good people.

Non-Consequentialism

Non-consequentialism, as the name suggests, is the opposing view of consequentialism. Instead of weighing the outcomes, this moral theory judges actions independently of consequences. The duty or commitment of individuals to rights, duties, and justice governs non-consequentialism. Deontology, previously discussed, would fall under this category.

Kant’s Categorical Imperative

Immanuel Kant, a chief proponent of deontology, suggests a specific non-consequentialist approach, known as ‘categorical imperative.’ Kant’s theory argues that we must act only in ways that we would want to become a universal rule.

Care Theory

Care theory does not just answer questions of right and wrong but also addresses how humans interact with each other within society. Developed by philosophers like Joan Tronto and Eva Feder Kittay, care theory maintains that care is central to morality. The person who takes care of others, especially those vulnerable, is seen as moral. This theory demonstrates an aspect of moral thinking historically overlooked by the male-dominated field of philosophy.

In conclusion, moral philosophy provides a range of perspectives and frameworks to help us understand our moral principles better. Different theories ask us to weigh actions based on consequences, follow ‘duties’ irrespective of results, strive for personal character excellence, or value care and responsibility to others. As always, the real world is more complex than our neat theories, but understanding them helps us bring critical thinking to our everyday choices and behaviors. Ultimately, a nuanced understanding will enable us to make better, more informed choices about right and wrong.

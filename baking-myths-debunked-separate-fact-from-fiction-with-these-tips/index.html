Baking Myths Debunked: Separate Fact from Fiction with These Tips


Baking is a passion for many, a skill that brings both joy and mouth-watering results. But with every cooking passion comes a set of beliefs, myths, and misconceptions. The world of baking is ripe with these bits of folklore, leading some home bakers to believe that certain techniques or ingredients are vital despite a lack of evidence or logic. In today’s blog post, let’s debunk some common baking myths once and for all.

Myths Debunked

Myth 1: Enriched flour is better than plain flour

One popular baking myth is that enriched flour is a superior choice for baking than plain, all-purpose flour. Enriched flour, as the name suggests, is “enriched” with added nutrients like iron, niacin, thiamin, and riboflavin. However, from a baking standpoint, the difference is negligible. These added nutrients consist of small amounts that do not noticeably improve baking results. When it comes to baking, the primary difference between these two types of flour lies in their protein content or absent components like bran and germ, rather than their nutrient content.

Myth 2: Butter is superior to margarine

Many bakers believe that butter always reigns supreme over margarine in the world of baking. In some cases, this belief is true – butter might produce a flakier, richer crust in certain pastries or pie crusts. However, when it comes to cakes, quick breads, or cookies, butter substitutes may provide similar outcomes without compromising on the outcome texture. Ultimately, the best choice largely depends on personal preference and preference for taste, texture, and dietary restrictions. Margarine options that contain stabilizers may offer equal or better baking performance than unsalted butter. So enjoy your cake and eat it too—switching between the two in relevant baking scenarios.

Myth 3: The quality of ingredients matters the most in baking

The old adage, “the quality of your ingredients will dictate the quality of your baked goods,” fails to account for the importance of technique and skill. It’s true that high-quality ingredients can enhance flavors and textures, but the real magic happens in the oven – you must master the perfect blend of proportions, baking times, and temperatures. Additionally, equalizing effects occur in baking processes, like creaming butter and sugar or folding ingredients into a batter, that can result in a delightful treat despite the choice of ingredients.

Myth 4: Baking powder must be freshly purchased for each baking endeavor

Baking powder is a leavening agent that helps batters rise. Many bakers are under the impression that opened cans of baking powder lose their effectiveness over time. However, this is only true if the baking powder gets wet. When stored in a dry environment, an opened can of baking powder can keep for about 6 months to a year. If moisture comes into contact with the powder, it activates and needs to be replaced. If the baking powder is still dry, it will still function just as well in your baking endeavors.

Myth 5: You should use cold eggs straight from the fridge

Using cold eggs straight from the fridge might sound like an efficient tactic, but in reality, it makes the batter or dough chill quickly, causing the butter to harden and slowing down the batter or dough’s rising process. Room-temperature eggs, on the other hand, mix and emulsify smoothly with other ingredients, yielding a more consistent texture than using cold eggs. Start preparing your eggs ahead of time and make them just slightly warm before proceeding with your recipe to keep butter and other ingredients homogenized.

Myth 6: Flour should be sifted for every recipe

Sifting flour is generally an unnecessary step in baking. The majority of recipes call for a gentle whisking or lightly scooping and leveling the flour to assure the right amount of leavening and moisture control is achieved. Sifting flour can remove some of that magical component called gluten, which gets proteins, or gluten-forming ingredients wound up together, and this results in a more desirable texture in many baked goods. So stick to whisking or gently spooning your flour into your ingredients to achieve the best results.

Myth 7: Refrigerating cookie dough makes them taste better

One common cookie myth is that refrigerating the dough makes it taste better by removing excess moisture and enhancing the flavor. However, refrigerating cookie dough doesn’t actually alter the flavor – it simply changes the texture due to the dough’s melding of flavors with the cookies’ fat and sugar. If you want to refrigerate the dough, do so only for the purpose of shaping or portioning– otherwise, keep it at room temperature for optimal flavor.

Myth 8: Only use plastic to store baked goods

Baking involves different storage techniques, and my advice is to experiment with various options. Aluminum foil, plastic wrap, and wax or parchment paper can keep baked goods moist and fresh. However, when it comes to energy efficiency, using glass containers, breathable wrapped goods or squirting additional toppings, like chocolate ganache onto cakes, are much better options as they’ll be more practical and sustainable in the long run.

Myth 9: Chilling cookie dough prevents spread

Some amatuer bakers believe that chilling cookie dough prior to baking will prevent them from spreading. While chilling the dough can enhance flavor and control spreading when applithot cookies, it doesn’t necessarily reduce spread. Cookie dough expands in the oven due to the leavening agents, water content, and fat reacting with the sugars. This expansion combined with the dough’s ability to melt the butter, impact your cookie size– so chill it to desired thickness but don’t expect it to stop spreading.

Myth 10: Hand mixer is a must for baking

Hand mixers are convenient and can make life easier in some baking situations, but that doesn’t mean they are the only option. Many bakers have used simple whisk and wooden spoons to create impressive desserts. A hand mixer can aerate and blend ingredients much faster, but the manual process may help develop your understanding of the specific ingredient stages in a mix and prevent overbeating. So, it’s ultimately a decision based on personal preference and energy level.

Now that we’ve tackled these misconceptions head-on, it’s time to get baking confidently and creatively. Happy baking!

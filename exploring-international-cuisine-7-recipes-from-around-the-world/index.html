Exploring International Cuisine: 7 Recipes from Around the World


Food is a universal language. It speaks to our hearts, connects us with different cultures, and serves as a bridge to understanding traditions and histories that may be far removed from our own. Exploring international cuisine opens up a realm of flavors and textures that delight the senses and challenge our culinary habits. In this post, we’ll embark on a flavorful journey across seven countries, each represented by a recipe that captures the essence of its culture and history. These dishes aren’t just meals—they’re stories, generations-old methods, and vibrant reflections of the communities that cherish them.

1. Italy: Risotto Alla Milanese

When it comes to Italian cooking, pasta and pizza often steal the show. But the risotto—a creamy, luxurious rice dish—offers a lesser-known and equally cherished taste of Italy. Risotto alla Milanese, known for its rich saffron-infused flavor and golden hue, originated in Milan. This dish exemplifies the Italian philosophy of elevating simple ingredients into something extraordinary.

Ingredients:

  • Arborio rice (1 cup)
  • Chicken or vegetable stock (4 cups)
  • Saffron threads (a generous pinch)
  • Butter (2 tbsp)
  • Onion (finely chopped, 1 small)
  • Grated Parmesan cheese (½ cup)
  • White wine (½ cup)
  • Olive oil (1 tbsp)

Preparation:

  1. Warm the stock in a saucepan and add the saffron threads to infuse.
  2. Heat olive oil and butter in a large pan. Add the onion and sauté until translucent.
  3. Stir in the Arborio rice, coating it well. Pour in the white wine and let it evaporate.
  4. Gradually add one ladleful of the warm saffron stock at a time, stirring continuously until the rice absorbs the liquid. Repeat until the rice is cooked to a creamy texture.
  5. Fold in Parmesan cheese and a dollop of butter just before serving.

This dish, with its signature golden color, radiates warmth and comfort, perfectly encapsulating Italian elegance and simplicity.

2. Mexico: Tacos al Pastor

Tacos al Pastor is a quintessential dish of Mexican street food. The recipe’s origins trace back to Lebanese immigrants who brought shawarma-style cooking to Mexico, blending Middle Eastern spices with Mexican ingredients. The result? A mouthwatering combination of marinated pork, pineapple, and a medley of complementary toppings.

Ingredients:

  • Pork shoulder (2 lbs, thinly sliced)
  • Adobo marinade (a mix of guajillo chilies, vinegar, garlic, and spices)
  • Pineapple slices (fresh or grilled)
  • Corn tortillas
  • Cilantro, onions, lime wedges (for garnish)

Preparation:

  1. Marinate the pork slices in the adobo sauce for 6-12 hours.
  2. Skewer the pork and cook it vertically on a spit (or grill the slices if using a stovetop).
  3. Warm the corn tortillas. Fill them with the sliced, grilled pork and top with fresh pineapple, cilantro, diced onions, and a squeeze of lime.

The smoky, sweet notes of Tacos al Pastor perfectly encapsulate Mexico’s vibrant culinary traditions and the influence of global fusion.

3. India: Butter Chicken (Murgh Makhani)

India’s rich spice culture comes alive in Butter Chicken, a dish that originated in Delhi. This creamy, mildly spiced tomato-based curry has become a staple of Indian cuisine globally. The balance of spices, butter, and cream creates a dish that is both indulgent and comforting.

Ingredients:

  • Chicken (1 lb, boneless, cut into cubes)
  • Yogurt (½ cup, for marination)
  • Ginger-garlic paste (2 tbsp)
  • Garam masala, turmeric, chili powder (1 tsp each)
  • Tomatoes (pureed, 4 large)
  • Heavy cream (½ cup)
  • Butter (2 tbsp)

Preparation:

  1. Marinate the chicken with yogurt, ginger-garlic paste, and spices for 2-4 hours.
  2. Cook the chicken until slightly charred. Remove and set aside.
  3. In the same pan, sauté the tomato puree with butter and additional spices. Simmer to reduce.
  4. Add the cooked chicken and stir in the cream. Simmer for another 10-15 minutes.

Serve Butter Chicken with naan or basmati rice for a flavor-packed experience.

4. Japan: Okonomiyaki

Okonomiyaki, often referred to as a savory Japanese pancake, is a versatile dish loved for its customizable toppings. Originating in Osaka, it combines batter, cabbage, and a variety of ingredients based on personal preferences.

Ingredients:

  • All-purpose flour (1 cup)
  • Water (¾ cup)
  • Eggs (2)
  • Shredded cabbage (2 cups)
  • Toppings: sliced pork belly, mayonnaise, bonito flakes, green onions, and Okonomiyaki sauce.

Preparation:

  1. Combine flour, water, and eggs into a smooth batter. Fold in shredded cabbage.
  2. Heat a griddle or non-stick pan, then pour in a ladle of the batter.
  3. Add pork belly or other toppings onto the surface of the pancake as it cooks.
  4. Flip and cook until golden brown on both sides, ensuring the pork is done.
  5. Top with Okonomiyaki sauce, mayonnaise, bonito flakes, and chopped green onions.

Okonomiyaki is more than just comfort food; it’s culinary artistry that reflects Japan’s love for seasonal, fresh ingredients.

5. Greece: Spanakopita

This spinach pie wrapped in flaky phyllo pastry encapsulates the essence of Greek cooking: fresh produce, olive oil, and deeply rooted traditions. Spanakopita is a dish with ancient origins, highlighting ingredients that have sustained Greek culture for millennia.

Ingredients:

  • Phyllo dough
  • Fresh spinach (2 lbs, chopped)
  • Feta cheese (1 cup, crumbled)
  • Eggs (2, beaten)
  • Olive oil
  • Dill (chopped, 2 tbsp)

Preparation:

  1. Sauté spinach in olive oil until wilted. Cool, then mix with feta, eggs, and dill.
  2. Brush each phyllo layer with olive oil. Layer half the sheets in a baking dish.
  3. Spread the spinach-feta mixture evenly, then cover with the remaining layers of phyllo.
  4. Bake at 375°F for 30-40 minutes, until golden and crispy.

Spanakopita is a perfect example of how Greek cuisine transforms simple staples into something extraordinary.

6. Morocco: Lamb Tagine

Moroccan cuisine is renowned for its aromatic, spice-laden dishes, and Lamb Tagine is no exception. Cooked in a traditional earthenware pot, this slow-cooked stew epitomizes the melding of sweet and savory flavors.

Ingredients:

  • Lamb (2 lbs, cubed)
  • Onions (2, finely chopped)
  • Raisins (½ cup)
  • Almonds (toasted, ½ cup)
  • Olive oil
  • Ras el Hanout spice blend (1 tbsp)

Preparation:

  1. Heat oil in a tagine or heavy-bottomed pot. Brown the lamb pieces and set aside.
  2. Use the same pot to sauté onions and spices. Add lamb back in with water or stock to cover.
  3. Simmer gently for 1.5-2 hours, adding raisins and almonds toward the end.

Serve with fluffy couscous, and savor a tapestry of flavors that define Moroccan hospitality and tradition.

7. Thailand: Pad Thai

Pad Thai is one of Thailand’s most famous exports, embodying the perfect harmony of salty, sweet, sour, and spicy flavors. This noodle dish is loved worldwide for its intricate yet balanced flavor profile.

Ingredients:

  • Rice noodles (1 package)
  • Shrimp or chicken
  • Eggs (2, beaten)
  • Tamarind paste (2 tbsp)
  • Fish sauce, sugar, and lime juice (to taste)
  • Crushed peanuts and bean sprouts (for garnish)
  • Green onions (sliced)

Preparation:

  1. Cook rice noodles according to package instructions. Set aside.
  2. Heat oil in a wok, then cook shrimp or chicken until done. Remove and set aside.
  3. Scramble eggs in the pan, then return protein and noodles.
  4. Mix tamarind paste, fish sauce, sugar, and lime juice. Pour over the noodles and toss well.
  5. Garnish with crushed peanuts, bean sprouts, and green onions before serving.

Pad Thai is a testament to Thailand’s mastery of bold, vibrant flavors.

These seven recipes provide a taste of the world’s diverse cuisines and traditions. Whether it’s an Italian risotto, a spicy Indian butter chicken, or a savory Greek spanakopita, each dish carries the essence of its origin. Cooking these recipes is more than a culinary endeavor—it’s an exploration of culture and a celebration of the global tapestry of flavors. Enjoy the journey and savor the stories behind every bite!

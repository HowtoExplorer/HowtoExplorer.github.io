The Do's and Don'ts of Job Interviews


Job interviews can be nerve-wracking, but with the right preparation and mindset, you can increase your chances of landing your dream job. In this article, we'll delve into the do's and don'ts of job interviews, providing you with expert advice to help you ace your next interview. Do: Prepare Thoroughly Before stepping into an interview, it's essential to prepare thoroughly. Research the company, its mission, values, and products or services. Review the job description and requirements, and make a list of the key qualifications and skills the interviewer will likely ask about. Prepare examples of your past experiences that demonstrate your skills and achievements. Science has proven that preparation reduces anxiety and improves performance. According to a study published in the Journal of Applied Psychology, individuals who prepared thoroughly for a job interview were more likely to have a positive interview experience and receive a job offer. Don't: Be Late or Tardiness-Aware Punctuality is essential in a job interview. Showing up late or being tardiness-aware can create a negative impression, leading the interviewer to question your responsibility and reliability. Plan to arrive at least 10-15 minutes before the interview to account for traffic, parking, or other unexpected delays. If you're running late, inform the interviewer as soon as possible and apologize for the inconvenience. A study by the Harvard Business Review found that punctuality is a key factor in determining a candidate's employability. Employers view punctuality as a sign of respect for their time and a commitment to being responsible. Do: Dress Professionally Your attire can say a lot about your professional credentials. Dressing professionally shows that you respect the company culture and are serious about the opportunity. Avoid wearing ripped jeans, t-shirts, or overly casual shoes. Opt for business attire that is clean, ironed, and well-fitting. Research has shown that people who dress professionally are perceived as more competent and trustworthy than those who dress casually. A study published in the Journal of Social and Clinical Psychology found that participants perceived the professional-dressed individuals as more trustworthy and motivated than those who were casually dressed. Don't: Overstay Your Welcome While it's tempting to ask questions and chat with the interviewer, be mindful of the time. An interview is a two-way conversation, and while you want to learn more about the company and the role, you also need to make a good impression and show that you're considerate of their time. A survey by Glassdoor found that the average interview lasts around 40-60 minutes. If you notice the interviewer glancing at their watch or checking the time, it's a cue that the interview is winding down. Do: Ask Insightful Questions Asking questions is an essential part of an interview, and it shows that you're genuinely interested in the company and the role. Prepare a list of thoughtful and insightful questions that demonstrate your understanding of the company's mission, goals, and values. According to a study by the Harvard Business Review, employees who are asked thoughtful questions are more likely to be engaged and committed to their work. A study by Gallup found that employees who have one-on-one meetings with their managers are 26% more likely to be engaged in their work. Don't: Share Personal Stories Unless Relevant While sharing personal anecdotes can be an effective way to build rapport and connect with your interviewer, it's essential to keep the conversation relevant and on track. Unless a personal story is directly related to the job description or skills required, it's best to avoid sharing them. A study by the Journal of Occupational and Organizational Psychology found that employees who were asked to share personal stories as part of their job interview were more likely to experience anxiety and stress. On the other hand, employees who were asked to discuss their skills and qualifications were more likely to experience confidence and motivation. Do: Use Positive Body Language Positive body language can make a lasting impression on the interviewer. Maintain eye contact, sit up straight, and smile. Nod your head to show engagement and understanding. Research has shown that positive body language can affect our mood and perception of others. A study published in the Journal of Nonverbal Behavior found that individuals who used positive body language were perceived as more confident and competent by others. Don't: Use Jargon and Technical Terms Without Understanding While using technical terms and jargon may make you seem more knowledgeable, it's essential to use them correctly and with confidence. Avoid using terms you're unfamiliar with, as it may lead the interviewer to question your expertise. A study by the Journal of Applied Psychology found that employees who were asked complex and technical questions were more likely to experience anxiety and stress if they were unsure of the answer. On the other hand, employees who were asked simpler and more straightforward questions were more likely to experience confidence and optimism. Conclusion Job interviews can be intimidating, but by following the do's and don'ts outlined in this article, you can increase your chances of landing your dream job. Remember to prepare thoroughly, dress professionally, and ask insightful questions. Avoid being late, overstaying your welcome, and sharing personal stories unless relevant. By being mindful of positive body language and avoiding jargon and technical terms you're unfamiliar with, you'll be well on your way to acing your next interview.

How to Write for Television: Tips for Aspiring Screenwriters


Television writing can be one of the most rewarding and challenging careers for aspiring writers. With the ever-expanding landscape of TV shows and streaming platforms, there is no shortage of opportunities to create compelling stories. However, the art of writing for television is unique, requiring both creativity and technical know-how. Whether you’re just starting out or looking to polish your skills, this guide will walk you through key strategies to become a successful TV screenwriter.

Understanding the Television Landscape

Before you begin writing for television, it’s essential to understand the environment in which TV scripts are developed. The TV industry is different from writing for film or books, primarily due to its episodic nature. TV writers are not just creating one story—they are developing a series of stories that unfold over multiple episodes or seasons. This means that not only do you need to think about character arcs and plot progression, but you also need to ensure that each individual episode has its own narrative that contributes to the overarching story.

TV shows typically fall into two categories:

  1. Serialized Shows: These are shows where the story builds over multiple episodes or seasons. Popular examples include Breaking Bad, Game of Thrones, and Stranger Things. The storyline carries forward from one episode to the next, and character development is often long-term.

  2. Procedural Shows: These shows usually follow a case-of-the-week format, with each episode having a self-contained story. While there may be ongoing character development, the focus is often on solving a problem or mystery in every episode. Examples include Law & Order, NCIS, and Grey’s Anatomy.

Understanding the difference between these formats will help shape your writing approach and determine the best strategy for your ideas.

The Basics of TV Writing

Writing for television can be daunting, especially for those who are new to the medium. It’s important to start by learning the basic structure and format that TV scripts follow. Knowing the basics allows you to craft stories in a way that is both readable and marketable.

1. TV Script Format

TV scripts have a specific format that is important for readability and consistency. The format allows producers, directors, and other crew members to follow the story efficiently. A standard TV script is typically broken down as follows:

  • Scene Heading: This tells the location and time of day. It typically starts with INT. (Interior) or EXT. (Exterior), followed by the location and the time.
  • Action: This describes the visual action of the scene. It’s important to keep this concise and visual—TV scripts are meant to be seen, not read.
  • Character Name: The name of the character speaking appears above their dialogue.
  • Dialogue: The character’s spoken words follow their name.
  • Parentheticals: These brief instructions explain how a character delivers their line or what they are doing while speaking.
  • Transitions: These indicate how the scene moves from one to the next, often including cuts or fades.

It’s also crucial to remember that TV scripts are typically around 45-60 pages long for an hour-long show, and 22-30 pages for a half-hour show.

2. Act Structure

Television shows often follow a specific act structure, which can vary depending on the format. Hour-long dramas tend to have four acts, while half-hour comedies often have three acts. Each act serves to heighten conflict, provide character development, and set up cliffhangers or transitions that keep the audience engaged.

In serialized shows, the act breaks are where major storylines can develop or shift. In procedural shows, the act breaks often offer new twists or obstacles that advance the episode’s self-contained story.

3. Pacing

Pacing is critical when writing for television. Since most TV shows are made to fit a specific time slot, you must keep your writing concise and focused. A common mistake for new writers is overindulgence in unnecessary details, which can slow the narrative down. TV audiences expect things to move quickly, especially when watching a serialized series with multiple episodes.

For example, in procedural shows, the plot typically needs to be resolved by the end of the episode, while in serialized dramas, the episode should still have a clear structure with a setup, middle, and resolution—without prematurely resolving the broader story arc. The key to pacing is making sure each scene moves the plot forward, revealing character motivations, and developing the story in an engaging way.

Building Characters That Resonate

TV shows often rely on characters that evolve and grow over time. As a screenwriter, you must create characters that viewers will care about and relate to. However, creating compelling characters in TV writing is not just about giving them interesting traits or backstories—it’s about understanding their emotional journey and how they fit into the larger story.

1. Character Arcs

Character arcs are the emotional and psychological transformation that characters undergo throughout the series. In serialized television, a character’s journey unfolds over the course of multiple episodes or seasons, allowing them to change and grow. In episodic series, arcs can be shorter but still need to feel satisfying.

When developing a character, ask yourself questions like:

  • What does the character want, and why?
  • What internal conflicts does the character face?
  • How will they change over time? What will make them grow or regress?

Consider characters like Walter White from Breaking Bad, who transforms from a mild-mannered teacher to a ruthless drug kingpin. His gradual evolution is a key aspect of the show’s storytelling.

2. Dialogue and Voice

The way characters speak is just as important as what they say. Dialogue is one of the most powerful tools you have as a TV writer, and it must sound natural and true to the character.

Think about how people talk in real life—do they use slang? Are they formal or informal in their speech? Do they have specific quirks in the way they express themselves? Make sure that each character’s voice feels distinct. This adds layers to the story, making it more dynamic and engaging.

3. Relationships

A great TV show often revolves around the relationships between characters. These relationships should evolve over time, whether they are friendships, romantic entanglements, or rivalries. Pay attention to how these relationships grow and change, as they can be a major driver of your show’s drama or comedy.

In The Office, for example, the relationship between Jim and Pam is a central element of the show, evolving from playful flirtation to a committed marriage. On the other hand, the dynamic between Michael Scott and his employees plays a significant role in the series’ humor and heart.

Crafting a Compelling Plot

While characters are at the heart of any good TV show, the plot is the engine that drives the story forward. A strong plot engages the audience, keeps them invested, and provides a foundation for character growth. Writing a TV plot involves creating conflicts, building tension, and satisfying the audience’s expectations.

1. Episode Structure

Each episode should have its own arc that complements the overall series narrative. This means that while the episode needs to contribute to long-term character growth, it should also have a complete and satisfying storyline by the end. This can be tricky, especially for serialized shows, where there are many overlapping storylines. Strive to create episodes that can be enjoyed on their own, but also contribute to the larger world you’ve built.

For example, in The Simpsons, each episode is often a standalone story, but the characters remain consistent, and some long-term developments are weaved into the show.

2. Conflict and Tension

Conflict is the backbone of any TV plot. Whether it’s internal conflict within a character or external conflict between characters, drama cannot exist without it. Tension is built through these conflicts, and keeping the audience engaged means raising the stakes and creating moments of suspense.

Serialized shows, like The Crown or Fargo, often have long-term conflicts that continue to build throughout the season, leading to big moments of resolution. Procedural shows, like Castle or Bones, often build tension with every case, while also allowing room for personal conflicts to emerge in the background.

3. Plot Twists and Cliffhangers

One of the best ways to keep viewers hooked is by incorporating plot twists or cliffhangers. TV shows, particularly serialized ones, thrive on shocking moments that make viewers eager for the next episode. But don’t make twists just for the sake of surprises—make sure they’re earned and logically fit into the overall narrative.

Think about The Good Place, where a major twist in the first season completely changes the direction of the show. This kind of twist keeps the audience guessing and engaged, ensuring that they’ll return to see what happens next.

Practical Tips for Aspiring TV Writers

  • Write, Write, Write: Like any other skill, writing for television improves the more you practice. Write consistently, experiment with different genres and formats, and always push your creativity.
  • Watch TV Critically: As you watch your favorite shows, pay attention to how they’re structured. Analyze character arcs, plot development, and how the dialogue feels. Understand what works and what doesn’t.
  • Find Your Voice: Every writer has a unique perspective, and it’s important to find yours. Don’t try to mimic other writers—find your own style and narrative voice.
  • Network: TV writing is a collaborative industry. Attend writing workshops, networking events, and screenwriting seminars. Meet other writers, directors, and producers, and learn from their experiences.
  • Read TV Scripts: Study professionally written TV scripts to understand format, pacing, and structure. Many scripts are available online for free, and reading them will give you insight into how TV shows are created.

Conclusion

Writing for television requires both passion and a deep understanding of the medium’s unique storytelling demands. By focusing on strong character development, compelling plotlines, and mastering TV script structure, aspiring screenwriters can increase their chances of success. Keep honing your craft, watch critically, and always be ready to adapt to the ever-changing world of television. Whether you’re writing a drama, a comedy, or a procedural, your ability to craft unforgettable characters and stories will set you apart in this exciting field.

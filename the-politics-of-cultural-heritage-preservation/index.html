The Politics of Cultural Heritage Preservation


As human beings, we all share a common history and heritage that defines our collective identity. Cultural heritage - tangible or intangible, natural or man-made - forms an essential part of our identity. However, this rich legacy is under persistent threat from various developmental projects, time, and climate change. Cultural heritage preservation then becomes a significant political matter involving complex societal, moral, and environmental considerations.

Let’s begin with one of the most explicit examples: the Great Wall of China. Built over two millennia ago, the wall is a testament to ancient Chinese engineering prowess, architectural brilliance, and cultural ingenuity. As a UNESCO World Heritage Site, it’s protected from commercial use, tourist damage, and even climate change-induced erosion. However, its preservation implicates the Chinese government in substantial policy decisions. These decisions involve possible compromises with the wall’s integrity (for instance, in tourism development) and public perceptions of Chinese cultural heritage. Thus, the politics of preservation come into play.

These politics are further complicated by the tug-of-war between the interests of different stakeholders. Take the case of Australia’s Uluru-Kata Tjuta National Park, sacred land for the Anangu people. Bordered by leased land for a cattle station, mining, and a tourist resort, Uluru has faced numerous development pressures. The Anangu people’s ancestral rights conflict with commercial interests, sparking debates on the park’s management and resource allocation. This, in turn, affects the quality of cultural heritage preservation.

Cultural heritage sites also identify political divisions and shift our focus towards historical injustices, as seen in the case of African heritage. The Speke & Grant Memorial in Zanzibar, Tanzania, commemorates the British explorers Speke and Grant, who started the process of exposing Africa’s cultural heritage to the world. However, some argue that this commemoration contributes to a distorted Eurocentric narrative. As it stands today, it tells a story of European exploration in Africa and overlooks African agency and experiences. As such, the political aspect of this preservation highlights questions of historical representation and inclusion.

In all these cases, the preservation of cultural heritage implies negotiating a balance between honoring the past and facilitating contemporary needs. This inevitable political confrontation reflects the essential connecting link that cultural heritage is – between past, present, and future generations. The key to navigating these complexities is understanding that cultural heritage preservation transcends the domain of conservationists and historians.

Politics in cultural heritage preservation isn’t only about heritage protection but also regarding its social and economical implications. A museum director may face the complex task of showcasing their collection while ensuring its relevance in contemporary society and managing its budget accordingly.

Cultural heritage preservation reflects a society’s values and aspirations. It shapes our identity, influences our perception of the world, and evokes an emotional connection to our ancestors. And as such, it deserves to be nurtured and protected, preserving our shared humanity for generations to come. By acknowledging the political nature of cultural heritage preservation and understanding its nuanced implications, we can ensure that our shared legacy remains accessible, relevant, and respected worldwide.

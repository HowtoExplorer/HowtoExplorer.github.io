From Me to We: The Evolution of Morality and Why It Matters


Humans have always been a social species, continuously evolving and adapting to the world around us. As we developed and formed intricate societies, our moral understanding began to shift from a narrow focus on individual survival and wellbeing to a more holistic perception that encompasses the needs and welfare of everyone around us. This transformation from the “Me” mindset to a collaborative “We” mentality has had a profound influence on shaping the way we interact with one another and navigate through life. In this article, we will explore the fascinating journey of human morality and the ongoing quest for moral progress as we strive to create a kinder, more equitable world.

The Emergence of Morality

It was the Swiss biologist and naturalist, Jean Henri Fabre, who said, “Man measures everything by his individual sensations, but he need feel no shame in doing so, for there is no other method.” In the early stages of human existence, morality was primarily determined by instinct and personal experiences, as there were no formal systems to govern correct behavior. In a world where secure food sources were scarce and survival was a daily struggle, it made sense that individuals focused on promoting their self-interest alone.

However, as time went on and societies became more complex, the need for cooperative behavior among individuals became evident. This emergence of moral reasoning began with the development of empathy and the ability to understand that others felt emotions too. Anthropologists have found evidence that even prehistoric art hinted at empathetic connections between ancient humans. It was the ability to forge bonds and work together that helped ensure survival, even in the face of adversity.

The Evolution of Morals

Our evolving understanding of morality can be viewed through the lens of the tripartite division of morality proposed by Jonathan Haidt – Care/Harm, Fairness/Reciprocity, Loyalty/A authority, and Sanctity/Cleanliness. This framework outlines how human morality is rooted in five foundational values that have shaped our moral sense over time.

1. Care/Harm

At the heart of this value is the innate drive to alleviate the suffering of others and prevent harm. As our primitive consciousness expanded, we began recognizing that the pain of others paralleled our own, and we set out to create programs that protected individuals from harm.

2. Fairness/Reciprocity

The concept of fairness became ingrained in our moral code as communities worked together to share resources, divide tasks evenly, and safeguard the rights of each member of society. This led to the development of property laws, labor policies, and humanitarian movements.

3. Loyalty/Authority

Humans are inherently social creatures and have a need for belonging. To maintain order and unity in societies, we formed hierarchies, established formal leadership structures, and adhered to a set of rules that kept groups cohesive.

4. Sanctity/Cleanliness

Sacred beliefs guided the virtues and boundaries ingrained in ancient communities. This moral foundation strictly forbade immoral acts that violated these beliefs, thereby promoting a society that valued integrity and moral values.

While these pillars continue to influence our moral compass today, their weight differs across cultures and societies. This highlights the fact that morality is not uniform but is a flexible, adaptive system that evolves to address the specific needs of different communities and their development.

The Moral Progress Paradox

Evolving moral standards indicate that as societies develop, our moral behavior and our perceptions of what is right and wrong change, but this progress is not always consistent. The paradox of moral progress lies in our capacity to advance and yet repeatedly revert or contend with new issues that challenge our moral foundation.

For example, although we have made tremendous progress in gender equality, we continue to experience ongoing battles for equal rights, justice, and recognition for marginalized groups, like the ongoing Black Lives Matter movement.

Similarly, advancements in technology and scientific methods have exposed humanity to a myriad of global issues that were previously unconsidered, such as climate change, and require a sophisticated moral deliberations about our relationship with the environment.

Why Morality Matters

Today, our moral journey has transformed from self-preservation to a broader understanding of fostering kindness, empathy, and fairness in our communities. This evolution demands that we develop a multifaceted moral compass that enables us to identify and address increasingly complex global issues.

As Mahatma Gandhi stated, “Be the change that you wish to see in the world.” We are called upon to be active participants in our moral evolution and to shape a future where we recalibrate our values and moral principles to create a significantly more compassionate and equitable society.

With our efforts in understanding and embracing the evolving nature of morality, we are well-equipped to tackle the challenges that lie ahead and move humanity closer to achieving the revised vision of our collective morality - prioritizing the welfare of all.

Conclusion

In essence, the shift from an individualistic “Me” mindset to a collaborative “We” perspective marks a crucial moment in human history. Our understanding, perception, and application of morality have dramatically progressed, raising essential questions about what it means to live a meaningful and moral life. The ongoing evolution of our moral standards presents us with both an opportunity and a responsibility – to engage in discourse, empathize, and work diligently to create a world grounded in the timeless values of compassion, fairness, and understanding.

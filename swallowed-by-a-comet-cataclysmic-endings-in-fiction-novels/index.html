Swallowed by a Comet: Cataclysmic Endings in Fiction Novels


The final moments of a book can make or break its overall impact. A well-conceived ending remains memorable and satisfying, allowing readers a sense of closure and fulfillment. In the hands of skilled authors, cataclysmic endings can leave an indelible mark on our collective cultural consciousness - such as in the case of a comet swallowing an entire planet.

This blog post will take us on a journey through the cataclysmic endings in fiction novels, with a particular focus on the impending doomsday scenario of being “swallowed by a comet.” From H. G. Wells’ The First Men in the Moon to Arthur C. Clarke’s Childhood’s End – we’ll explore the significance and significance of these endings, and why they captivate readers.

The First Men in the Moon by H. G. Wells

Published in 1901, H. G. Wells’ The First Men in the Moon is a tale that revolves around two men who land on the moon. Their lunar mission transforms into a fight for survival as their shelter is destroyed by a “meteor swarm.” Among these harmful meteors is a pulsating violet sphere. Though Wells does not delve into the destruction of Earth, the mere mention of a comet “swallowing” his own world is enough to evoke cataclysmic fears.

Childhood’s End by Arthur C. Clarke

Fifty-six years later, in 1953, Arthur C. Clarke penned Childhood’s End, a novel that centers the Overlanders’ lives during the Overlords’ rule on Earth. This sci-fi story takes an extraordinary and unexpected twist when a gigantic “Mother Comet” lands on Earth, ushering in the Oversoul—a higher consciousness that pervades humanity. Earth is effectively devoured in a cosmic sense, as the comet serves to dispel our fear of being engulfed by a celestial body.

Lucifer’s Hammer by Larry Niven and Jerry Pournelle

Sticking with the cosmic theme, Lucifer’s Hammer (1977) by Larry Niven and Jerry Pournelle, depicts the harrowing tale of Earth being struck by a nuclear-tail-end cometary fragment, known as “Lucifer’s Hammer.” Destructive plagues, mass fatalities, and societal collapse follow, prompting survivors to band together in the face of such devastation. Instead of being swallowed whole like in earlier examples, an impending global cataclysm seals the characters’ ultimate fate.

The Advocates of Algiers by Frederick Pohl

Lastly, we arrive at The Advocates of Algiers (1964) by Frederick Pohl, where a comet with the Earth’s destruction seemingly imminent consumes the planet. The ending left readers with a sudden jolt as the characters come to grips with their impending doom. Though short, this section leaves a profound impression on those familiar with Pohl’s work—it’s abruptness effectively creates a sense of desolation, fear, and uncertainty.

While these examples illustrate different perspectives on the massive destruction and obliteration of entire planets, comets often serve as harbingers of disaster and devastation. These cataclysmic endings force the characters and readers facing this scenario to grapple with finite existence, the fragility of life, and the true nature of the cosmos—themes echoed in modern and classic science fiction alike.

Swallowed by a Comet: Cataclysmic Endings in Fiction Novels, From Classic to Contemporary Ending a novel on a cataclysmic note can stimulate our collective curiosity and remind us of our place within a broader, indomitable universe. As these authors demonstrate, these fateful endings speak to the fears and worries which resonate within the human psyche—the desire to explore the unknown, coupled with an inherent sense of vulnerability. As we continue to forge our connections between space and Earth, these unforgettable endings in fiction remind us of the importance of exploring science fiction through any apocalypse the cosmos may have up its sleeve.

By exploring the aforementioned novels, we are not only left with unforgettable memories but also a renewed appreciation for the power of science fiction in our literary landscape. In some cases, as seen through various interpretations, the earth is not so much destroyed as consumed whole. This makes for an ending that leaves a lasting, eerie mark, reminding us of our ephemeral existence.

To conclude with an exhilarating twist, should we one day face the imminent wrath of a celestial body such as a comet, would we be swallowed whole or be left to defend and adapt against the seemingly inevitable odds stacked against us?

Beneath the Surface: Gabbing Gurus Reveal Communication Myths


In today’s interconnected world, communication can make or break relationships, professional success, and personal satisfaction. Expanding beyond the lover, the therapist, and the mentor, everyone is a communication guru in their daily lives. Whether it’s an art or a skill, it’s important for all of us to dissect the common communication myths that pervade society.

Myth 1: “Right” and “Wrong” Communication

One of the most pervasive myths is that there is such a thing as “right” and “wrong” communication. Everyone has their own communication style that works for them. What one person considers effective communication might be seen as aggressive or passive by another. This is especially true in the various cultural contexts. Instead of subscribing to the idea of “right” and “wrong” communication, we should strive to understand and adapt to different communication styles.

Moreover, good communication is not just about the words we choose or our body language – it’s about “active listening.” It’s about paying attention to the nonverbal cues like facial expressions and tone of voice, rather than just focusing on the message being conveyed.

Myth 2: “Communication is 50% Listening and 50% Speaking”

The 50-50 myth about communication skills suggests that communication consists of equal parts talking and listening. But is this really true? According to communication experts like Dr. Julian Treasure, “Most people speak at a volume of 60 to 80 decibels, but listen at only 20 to 30.” Hence, it seems the ratio of listening to speaking should be higher.

This goes beyond the simple act of hearing - listening requires focus and attention. It is often said that the best conversations require eighty percent listening. When we redirect our energy to listening attentively, we can gain more insights, foster deeper connections, and also, forge richer communication experiences.

Myth 3: “We have to Agree to Disagree”

Often, the phrase “agree to disagree” circulates in conversation. It implies that the key to dispute resolution is to agree to part ways, without exploring each other’s perspectives. Communication is not just the exchange of information or opinions – it’s about conveying empathy and understanding. When we simplify disagreement to merely a ‘disagreement’, we lose the opportunity for deep communication.

Moreover, life is too short to hold onto grudges, but it’s also too precious not to gain new perspectives. So instead of merely agreeing to disagree, we need to delve deeper into these differences and explore the reasoning behind them. This can lead to richer communication, mutual understanding, and sometimes, even empathy.

Myth 4: “Texting is Communication”

Do emojis in messages convey understanding? The rise of digital communication poses new challenges to effective communication. Texting, messaging, and other forms of digital communication have changed the way we communicate. Texts can be cluttered, ambiguous, and lacking the subtleties of face-to-face interactions. Conveying emotions, tone, and intonations through texts can be extremely challenging, so we might seem less connected overall. Therefore, texting and other forms of digital communication are a part of the larger, more complex communication. Communicating through these channels requires extra caution and consideration.

Remember, while they are convenient and widely used, digital forms of communication can’t substitute for the richness of true conversation that includes facial expressions, body language, and tones.

Myth 5: “Direct Communication Saves Time”

Although we may believe that direct communication is efficient, it neglects the importance of empathy, emotions, and understanding in communication. Communication is not always about getting to the point; it’s about nurturing relationships, cultivating a support system, understanding another person’s feelings, and fostering empathy.

Hence, taking our time to communicate directly through hard-headed conversations can sometimes create emotional distances. The true essence of communication - creating connection and sharing emotions, becomes lost in the exchange.

Final Thoughts

As communication gurus, we must go “beyond the surface” and challenge our underlying beliefs about communication. The key to effective communication lies in exploring our biases, adaptation, empathy, and mutual understanding. Let’s exchange “Agree to Disagree” for a heartfelt conversation - for that is what makes communication more meaningful. Aren’t we catering to the deepest needs of our mind: Expressing and understanding the plurality of human experiences?

Breaking Bread: Why Conversation and Moral Empathy are the Keys to Change


The moment we sit at the table – whether it be with family, friends, a significant other, or even a stranger – something magical begins to happen. As we share breaking bread, we also create an opportunity to engage in deep, meaningful conversation. These dialogues open up doors to developing moral empathy, the engine for driving positive change in the world.

Often, people equate breaking bread solely with the act of eating together. Of course, sharing a meal doesn’t just nourish our bodies; it can also bring people together through shared experiences and bring comfort in uncertain times. But, in this piece, I want to explore what happens when the conversation takes center stage. It is the silent bard we feed, and the key to unlocking the possibilities of moral empathy.

Imagine this scenario: you and your loved ones are gathered around the dining table, swapping stories over a delicious homemade meal, laughter filling the air, and memorable moments being etched into everyone’s memory. Now, consider another scenario: you are in a casual cafe setting, engaging in deep conversation with a complete stranger while sharing a cup of coffee. The bond that forms in these different spaces is rooted in the art of conversation, and ultimately, the crucial role it plays in building our capacity for moral empathy.

As humans, we have an innate desire to connect with others, to truly feel seen and understood. Conversation enables us to create an emotional bridge between the experiences and thoughts we have in common, and the ones that are foreign or perplexing to us. This is the innovation of moral empathy – the ability to step into someone else’s shoes, see their perspectives, and understand their feelings, even if we disagree with them.

Moral empathy is essential to building a more humane and inclusive world. Change begins when individuals are moved by the experiences of others, whether near or far. We can only truly understand a situation if we are able to communicate openly with one another. So, it’s when conversations deepen and people listen attentively that moral empathy starts to flourish.

Think of being a kid in school when you first started learning about the contributions of various civilizations to our collective human history. Before that, their existence was theoretical and abstract. However, when you started engaging in discussions about their legacies, sharing your thoughts with fellow classmates and exchanging views with your teacher, your understanding of their history became much more solid and real.

The ability to connect over our shared humanity becomes even more apparent when we break bread with people outside our proverbial comfort zone. Our differences no longer become barriers but rather opportunities to learn, grow, and practice empathy towards others. These conversations allow us to experience the benefits of biodiversity on a microcosmic level – learning to appreciate and thrive in an environment enriched with a variety of diverse voices and perspectives.

In order to promote and foster moral empathy, we must seek opportunities to engage in constructive debates, listen actively to others, and respect the perspectives of those around us. Conversation should be a multi-layered dance of dialogue and silence, allowing ample time for reflection and consideration.

An essential aspect of these conversations is showing that even complex issues have the ability to be investigated, understood, and discussed openly without escalating to accusations, blame, or defensiveness. Free from prejudice, our words become instruments of unity. By deliberately choosing vulnerability and humility, we allow for emotional and empathic connections to crystalize.

Recently, a somewhat controversial movie, “Hacksaw Ridge,” was released. The film tells the story of Desmond Doss, an American pacifist who served as a combat medic in the United States Army during World War II. Though raised by a religiously conservative father who believed in the sanctity of life, Doss eventually decided to put his own beliefs into action when he refused to carry a gun and chose to save lives in turmoil, even amidst enemy fire.

A film like this is a perfect example of breaking bread and conversation fostering moral empathy. The narrative unfolds amidst intense and vivid scenes of war, providing an opportunity for an audience to empathize with the experiences of soldiers, regardless of their stand on war or the acceptance of violence.

In conclusion, breaking bread and engaging in meaningful conversations lay the groundwork for moral empathy to grow and flourish. They provide the context for individuals to reach a deeper understanding of one another, fueling change on both an individual and societal level.

We need not limit ourselves to formal settings to invoke these transformative experiences; they can happen anytime, anywhere. Whether it’s a casual chat over a cup of morning coffee or a heart-to-heart conversation about social issues, by cultivating an atmosphere of openness, authenticity and deep listening, we can enhance and improve the human condition together.

And as we embark on this empathetic journey together, I invite you all to consider the following questions: How can you engage in conversations that require active listening and the acknowledgment of others’ perspectives to promote moral empathy? Can you lead by example and encourage those around you to freely exchange their ideas and experiences? By breaking bread and delving deep into conversation with humility, integrity, and open minds, we pave the way for progress that is both active and empathic. The decisions we make today will echo through generations to come, and it all starts with a simple conversation. We just have to have the will to participate, to commit, and to make a societal shift that is inclusive, collaborative, and bilingual in empathy.

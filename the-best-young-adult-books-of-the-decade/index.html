The Best Young Adult Books of the Decade


Young adult literature has undergone a remarkable transformation in the past decade. What was once a genre defined by coming-of-age stories and high school dramas has blossomed into a captivating realm of creativity, diversity, and meaningful narratives that capture the imagination of readers worldwide. The past ten years have seen the rise of books that not only entertain but also challenge societal norms, ignite necessary conversations, and celebrate voices that were often overlooked in previous eras of literature. In this list, we’ll delve into some of the standout YA books that have defined this decade, exploring how they’ve made an impact and why they remain unforgettable.

A New Era of Representation: Diverse Voices Redefine YA Literature

The 2010s and early 2020s heralded a movement toward authenticity and inclusivity in storytelling. Authors from diverse cultural, racial, and sexual backgrounds rose to prominence, enriching the literary landscape with perspectives that once felt absent from the mainstream.

One shining example is “The Hate U Give” by Angie Thomas. This groundbreaking novel tells the story of Starr Carter, a Black teenager who witnesses the devastating consequences of police brutality when her childhood friend is shot by an officer. Through Starr’s eyes, readers are given a poignant look into systemic racism, the challenges of code-switching, and the resilience of a community demanding change. By blending raw truths with heartfelt moments of hope, Thomas set a benchmark for modern YA literature and inspired a wave of books addressing critical social issues.

Then there’s Elizabeth Acevedo’s “The Poet X”, a novel written in verse that follows Xiomara Batista, a young Dominican-American girl navigating her tumultuous relationship with religion, family expectations, and her burgeoning identity as a poet. The lyrical format immerses readers in Xiomara’s fiery passion and vulnerability, making it a standout work that has resonated deeply with teens and adults alike. Acevedo’s authentic voice has paved the way for more YA novels that explore the intersection of identity, art, and culture.

These stories highlight the power of representation and the importance of young readers seeing themselves in fiction. They have carved out paths for authors seeking to authentically portray the richness of their heritage and experiences.

Fantasy Worlds That Feel All Too Real

YA fantasy has long been a pillar in the genre, but the past decade saw a surge of creative world-building that combined gripping mythical adventures with deeply human themes.

One of the game-changers was “Six of Crows” by Leigh Bardugo. Set in the dark, dangerous world of the Grishaverse, this gripping heist story follows six outcasts brought together to pull off the impossible. With morally complex characters, intricate plotting, and dazzling world-building, Bardugo’s novel enchanted readers across the spectrum. Its exploration of themes like loyalty, trauma, and personal redemption ensures it resonates beyond its fantastical elements.

Likewise, Tomi Adeyemi’s “Children of Blood and Bone” brought West African mythology to the forefront with its vibrant, magical tale of rebellion and resilience. The book captures the struggle against oppression while exploring individual and collective power. Adeyemi’s work reinvigorated the fantasy genre, providing a culturally rich setting that captivated audiences.

Fantasy has always offered an escape, but these novels show how it can also be a lens through which readers explore real-world issues. They’ve set a high bar for authors to craft stories that balance escapism and relevance.

Tackling Mental Health and Emotional Complexity

The past decade has also witnessed a marked shift toward exploring mental health topics with nuance and empathy, reflecting the challenges many young people experience but rarely discuss openly. These books foster understanding and create space for conversations about struggles that often go unseen.

One of the standout contributions is “We Are Okay” by Nina LaCour. This quiet yet powerful story examines grief, loneliness, and healing as Marin, a college freshman, grapples with the aftermath of a traumatic event. LaCour’s tender approach to depicting emotional pain resonates deeply, shining a light on the importance of allowing oneself to feel and heal.

Similarly, “Turtles All the Way Down” by John Green takes readers directly into the mind of Aza Holmes, a teenager with obsessive-compulsive disorder. Green’s unflinchingly honest portrayal of Aza’s intrusive thoughts and anxiety gave representation to a mental health condition that is often misunderstood. Unlike his earlier hits, this novel stands out for its raw portrayal of mental illness and its impact not only on the individual but also on their relationships.

Books like these prove that young adult literature can address sensitive topics with care and authenticity, offering readers understanding and a sense of belonging.

Love Stories That Break Barriers

Romance has always been a staple of YA fiction, but the 2010s and beyond have shifted toward romantic narratives that celebrate inclusivity and defy traditional tropes.

A prime example is “Simon vs. the Homo Sapiens Agenda” by Becky Albertalli. This heartwarming story follows Simon, a gay teenager navigating high school, identity, and an anonymous online romance. Its charm lies in its relatability—Simon’s struggles and triumphs feel universal, even as it addresses the specific experiences of LGBTQ+ teens.

A companion to this is Adam Silvera’s “They Both Die at the End”, a bittersweet tale that combines romance, mortality, and the fleeting beauty of life. Though deeply emotional, Silvera’s unique approach offers a celebration of love and connection, even in the face of inevitable loss.

These stories remind us that love exists in myriad forms and showcases its power to change lives, making them enduring favorites for YA readers.

Dystopian Themes That Still Resonate

No discussion of the past decade in YA would be complete without revisiting dystopian literature. While its popularity spiked in the early 2010s, the themes explored in dystopian novels remain ever-relevant.

Books like “The Hunger Games” series by Suzanne Collins may have laid the groundwork, but newer entries like Tahereh Mafi’s “Shatter Me” series brought a fresh lens to the genre. Mafi’s protagonist, Juliette, deals with internal battles as much as external ones, holding up a mirror to the complexities of control, freedom, and individual agency. The lush prose and evolving character dynamics give this series an edge that has stood the test of time.

Another dystopian gem is Veronica Roth’s “Divergent” trilogy, which questions societal divisions and the labels that confine us. The story of Tris Prior’s journey of self-discovery and courage struck a chord with many readers navigating their own identities in an increasingly divided world.

These dystopian tales resonate because, at their core, they challenge readers to question authority, imagine alternate futures, and discover their strength.

The Decade’s Legacy in YA Literature

The past decade has propelled YA literature to new heights, offering stories that are thought-provoking, inclusive, imaginative, and deeply empathetic. The novels that defined this era go far beyond the limits of their genre, leaving readers forever changed by their words and messages.

What sets this era apart is the way these stories connect to readers so deeply, no matter their age. By holding up a mirror to the world, celebrating diversity, tackling tough truths, and opening doors to fantastical realms, these books have solidified their place as some of the best young adult titles of the decade.

As we move forward, these novels continue to be a wellspring of inspiration for both writers and readers, ensuring that YA literature remains not just a genre but a movement that transforms the hearts and minds of all who engage with it.

Political Violence and Conflict Resolution


As the waters of political discord swell, conflict resolution has emerged as an essential hammer for shaping a peaceful society. In this unsettling era of escalating political violence, this guide is meant to illuminate the dark corners of this topic and illuminate the way forward.

Political violence, while taking many forms and causing significant stress globally, can be broadly classified as Passive and Active. Passive political violence is mainly non-violent civil resistance, including boycotts, protests, and strikes. Conversely, Active violence can run the gamut from mob violence, riots, and arbitrary state violence to organized crime and war.

Navigating the subtleties of conflict requires a solid understanding of the factors that drive such unrest, including history, society, religion, and politics. A “one-size-fits-all” approach is rarely effective as every conflict is unique, with its causes, complexity, and context.

Conflict resolution, therefore, begins with an analysis of these intricacies to create tailor-made solutions.

One effective strategy for conflict resolution lies in utilizing the transformative power of dialogue. Dialogue here goes beyond mere negotiation, fostering a sense of trust and understanding that is crucial to peace-building. Dialogue has been appreciated the world over owing to its capacity to help conflicting parties recognize each other’s narratives fostering a symphony of understanding in place of discord.

Dialogues, however, need potent mediators who are patient, well-versed with the culture, traditions, and histories, and above all, lack any prejudice. Mediators act as facilitators, driving parties towards understanding, addressing underlying issues, and crafting agreed-upon ways forward.

Such a nondogmatic, open-hearted approach strengthens the hands of peacemakers in conflict-ridden areas.

Another significant tool in the toolbox of conflict resolution is the concept of ’truth-telling.’ South African’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission is a profound example of how truth-telling can facilitate healing and reconciliation after extensive political violence.

Institute of Restorative Justice at the University of the Western Cape proved that heartfelt, sincere attempts at ending the ‘culture of silence’ characteristic of the aftermath of political violence made a significant difference.

Dealing with past wrongs, learning and acknowledging the truth about each other’s actions, and seeking forgiveness are substantial steps toward therapeutic reconciliation.

In contrast, retributive justice often degenerates into another problematic context, further poisoning the atmosphere of the conflict.

Lastly, education represents a potentiating force in tackling political violence, creating informed citizens, and preventing an injustice senselessly repeating itself. This education encompasses individuals and institutions alike, fostering a sense of empathy towards —and the understanding of —the hardships of various groups, preventing conflict before it starts.

Education facilitates mutual respect, trust, and secure intergroup relationships, challenging hatred, stereotyping, and ignorance.

As our world continues to whirl in a turbulent dance with an ever-evolving array of conflicts and political unrest, sticking with a potent mix of dialogue, restorative justice, and truth-telling, coupled with widespread education, might offer the direction towards a more peaceful future. However, the success of these instruments will largely depend on the receptiveness of the parties in conflict and on the ground realities. Each conflict’s resolution is different; therein lies our challenge and our hope.

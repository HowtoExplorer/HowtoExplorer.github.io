Mathematics and Music: The Hidden Music in Mathematics and Vice Versa


Have you ever wondered what music has to do with mathematics? Surprisingly, the two share a closer relationship than one might initially think. The fascinating synergy between mathematics and music has spurred countless investigations, drawing links between their respective properties and generating remarkable insights for both fields. This essay aims to provide a nontechnical introduction to this captivating subject and reveal the hidden music in mathematics and vice versa.

One might question how music and mathematics connect. Undeniably, music evokes emotions and captivates listeners, while mathematics presents an abstract subject often dismissed as dry and frivolous. However, music and mathematics share much more than one might expect. The basis of any musical scale stems from the consecutive whole numbers related to frequency and time, a testament to the mathematical foundations of music.

The seventh note of the diatonic scale (A) is 72.98 Herz, the twelfth note (G) is 82.41 Hz, while the seventeenth note (F-sharp) is 93.62 Hz. These relationships indicate a profound link between mathematics and music. The arithmetic progression of these notes is an outcome of human intuition of consonance and dissonance, linking the musical sense to the mathematical idea of series.

Fibonacci Sequence and Music

It is beyond the scope of this article to delve into detail on every possible connection between mathematics and music, but we can explore one captivating example – the Fibonacci Sequence – which has been incorporated in musical compositions.

The Fibonacci Sequence was introduced in India during the Griffith period around 5th century AD and spread throughout Europe during the 13th century. This sequence is a series of numbers in which each term is the sum of the two preceding terms, exemplified as 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, and so forth. This sequence is ubiquitous in nature and art, and when intersected with the chromatic scale, results in an astonishing connection with music.

Leonardo da Vinci, a known admirer of the Fibonacci Sequence, evidenced this fascination through his research and art. In music, more recent composers like Ralph Vaughan Williams and Bela Bartók have composed pieces based on imperfect Fibonacci spirals, deriving inspiration from mathematical formulas.

Pythagorean Theorem and Musical Intervals

Pythagoras, the famous Greek philosopher, discovered a crucial link between the mathematical theory and music: the Pythagorean Theorem. Pythagoras found that when two notes were sounded together, if the sum of their sizes measured on an arbitrary logarithmic scale (such as a Musical Helmholtz Ear Trainer) equaled the size of a fourth, fifth, or octave, the tones formed a concsonance. Conversely, if the sum was equal to the size of a semitone, the tones resulted in dissonance. This feat established a cohesive link between mathematical ratios and musical harmony.

Music as a Tool to Teach Mathematics

Music promotes spatial-temporal learning, enhancing the education of the brain. Mathematics can be utilized as a musical tool, creating a more profound understanding of the mathematical principles and methods. Students can grasp complex mathematical concepts through musical melodies or rhythmic/geometric patterns. This integrative approach subsequently led to an advancement in mathematical proficiency, further demonstrating the profound connection between music and mathematics.

In conclusion, mathematics and music complement each other magnificently, captivating our senses and stimulating our intellects. As we explore the intricate layers of harmony within mathematics and music, we unveil a mesmerizing interplay BETWEEN the two disciplines. This interplay not only enriches our understanding of math and music, but fosters an intellectual curiosity that transcends the boundaries of our perceptions, opening our minds to the fascinating world beyond the visible spectrum.

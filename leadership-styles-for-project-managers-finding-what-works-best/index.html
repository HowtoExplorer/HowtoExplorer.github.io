Leadership Styles for Project Managers: Finding What Works Best


Project managers have numerous leadership styles at their disposal, each with its unique strengths and weaknesses. Successfully navigating the various leadership styles is critical to completing projects on time, within budget, and to the satisfaction of all stakeholders. Let’s explore some of the most prevalent leadership styles and understand when and how to use them.

The Authoritarian Leadership Style

Authoritarian leaders demand loyalty and respect from their team members and are in charge of virtually all decision-making processes. This style has its roots in the military, where a clear chain of command is crucial.

Pros:

  • Direct leadership not leaving room for confusion
  • Clear direction and structure
  • Unambiguous decision-making power

Cons:

  • Limited input from team members
  • Less creative problem-solving
  • A risk of demotivating team members

Authoritarian leadership is suitable when a project has set objectives and little room for deviation. However, with a diverse team that values contribution and collaboration, this style may not work well.

The Democratic Leadership Style

Democratic leaders encourage open communication and obtain opinions from team members before making decisions. They seek input from their team to incorporate valuable insights and motivate individuals to take ownership of the project.

Pros:

  • Team involvement and improved commitment
  • Enhanced problem-solving through diverse perspectives
  • Better trust and rapport among team members

Cons:

  • Slower decision-making processes
  • Possible difficulty in retaining control over the project
  • The risk of conflicts arising due to group discussions

Democratic leadership excels in situations where team members have valuable input and creativity plays a crucial role.

The Transformational Leadership Style

Transformational leaders inspire their team members to strive for excellence and achieve more than they initially thought possible. This style empowers team members to work collaboratively towards a shared vision.

Pros:

  • Increased motivation and commitment to the project
  • Enhanced creativity and innovation
  • Greater vision and communication sharing among team members

Cons:

  • Requires a certain level of literacy, trust and respect among team members
  • Not suitable for every situation or for every type of team
  • Vulnerable to exploitation if not appropriately managed

Transformational leadership thrives when managing complex projects requiring creative problem-solving and vision.

The Servant Leadership Style

Servant leaders prioritize the needs and well-being of their team. They work tirelessly to empower team members, create a positive work environment, and provide opportunities for personal and professional growth.

Pros:

  • Higher team morale and satisfaction
  • Effective team-building
  • Improved retention rates and performance

Cons:

  • Time-consuming to manage
  • May face resistance from traditional-minded team members
  • Relies on the leader’s emotional intelligence

Servant leadership is ideal to foster long-term, exceptional performance and to accommodate teams that require stable support and a conducive work environment.

As a project manager, it is crucial to assess the project’s goals, team dynamics, and individual personalities to determine the most suitable leadership style. A successful project manager continually adapts their leadership approach, recognizing that no one-size-fits-all solution exists. Through understanding and application of these varied leadership styles, a project manager can create a dynamic, effective working environment and achieve project success.

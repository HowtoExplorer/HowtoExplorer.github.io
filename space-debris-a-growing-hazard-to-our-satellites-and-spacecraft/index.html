Space Debris: A Growing Hazard to Our Satellites and Spacecraft


The world has come a long way since the first satellite, Sputnik, was launched into orbit in 1957. Since then, we have sent rovers to Mars, sent astronauts to the moon, and created countless satellites to aid in communication, navigation, and surveillance. However, as space exploration has grown, so too has the amount of debris floating around our planet. This accumulation of space debris is a growing hazard to our satellites and spacecraft, and it’s time for us to start taking it more seriously.

Our reliance on satellites has increased dramatically since the dawn of the information age. For communication, navigation, Earth observation, and research purposes, satellites are vital to modern society. Unfortunately, these man-made objects are often poorly designed, overused, or improperly de-orbited, leading to them becoming space debris. In the vastness of space, the chances of two objects colliding are extremely low, right? Unfortunately, no.

The sheer volume of space debris poses a significant risk to both active and proposed space missions. Space debris is any defunct human-made object orbiting Earth, such as discarded rocket stages, non-functioning satellites, and fragments of satellite collisions. According to the European Space Agency, there are an estimated 170 million pieces of debris larger than a centimeter, and over 7,000 operational satellites currently orbiting Earth.

The impact of space debris on modern spacecraft is profound. High-speed collisions with space debris can cause critical damage to delicate spacecraft components, compromise structural integrity, and result in disintegration. These collisions are difficult to predict, as identifying and tracking all space debris is nearly impossible. Most debris is invisible to the naked eye, and even at a microscopic scale, it can still cause severe damage.

Moreover, in the intense and unforgiving environment of space, additional micrometeoroids and orbital debris can penetrate an object’s protective layers, creating catastrophic damage. The quantity of debris surrounding our planet is quickly reaching a tipping point, making space risky and crossing the line from adventure to peril.

So, what can be done to address this growing hazard? For starters, we must develop better and more effective space-tracking technologies. Satellite owners and operators should collaborate to maintain databases of current satellite positions, predict future locations, and monitor on-orbit behaviors. Improved surveillance capabilities and enhanced space traffic management initiatives hold promise for preventing and minimizing incidents.

Secondly, the design and production of spacecraft should prioritize sustainability and ease of de-orbit. This change demands forward-thinking engineering, minimizing the probability of debris generation and ensuring readiness for controlled de-orbit around the end of each satellite’s lifespan. Furthermore, space enthusiasts must embrace proper disposal practices and seek to reduce the number of uncontrolled re-entries and break-ups of spacecraft in orbit.

Lastly, we must also educate and emphasize the importance of responsible space behavior with the younger generation. Inspiring future generations to become responsible space citizens is key to mitigating the growing hazard of space debris.

The fate of our space exploration lies in our hands. It’s time to consider the long-term consequences of our actions and collectively address the issue of space debris. The benefits of humanity exploring space are immeasurable, but we must ensure we take care not to jeopardize them by neglecting the growing hazard of space debris. Only by taking majority ownership, responsibility, and initiative can we ensure the preservation of our magnificent skies and the continued success of space missions. Let’s work together to ensure a sustainable future for space exploration.

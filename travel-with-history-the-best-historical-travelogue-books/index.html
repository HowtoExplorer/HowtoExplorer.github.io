Travel with History: The Best Historical Travelogue Books


Historical travelogues offer readers an enchanting blend of adventure, enlightening cultural insights, and a glimpse into the past. These books allow us to explore the world through the eyes of brilliant writers and adventurers who have traversed continents, conquered peaks, and crossed deserts throughout history. In this blog post, we bring you the best of historical travelogue books that have left indelible marks on the annals of travel literature.

1. The Innocents Abroad by Mark Twain

Undoubtedly one of the most renowned travelogues, The Innocents Abroad is also Mark Twain’s first travel book. Published in 1869, this memoir recounts his six-month-long journey through Europe and the Holy Land aboard a paddle-wheel steamship. Twain, known for his biting wit and hilarious anecdotes, narrates the story of a group of American tourists navigating foreign lands, encountering exotic sights, and unraveling enigmas of the past. Twain’s unique style brings the tour to life, giving readers an unforgettable experience of seeing the world through the eyes of an astute observer.

2. Around the World in Eighty Days by Jules Verne

First published in 1873, Around the World in Eighty Days is a classic adventure novel by French author Jules Verne. Hilarious mishaps, ingenious planning, and outlandish conjectures make up the narrative where Englishman Phileas Fogg must circumnavigate the globe in just eighty days to claim the bet of his life. Verne paints a picture of the world in the Victorian era, entwining fictional tales with current geographic and scientific knowledge, creating a thrilling travelogue that leaves readers inspired and awestruck.

3. The Journeyer by Tony Horwitz

In The Journeyer, American journalist Tony Horwitz retraces the centuries-old search for America by the 16th-century Venetian explorer, John Cabot. A Pulitzer Prize-winning author, Horwitz brings Cabot’s enigmatic voyage to life by visiting modern-day ports, forests, and battlefields in England, Italy, and the United States. Through meticulous research, Horwitz crafts an engaging story, examining the historical implications of Cabot’s legacy and reflecting on his own personal transformation throughout the journey.

4. The Hiding Place by Corrie ten Boom

While not a traditional travelogue, The Hiding Place is an extraordinary account of World War II resistance, survival, and faith. Written by Dutch author Corrie ten Boom, this compelling memoir tells the story of a family who hid Jews in their home during the Nazi occupation, for which they were ultimately imprisoned in a concentration camp. Ten Boom’s narrative encompasses her harrowing experiences in Ravensbrück, her remarkable resilience, and the importance of faith. The book offers a rare and humbling view into the tragedies of war, the power of forgiveness, and the indomitable spirit of humanity.

5. The Looming Tower by Lawrence Wright

Taking readers back to the late 20th century, Pulitzer Prize-winning author Lawrence Wright chronicles the rise of Al-Qaeda in The Looming Tower. Drawing from extensive research and interviews, Wright brilliantly intertwines the personal stories of three figures pivotal in the events leading to 9/11: John O’Neill, a former FBI official obsessed with the growing terrorist threat; Osama bin Laden, the mastermind behind the attacks; and Sheikh Omar Abdel-Rahman, a radical cleric whose fiery preaching shaped Muslim extremism. This gripping historical travelogue sheds light on the power of ideologies and the repercussions of political discord in shaping our world.

6. The Eiger Sanction by Trevanian

Set in the majestic Alps, The Eiger Sanction combines elements of travelogue and espionage by painting an intricate picture of mountaineering, art, and murder. American art historian and professor Jonathan Hemlock — with his expertise in climbing and knowledge of rare artworks — is entrusted with a covert mission. Suspenseful and captivating, this adventure-filled travelogue intertwines Hemlock’s academic pursuits with perilous mountainous escapades, offering readers a panoramic view of the treacherous world of high-altitude climbing.

Conclusion

Travelogues serve as testimonials of human experiences, allowing us to challenge our perceptions, immerse ourselves in unfamiliar cultures, and explore uncharted terrains. By delving into these historically rich and captivating accounts, we gain a deeper comprehension of the world and our shared past. So, whether you’re drawn to the rollicking escapades of globe-trotting adventurers, the heart-wrenching accounts of war and resistance, or the meticulous documentation of historical voyages, these travelogues are a must-read for every curious and intrepid explorer at heart. Happy reading!

Cybersecurity for Nonprofits: Best Practices


In today’s digital age, nonprofits are increasingly leveraging technology to optimize their operations, manage donor information, and expand their reach. However, this growing reliance on digital tools and platforms exposes these organizations to a range of cybersecurity threats, some of which can have devastating consequences. Cybersecurity is no longer optional—it’s a necessity for safeguarding sensitive data, maintaining trust, and ensuring that valuable resources are directed toward fulfilling the nonprofit’s mission.

While large corporations may have the budgets and expertise to build robust cybersecurity infrastructures, smaller nonprofits often operate with limited resources, making them particularly vulnerable to cyberattacks. However, prioritizing cybersecurity doesn’t have to be daunting. With attention to strategic practices, education, and planning, nonprofits can effectively safeguard their digital environments without breaking the bank.

The Unique Cybersecurity Challenges Facing Nonprofits

Before exploring the best practices, it’s essential to understand why nonprofits are at risk. Although every organization faces cybersecurity threats, nonprofits encounter certain unique challenges:

  1. Limited Budgets and Resources
    Nonprofits often allocate the majority of their funds to programmatic activities, leaving little room for investment in cybersecurity measures. This financial strain can limit access to advanced tools, professional services, or regular security audits.

  2. Sensitive Data
    Nonprofits frequently handle sensitive information such as donor details, payment card data, employee records, and beneficiary data. A breach of this information can damage the nonprofit’s reputation and result in legal or regulatory penalties.

  3. Target Status
    Many nonprofits underestimate their appeal to cybercriminals, assuming they are “too small” or “unimportant” to be targeted. On the contrary, their lack of robust defenses makes them an attractive target for hackers looking for easy access points.

  4. Lack of Cybersecurity Expertise
    Nonprofits may lack the necessary in-house expertise to implement a comprehensive cybersecurity strategy. Employees and volunteers may not have adequate training or awareness, which can lead to human error—one of the leading causes of data breaches.

  5. Reliance on Third-Party Tools
    To save costs, nonprofits often rely on third-party platforms for donor management, email campaigns, and more. While these tools are valuable, they also open the organization to vulnerabilities from outside their control if the tools are not secure.

The Impact of Cyber Threats on Nonprofits

The consequences of a cyberattack can be particularly devastating for nonprofits. A successful breach may result in:

  • Loss of donor trust and reputation damage
  • Diversion of already scarce financial resources to address the fallout
  • Operational disruption, affecting mission-critical activities
  • Legal and regulatory penalties for non-compliance with data protection laws

Understanding these high stakes underscores why nonprofits must take proactive steps to protect themselves. Below, we outline essential cybersecurity best practices tailored to the specific needs of nonprofit organizations.

Best Practices for Nonprofit Cybersecurity

The journey toward a secure digital environment begins with awareness and proactive planning. Here are the best practices every nonprofit should adopt:

1. Prioritize Cybersecurity Training

Your workforce is your first line of defense. Volunteers, staff, and board members should all receive regular training on cybersecurity awareness. Sessions should cover topics such as:

  • Recognizing phishing emails
  • Avoiding social engineering scams
  • Creating strong, unique passwords
  • Reporting suspicious activity

Consider tailoring training sessions to make them relevant to specific roles within your organization. Interactive exercises, such as simulated phishing tests, can help reinforce key lessons.

2. Implement Robust Access Controls

Restricting access to sensitive information is crucial to minimizing risk. Adopt the principle of “least privilege,” where individuals only have access to the systems and data they need for their specific roles. Key steps include:

  • Utilizing role-based access controls for software and databases
  • Routinely reviewing and updating permissions
  • Locking accounts immediately when staff or volunteers leave the organization

Additionally, consider using Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for an added layer of security.

3. Secure Donor Data

As donors place their trust in your organization, safeguarding their information should be a top priority. Steps to secure donor data include:

  • Using secure, trusted platforms for online transactions
  • Encrypting sensitive information both in transit and at rest
  • Complying with privacy regulations, such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) or the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), depending on your location

Transparency is also key. Inform donors about how their data will be used, and reassure them of your commitment to safeguarding their information.

4. Keep Software and Systems Up to Date

Cybercriminals often exploit outdated software vulnerabilities. Combat this by implementing a strict patch management policy:

  • Enable automatic updates for operating systems, applications, and antivirus software
  • Routinely monitor for end-of-life software and replace it with supported versions
  • Include any third-party tools in your update procedures

By ensuring your systems are up-to-date, you reduce the risk of known vulnerabilities being exploited.

5. Establish a Strong Password Policy

Weak and reused passwords are a common entry point for hackers. To address this:

  • Require passwords to be long (12+ characters) and incorporate a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols
  • Mandate periodic password updates
  • Prohibit password reuse across multiple systems
  • Encourage the use of password managers for securely generating and storing unique credentials

Password hygiene plays a significant role in enhancing organizational security.

6. Back Up Data Regularly

Routine backups are a crucial measure to mitigate the impact of ransomware attacks or accidental data loss. Follow these best practices for backups:

  • Schedule automatic daily backups of critical data
  • Use the 3-2-1 rule: Keep three copies of your data, on two different media types, with one copy stored offsite
  • Test your backups periodically to ensure they can be restored without issues

Having reliable backups ensures that your organization can recover quickly and continue operations following an incident.

7. Adopt Network Security Protocols

Protecting your nonprofit’s network is foundational for cybersecurity. To achieve this:

  • Install and configure firewalls
  • Enable encryption for your Wi-Fi connection
  • Use Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) for remote working
  • Regularly scan for and address vulnerabilities within your network infrastructure

Encouraging staff to connect to secure networks—especially when working remotely—can further reduce risks.

8. Plan for Incidents with a Response Strategy

Even with strong defenses, breaches can still occur. Preparing for these scenarios with an Incident Response Plan (IRP) minimizes downtime and mitigates damage. Your plan should:

  • Define roles and responsibilities during a cybersecurity event
  • Include clear guidelines for identifying, escalating, and addressing threats
  • Detail communication protocols (both internal and external)
  • Incorporate regular testing through mock drills to refine your approach

Knowing how to respond promptly can make a significant difference during a crisis.

9. Secure Your Third-Party Relationships

Since many nonprofits rely on external vendors for services, it’s essential to evaluate these partners’ cybersecurity practices. Some steps to take include:

  • Reviewing contracts to ensure accountability for data protection
  • Monitoring vendor compliance with relevant security standards
  • Limiting data shared with third parties to only what is necessary

Strong vendor management practices help mitigate risks that are outside your direct control.

10. Consider Cyber Insurance

Cyber insurance provides financial protection and resources in the event of a data breach or cyberattack. Policies may cover costs such as legal fees, public relations efforts, and recovery expenses. While an additional expense, it’s worth exploring whether cyber insurance aligns with your budget and risk management strategy.

A Commitment to Security Empowers Your Mission

Cybersecurity is a journey, not a destination. As a nonprofit, adopting and maintaining security best practices demonstrates your commitment to protecting the people and communities you serve. By taking proactive steps to secure your operations, you bolster trust among your supporters, safeguard critical resources, and enable the organization to focus on its larger mission.

While the challenges may seem daunting, even small, consistent changes can lead to a significant improvement in your cybersecurity posture. Remember: cybersecurity isn’t just about technology—it’s about fostering a culture of awareness, responsibility, and resilience. Developing such a culture ensures that your nonprofit continues making a positive impact in a digital world rife with threats.

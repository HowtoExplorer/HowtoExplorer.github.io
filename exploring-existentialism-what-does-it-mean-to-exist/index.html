Exploring Existentialism: What Does It Mean to Exist?


Existentialism is one of the most compelling philosophical movements of the 20th century, and it continues to influence various fields of study today. It delves into profound questions about life, freedom, and the meaning of existence itself. What does it truly mean to exist? Is existence inherently meaningful, or is meaning something that we, as individuals, must create ourselves? These are central questions in existentialism, a philosophy that has captured the minds of thinkers, writers, and artists alike for decades.

In this post, we will explore the fundamental ideas of existentialism and examine how this philosophy encourages us to reflect on our existence. We will also discuss its key themes, major existentialist philosophers, and the impact existentialism has had on contemporary thought.

The Essence of Existentialism

At its core, existentialism is a philosophical inquiry into human existence. It explores themes like freedom, choice, isolation, and the search for meaning in a seemingly indifferent or absurd world. Existentialist thinkers reject the idea that life has an inherent, preordained meaning. Instead, they propose that it is up to each individual to create meaning through their own actions, decisions, and experiences.

Existentialism emerged in the 19th and 20th centuries, largely as a response to the growing sense of alienation and disillusionment that arose from industrialization, scientific advancements, and the decline of traditional religious beliefs. In an increasingly secular world, people began to question the fundamental assumptions about the purpose and nature of human life. Existentialist thinkers aimed to answer these questions, focusing on the individual’s experience of existence rather than abstract or universal truths.

Key Themes in Existentialism

Existentialism is not a monolithic philosophy, but rather a diverse movement with varying perspectives. However, several core themes run through the work of most existentialist thinkers. These themes include:

  1. Freedom and Responsibility Existentialism places great emphasis on human freedom. According to existentialists, we are fundamentally free to choose our actions and define our lives. However, this freedom comes with a heavy responsibility. In a world without inherent meaning, individuals must take full responsibility for their choices, and they cannot escape the consequences of their actions.

    This concept is famously encapsulated by Jean-Paul Sartre’s phrase “existence precedes essence.” Sartre argued that humans are not born with a predefined purpose or nature; instead, we must create our own essence through our choices. While this freedom is liberating, it can also be overwhelming and lead to feelings of anxiety or “angst,” as individuals realize the weight of their responsibility.

  2. The Absurd One of the most prominent existential themes is the idea of the absurd. The absurd refers to the conflict between our desire for meaning and the apparent meaninglessness of the universe. This theme was developed in-depth by Albert Camus, who argued that life is inherently without purpose, yet humans persist in searching for significance. The absurd arises from this tension between our need for order and meaning and the chaotic, indifferent universe in which we find ourselves.

    Camus proposed that we must confront the absurd head-on, without resorting to false hopes or illusions. In his essay The Myth of Sisyphus, Camus likens the human struggle to that of the Greek mythological figure Sisyphus, condemned to push a boulder up a hill only for it to roll back down every time he nears the top. While Sisyphus’s task may seem futile, Camus suggests that we, too, must find joy in the struggle itself, embracing the absurdity of life without surrendering to despair.

  3. Authenticity and the Self Authenticity is another central theme in existentialism. To live authentically is to live in accordance with one’s true self, rather than conforming to societal expectations or external norms. Existentialists argue that many people lead inauthentic lives, influenced by the pressures of society, tradition, or religion. They may adopt roles, identities, or values imposed by others, rather than choosing them for themselves.

    The journey toward authenticity is an ongoing process of self-discovery and self-creation. It involves recognizing and embracing one’s freedom, accepting responsibility for one’s choices, and confronting the often uncomfortable truths about human existence. To live authentically is to fully engage with life, accepting both its joys and its hardships, without relying on illusions or distractions.

  4. Isolation and Alienation Existentialist thinkers also explore the feelings of isolation and alienation that often accompany human existence. This sense of alienation arises from the realization that we are ultimately alone in the universe. While we may form relationships and communities, the existentialist view is that no one can truly share the full experience of our individual existence. This isolation is exacerbated by the realization that, in a universe without inherent meaning, we must create our own sense of purpose and meaning, often in isolation.

    The existentialist concept of “bad faith” refers to the tendency to avoid the anxiety of existential freedom by embracing false or superficial beliefs. People may conform to societal norms, adopt religious dogma, or seek comfort in distractions, all to avoid confronting the reality of their own freedom and responsibility. However, existentialism asserts that such evasions ultimately lead to inauthentic living and deeper feelings of alienation.

Major Existentialist Philosophers

Existentialism is a broad and diverse philosophical movement, and many thinkers have contributed to its development. While the following philosophers are not the only ones to have shaped existentialism, they are among the most influential:

Søren Kierkegaard

Often considered the “father” of existentialism, Søren Kierkegaard was a 19th-century Danish philosopher whose work focused on themes of faith, anxiety, and the individual’s relationship with God. Kierkegaard is best known for his concept of “the leap of faith,” which suggests that individuals must make a personal, existential commitment to faith, despite the uncertainty and irrationality of existence. He also explored the idea of despair, which arises when individuals fail to live authentically and embrace their true self.

Friedrich Nietzsche

Friedrich Nietzsche, a German philosopher, is another key figure in existentialism. Nietzsche is famous for his declaration that “God is dead,” which reflects his belief that traditional religious and moral systems no longer provide adequate guidance for modern life. Nietzsche rejected the notion of an objective, universal morality and argued that individuals must create their own values in order to live fully. His concept of the “Übermensch” (superman) represents the ideal of a person who has transcended conventional morality and embraced their creative potential.

Jean-Paul Sartre

Jean-Paul Sartre is perhaps the most well-known existentialist philosopher. Sartre’s philosophy emphasizes radical freedom and responsibility. In his major work, Being and Nothingness, Sartre explores the idea that human beings are condemned to be free. He argues that there is no predetermined essence or purpose to human life, and that individuals must create their own meaning through their choices and actions. Sartre’s concept of “bad faith” refers to the tendency to avoid the anxiety of freedom by pretending that one is not responsible for their choices.

Simone de Beauvoir

Simone de Beauvoir, a French existentialist and feminist, is best known for her work The Second Sex, in which she examines the ways in which women have been historically oppressed and reduced to a secondary status. De Beauvoir’s existentialism is deeply concerned with the ways in which individuals create their own identity, and she emphasizes the importance of recognizing and overcoming societal constraints. She famously wrote, “One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman,” arguing that gender roles are socially constructed rather than biologically determined.

Albert Camus

Albert Camus, a French-Algerian philosopher, is known for his exploration of the absurd. In works like The Myth of Sisyphus and The Stranger, Camus explores the tension between the human desire for meaning and the apparent meaninglessness of the world. Rather than succumbing to despair, Camus advocates for embracing life in all its absurdity and continuing to seek meaning, even in the face of an indifferent universe.

The Impact of Existentialism on Modern Thought

Existentialism has had a profound impact on a wide range of disciplines, from philosophy and literature to psychology and political theory. It has influenced the works of writers such as Franz Kafka, Samuel Beckett, and Virginia Woolf, who explored existential themes in their novels and plays. Existentialist ideas have also shaped contemporary psychology, particularly in the field of existential psychotherapy, which focuses on helping individuals confront the challenges of freedom, isolation, and meaning.

Existentialism has also played a significant role in political thought, particularly in relation to the concepts of individual freedom and authenticity. The existentialist emphasis on personal responsibility and choice has inspired movements advocating for human rights, freedom of expression, and social justice.

Conclusion

In conclusion, existentialism offers a powerful lens through which we can examine the nature of human existence. It challenges us to confront the fundamental questions of life, freedom, and meaning, and encourages us to take responsibility for the choices we make. While existentialism can be unsettling in its acknowledgment of life’s inherent uncertainty and absurdity, it also offers the potential for deep personal transformation. By embracing our freedom and living authentically, we can create meaning in our lives, even in a world that may seem indifferent to our existence.

Ultimately, existentialism invites us to explore what it means to exist—not just as passive observers of the world, but as active participants who shape our own destiny through our choices, actions, and relationships. The journey of self-discovery and meaning-making is ongoing, but it is one that can lead to a richer, more authentic life.

The Role of Follow-up Emails After an Interview


After an interview, you've accomplished the initial step, but the process is far from over. In fact, follow-up emails can be the determining factor in whether you seal the deal or lose your chance at securing the position. In this crucial phase, it's essential to strike the perfect balance between giving yourself a chance to confirm your interest in the position and the company and expressing your gratitude and professionalism to the hiring manager. Sending a reminder after a week reiterates your interest in the role and gives you a second chance to reconnect with the hiring manager. The Power of Follow-up Emails After an Interview: How to Close the Deal and Build a Strong Relationships Why Follow-up Emails Matter Sending a follow-up email after an interview is a crucial step in the hiring process. It's a way to reiterate your interest in the position, inquire about the status of your application, and leave a lasting impression on the hiring manager. This is especially important in today's competitive job market, where emails can easily get misplaced or overlooked. When to Send Follow-up Emails Timing is everything when it comes to sending follow-up emails. It's recommended to send the first follow-up email one week after the interview. This allows the hiring manager sufficient time to review your application and compare you with other candidates. Sending a reminder after a week reiterates your interest in the role and gives you a second chance to reconnect with the hiring manager. How to Craft a Follow-up Email Crafting a follow-up email requires a unique blend of confidence, politeness, and consistency. Here are some essential steps to keep in mind when writing your follow-up email:
  5. Keep it Short and Sweet A follow-up email shouldn't be too long, but it should be substantial enough to make an impression. Aim for a email that's around 150-200 words.
  6. Personalize it A well-written follow-up email should include specific examples of how your skills align with the role and how you can contribute to the company. This shows that you've taken the time to research and understand the company's needs.
  7. Express Your Gratitude Restate your gratitude for the opportunity to interview and reiterate your interest in the position. This demonstrates your enthusiasm for the role and the company, and it leaves a positive impression on the hiring manager.
  8. Show Interest in the Application Process Ask about the status of your application and when you can expect to hear back from the hiring manager. This shows that you're interested in the role and eager to move forward in the process.
  9. End on a Positive Note Close the email with a positive note by thanking the hiring manager again and reiterating your confidence in your abilities. This leaves a lasting impression and maintains a positive tone. Example of a Follow-up Email Here's an example of a follow-up email that incorporates the steps above: Dear [Hiring Manager], I wanted to follow up on our interview from [Date] and express my continued interest in the [Position] role at [Company]. I was impressed by the company culture and the team's enthusiasm for the project. I'm confident that my skills and experience make me a strong fit for the position. I would appreciate an update on the status of my application and any additional information you may need from me to move forward in the process. Please let me know if there's anything else I can provide to further support my application. Thank you again for the opportunity to interview, and I look forward to hearing back from you soon. Best, [Your Name] Conclusion A follow-up email is a crucial step in the hiring process that shows your interest in the position and leaves a lasting impression on the hiring manager. By crafting a well-written follow-up email that incorporates the steps above, you can increase your chances of getting hired and build a strong relationship with the hiring manager. Remember to personalize the email, express your gratitude, and show interest in the application process.

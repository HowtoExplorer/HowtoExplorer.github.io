The ExoMars Mission: The European Quest for Martian Exploration

3 min · 594 words

The Red Planet has always captivated the imagination of humans. With its barren landscapes and potential for harboring signs of life, Mars has become the focal point of numerous space missions over the years. One such mission that has gained significant attention is the ExoMars mission, a joint endeavor by the European Space Agency (ESA) and Roscosmos, the Russian space agency. In this blog post, we delve into the intricacies of the ExoMars mission and explore the European quest for Martian exploration.

Unraveling the Mysteries of Mars

The ExoMars mission aims to answer some of the most profound questions about Mars and its potential for sustaining life. It consists of a series of missions designed to explore the planet’s atmosphere, geology, and search for signs of past or present life. The mission’s primary objectives are:

  1. Searching for Evidence of Life: One of the main goals of the ExoMars mission is to search for signs of life on Mars. The mission includes the deployment of the Rosalind Franklin rover, equipped with a suite of instruments capable of analyzing the Martian soil and drilling up to two meters below the surface. By studying the planet’s geology and searching for organic molecules, the mission aims to uncover clues about the possibility of life on Mars.

  2. Understanding the Martian Environment: The ExoMars mission also focuses on understanding the Martian environment and its potential for supporting life. The Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO), a key component of the mission, is tasked with studying the planet’s atmosphere and searching for trace gases, such as methane. These gases could be indicators of geological or biological activity on Mars.

  3. Advancing Technological Capabilities: The ExoMars mission serves as a stepping stone for future Mars exploration missions. Through the development and testing of advanced technologies, the mission aims to pave the way for more ambitious projects, such as human missions to Mars.

The ExoMars Mission Timeline

The ExoMars mission is divided into two phases, each with its own set of objectives and spacecraft:

  1. ExoMars 2016: The first phase of the mission, ExoMars 2016, consisted of the launch of the Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) and the Schiaparelli lander. The TGO successfully entered Mars’ orbit in October 2016 and began its atmospheric studies. However, the Schiaparelli lander, intended to demonstrate the landing technology, unfortunately crash-landed on the Martian surface.

  2. ExoMars 2020: The second phase of the mission, ExoMars 2020, is set to launch in 2022. It includes the Rosalind Franklin rover, a state-of-the-art robotic vehicle equipped with a drill and a suite of scientific instruments. The rover will be tasked with collecting samples from the Martian surface and analyzing them for signs of life.

Challenges and Collaborative Efforts

The ExoMars mission is not without its challenges. Mars is a harsh environment, with extreme temperatures, thin atmosphere, and high levels of radiation. Designing spacecraft capable of surviving these conditions and conducting scientific experiments is no easy feat.

To overcome these challenges, the European Space Agency (ESA) has collaborated with Roscosmos, pooling their resources and expertise. The joint effort ensures that the mission benefits from the collective knowledge and experience of both agencies. Additionally, the ExoMars mission has also involved contributions from various international partners, further enhancing the scientific and technological capabilities of the mission.

Looking Ahead: The Future of Martian Exploration

The ExoMars mission represents a pivotal moment in the European quest for Martian exploration. By unraveling the mysteries of Mars, searching for signs of life, and advancing technological capabilities, the mission is poised to reshape our understanding of the Red Planet.

As the ExoMars 2020 phase approaches, anticipation builds, and the scientific community eagerly awaits the data and discoveries that will be brought back to Earth. The European quest for Martian exploration continues, and with each mission, we inch closer to unraveling the enigmatic secrets of our neighboring planet.

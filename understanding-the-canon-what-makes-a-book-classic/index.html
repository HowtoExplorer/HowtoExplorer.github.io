Understanding the Canon: What Makes a Book Classic?


A book's status as a classic is often determined by factors beyond its publication date or literary merit. While some books may be widely known and admired for generations, others may be less familiar, even if they share similar qualities. So, what makes a book a classic, and how do we distinguish it from other notable works of literature? Historical Significance One key aspect of a classic is its historical significance. A classic often represents a pivotal moment in time, reflecting the major concerns and events of its era. For example, Nathaniel Hawthorne's The Scarlet Letter (1850) explores the complexities of Puritanism in 17th-century Massachusetts, conveying the darker aspects of American history. This connection to the past provides readers with a unique perspective on the author's treatment of themes and ideas that were relevant in the past but continue to resonate with contemporary society. Thematic Resonance Classics often possess timeless themes that transcend their original context. These themes, though first introduced in the book's time, are relatable to modern readers, and their exploration remains relevant today. Fyodor Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment (1866) exemplifies this quality with its exploration of existential crises, uncertainty, and redemption. As we continue to grapple with the human condition today, this novel's uncanny understanding of psychological complexes retains its impact. Aesthetic Expression The writing style, structure, and form of a classic represent innovative expressions of artistic storytelling. Classics often subvert or reflect prevailing modes of writing, contributing to their influence on literary history. For instance, James Joyce's Ulysses (1922) predictably wears a unique mask of novelistic mastery with stream-of-consciousness narration, weather than rich poetic precursors.

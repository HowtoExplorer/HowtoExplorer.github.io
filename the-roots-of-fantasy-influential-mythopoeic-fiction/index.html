The Roots of Fantasy: Influential Mythopoeic Fiction


Fantasy, a genre born from the dreams and desires of countless authors, has captivated our imagination since time immemorial. With origins tracing back to the ancient mythologies and folklore that fed our ancestors’ hunger for the unknown, it has now grown into a multi-billion dollar industry the world over. But where, precisely, do the roots of fantasy lie? The answer lies in mythopoeic fiction—a term coined by author and philosopher J.R.R. Tolkien, referring to fiction that constructs its own mythology. In this blog post, we will delve into the murky depths of past tales and legends, exploring five influential mythopoeic works that have fractured the boundaries of our reality, opening doors to realms we dare not dream of.

  1. The Vedas and The Epics of Ancient India

Perched on the cradle of human civilization in the fertile plains of the Indus and Ganges rivers lies the tapestry of ancient Indian mythology. This drape thickens with the arrival of the Vedic Age, a period well known for its four canonical sacred texts known as the Vedas—Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, and Atharvaveda. Treating readers to verses about gods and goddesses like Indra, Agni, and Varuna, the Vedas emerge as the backbone of mythopoeic fiction.

Nourished by fertile soil, the seeds of ancient Indian epics—Ramayana and Mahabharata—sprouted, laying the foundations of mythopoeic fiction. Telling tales of divine battles, cosmic romances, grand sagas, and moralistic allegories, the epics enriched the lives of future fantasy authors from J.R.R. Tolkien to George R.R. Martin.

  1. The Norse Sagas

While the ancient Indian epics capture the beauty of the eastern world, the Norse sagas render the tales of the Vikings and heroes like Odin, Thor, and Loki. Fragments of these breathtaking and brutal tales have found their way into modern fantasy literature. In fact, Tolkien’s Middle Earth became heavily influenced by Norse mythology. From the awesome might of the one-eyed god, Odin, to the thunderous weapon, Mjolnir, wielded by Thor, echoes of Norse deities resound through fantasy realms.

  1. The Arrival of Tolkien’s Middle Earth

When J.R.R. Tolkien released The Hobbit in 1937 and The Lord of the Rings trilogy in 1954-1955, the fantasy genre gained unprecedented prominence. Tolkien painstakingly crafted a complete mythopoeic world, with its own languages, races, history, and ecosystems. The creation of Middle Earth bore the marks of numerous mythologies—the Norse sagas, Welsh and Irish legends, and the Germanic and Nordic mythologies. Using an intricate framework of customs and history, Tolkien unlocked the potential of mythopoeic fiction, setting the stage for an entire subgenre of fiction to blossom.

  1. The Multiverse of Fritz Leiber

Although Tolkien’s Middle Earth tilled the earth for this budding genre, Fritz Leiber, an American author, took it to the skies. Leiber introduced us to Lankhmar, a city of thieves, wretches, and heroes. His tales of Fafhrd and Grey Mouser, two tall, free-spirited swordsmen, centered around complex themes of friendship, adventure, and philosophical musings. Fritz Leiber’s Lankhmar, with its blend of humor, action, and thought-provoking themes, served as a catalyst for modern fantasy fiction.

  1. Urban Fantasy Legacy of Terry Pratchett

Hailing from the grim darkness of the 21st century comes Sir Terry Pratchett, a true enigma in the realm of mythopoeic fiction. Instead of creating entire new worlds, Terry Pratchett decided to drench the world we live in with fantasy. His Discworld series, a surreal twisted disc balanced on the backs of four elephants who, in turn, stand on a giant turtle named Great A’Tuin, encouraged readers to zoom in on the absurdities of everyday life and the quirks of humanity.

In the labyrinthine fiction universe, Terry Pratchett infused his unique wit and humor, challenging and reshaping mythopoeic fantasy itself. His stories about misfit characters navigating this strange world unearthed contemporary social and political themes, a stark contrast to Tolkien’s medieval romps.

In conclusion, the roots of fantasy lie in mythopoeic fiction. The seeds of this genre can be traced back to ancient civilizations that developed their own mythologies, but the plants grew only when the ground was prepared by stopping the flow of time, the imaginative worlds of ancient India, Norse sagas, Tolkien’s Middle Earth, Leiber’s Lankhmar, and the very familiar Discworld. What remains to be seen is where mythopoeic fiction will go from here—a journey worth taking, for who knows where the fertile soil of the human imagination may lead us?

The genre of fantasy might just be an elaborate scheme to power the engine of imagination, allowing us to dream and explore new worlds without ever leaving the comfort of our reading nooks. And as we continue to mine more from the rich history of mythology and folklore, a brighter future awaits the boundless realm of mythopoeic fiction.

Active Listening: The Foundation of Strong Interpersonal Skills


Most of us are submerged in a tumultuous sea of information, from coworkers to friends and family. Our lives revolve around interactions with others, whether in business or personal settings. The cornerstone of these interactions lies in effective communication. Here, we examine the least understood and often neglected component - active listening.

What is Active Listening?

Simply put, active listening is focusing completely on the speaker, understanding their message, interpreting the verbal and non-verbal cues accurately, and responding appropriately. This not only involves verbal communication but also non-verbal cues like facial expressions, body language, and tone of voice. Active listening is a process of granting both speaker and listener equal power.

Why is it important?

The world is increasingly virtual, with most interactions tool-based. Still, human to human communication remains critical, whether resolving work conflicts or nurturing personal relationships. Active listening enhances our ability to empathize, understand, and respond to people’s emotions. It is an invaluable professional skill for conflict resolution and building healthy interpersonal relationships. Finally, active listening bolsters team productivity and communication success.

Four Pillars of Active Listening

  1. Preparation: Active listening begins with a mindset of open-mindedness and willingness to understand the speaker. Preparation includes mentally preparing oneself, focusing on the speaker, posing relevant questions to clear any doubts, and using positive language for effective engagement.

  2. Understanding: To comprehend the speaker’s message, we must break down and analyze the message. This step involves paraphrasing, summarizing, and reflecting on the content. Reflecting on information not only validates the speaker’s emotions but also assists listeners in deepening their understanding.

  3. Assessment: Verbal and non-verbal cues are equally crucial for accurate assessment. Awareness of paralinguistic features, such as speed, pitch, volume, and rhythm, not only generate a richer understanding of the speaker’s message, but they also reveal subconscious emotions and feelings. Effective listeners can use these cues to assess the speaker’s mood, facts, and motives.

  4. Application: The concluding step involves responding accurately to all aforementioned factors. In this phase, it is necessary to apply knowledge about the speaker to create mutual understanding and adapt communication style based on context.

Practice Makes Perfect

Whether it’s listening to coworkers, clients, or friends, combining these principles can lead to improved relationships, effective team performance, and enhanced self-awareness. Hence, continuous practice strengthens our active listening skills, benefiting both personal and professional lives. We must remember that active listening is not merely an opportunity to respond; rather, it is an art of understanding and showing empathy toward others.

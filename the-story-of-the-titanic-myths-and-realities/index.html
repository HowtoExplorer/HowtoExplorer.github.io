The Story of the Titanic: Myths and Realities


The RMS Titanic, a British passenger liner that met its fate on the night of April 14, 1912, has been a subject of fascination for many years. The tragedy that struck the world on that fateful night has given rise to numerous myths and misconceptions, often perpetuated through popular culture. In this article, we'll delve into the history surrounding the Titanic, debunk some common myths, and separate fact from fiction. The Background The story of the Titanic begins several months before its ill-fated maiden voyage. The ship's concept was generated by the Harland and Wolff shipyard in Belfast, Ireland, in response to the need for more transatlantic crossings. Designed to be the most luxurious and technologically advanced ship of its time, the Titanic was meant to symbolize the epitome of White Star Line's fleet. The White Star Line was a prestigious shipping company owned by John Pierpont Morgan's International Mercantile Marine Company. At the helm of the 'unsinkable' ship stood Captain Edward Smith, with a career spanning nearly 27 years of sailing experience, including serving in the early 1900s on the Olympic, the sister ship of the Titanic in the White Star Line.

When Good Employees Go Bad: Tips for Overcoming Red Flags in Interviews


Interviews are a crucial part of the hiring process. They provide insights into a candidate’s personality, communication skills, and how well they will fit into the company culture. However, sometimes even good employees exhibit red flags during interviews that may indicate future problems in the workplace. To help you make informed decisions, here are some tips for overcoming red flags and identifying potential issues early on.

  1. Be aware of candidate manipulation techniques

Some candidates may try to manipulate the interview process to appear more desirable to the interviewer. This could involve embellishing their resume, inflating their achievements, or even trying to find common ground with the interviewer excessively. Remember, it’s essential to keep an open mind while maintaining a level of skepticism. Look for consistent, honest responses rather than perfect answers.

  1. Watch for signs of dishonesty

While everyone may fudge a little on their resume, some candidates are more dishonest than others. Pay attention to inconsistencies in their employment history, gaps in their work experience, or fibs about specific skills. Trust your instincts and ask follow-up questions to clarify any discrepancies.

  1. Explore workplace fit

Workplace fit plays a crucial role in employee satisfaction and retention. Investigate the candidate’s preferences and values to gauge how well they will integrate with the company culture. Ask about their work style, communication methods, and problem-solving strategies to assess compatibility.

  1. Look for signs of attitude issues

Even if a candidate has impressive skills and experience, a negative attitude can create an atmosphere of dysfunction in the workplace. During the interview, observe their demeanor, verbal and non-verbal cues, and the way they treat others. Confidence is an asset, but arrogance, indifference, or dismissiveness should set off warning bells.

  1. Assess problem-solving abilities

The ability to think critically and solve problems is essential, especially in fast-paced environments. When discussing previous work experiences, listen for evidence of the candidate’s problem-solving skills and resilience under pressure. If they seem to be struggling to articulate their problem-solving process, consider if they’re capable of adapting and learning.

  1. Validate references

While you shouldn’t rely solely on references, the insights from past employers can add valuable context to a candidate’s application. Follow up with references and consider any red flags noted by their former colleagues and managers. Maintain confidentiality by respecting their feedback and remaining open-minded.

  1. Consider the bigger picture

Sometimes, red flags may be unrelated to the candidate’s work performance or character. Personal issues or extenuating circumstances could be affecting their behavior during the interview. As an employer, it’s essential to remain empathetic and discerning, striking a balance between understanding and skepticism.

In conclusion, recognizing and addressing red flags during interviews is crucial for building a successful team. By being aware of candidate manipulation, signs of dishonesty, workplace fit, and attitude issues, employers can make informed hiring decisions. Don’t overlook the importance of problem-solving abilities, validated references, and understanding the bigger picture when evaluating potential employees. Success is ultimately contingent on assembling a team of trustworthy, compatible, and skilled individuals who contribute positively to the company’s mission and values.

Cultural Influences on Fashion Sustainability


Fashion has always been a representation of culture, reflecting societal values, beliefs, and expressions of identity. The influence of culture on fashion sustainability is undeniable, as it shapes the way we consume, produce, and wear clothes. Fashion sustainability encompasses environmental and social aspects, focusing on reducing waste, preserving resources, and empowering communities. In this piece, we will explore how cultural influences impact the fashion sustainability movement, highlighting different beliefs and practices from around the world.

The slow fashion movement originated in developed countries, primarily influenced by environmentalism and individualistic values. This ideology promotes buying fewer, higher-quality garments, reducing the overall consumption of clothing. In contrast, sustainable fashion in Asia is driven by cultural beliefs emphasizing minimalism, recycling, and upcycling. Japanese fashion goes beyond the “less is more” philosophy, combining sustainability with an art form known as “kōgei,” or exquisite craftsmanship. The Japanese initiative “mend and make new” encourages consumers to repair and revitalize their garments instead of discarding them – a concept now gaining traction in the West.

Chinese culture has also had a significant impact on fashion sustainability. Traditional Chinese artforms, such as embroidery and batik, often repurpose old textiles or sacred fabrics that carry cultural significance, such as wedding gowns. These practices not only reduce waste but also preserve cultural heritage. Similarly, African tribal communities have been producing upcycled clothes for centuries. The Maasai, for instance, traditionally reuse their iconic red shukas multiple times before passing them down. Nigerian women craft stunning clothes from waste materials like abuko, a versatile and vibrant fabric made from discarded bottles.

India, a melting pot of diverse cultures and traditions, boasts a rich history of sustainable fashion. The ancient concept of “vastu shastra,” the science of architecture and design, explores how human lives intertwine with the environment. It influences Indian fashion designers’ approach to sustainable clothing, promoting organic and natural materials. Munir Zaki, a renowned Indian designer, creates sustainable garments using handwoven textiles by self-stimulating local Indian artisans’ economy.

Fashion sustainability finds a voice in Indigenous communities worldwide. Indigenous peoples often prioritize cultural heritage and environmental consciousness. Native American tribes utilize traditional natural dyes from plants, such as indigo and cochineal, while Australian Aboriginal groups embrace the concept of “Country” – a deep connection between people and their environment, underpinning the sustainable use of natural resources. These cultures often express resistance against Western commercial fashion, promoting a sustainable approach that celebrates the uniqueness and authenticity of their communities.

As fashion sustainability continues to grow in popularity, cultural influences spanning the globe profoundly impact its development. As we strive toward a more sustainable fashion industry, recognizing and respecting these diverse practices and perspectives will create a richer, more equitable, and inclusive movement promoting a sustainable future for all.

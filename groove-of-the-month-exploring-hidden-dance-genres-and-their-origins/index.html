Groove of the Month: Exploring Hidden Dance Genres and Their Origins


The world of dance music is vast, colorful, and ever-evolving. From the smooth beats of soulful R&B to the hard-hitting drops of dubstep, the spectrum of dance music is as broad as it is diverse. Some dance genres may be well-known to the masses, but there are a multitude of hidden gems just waiting to be explored. This month, let’s break past mainstream boundaries and journey through 5 lesser-known dance genres and their fascinating origins.

1. Basket Sekki (Japan)

Originating in the ancient city of Kish, Iraq, around 3000 BCE, basket sekki is a prime example of how worldly cultural exchange has shaped the dance music landscape. Decorated with basket-like patterns, this genre developed in Japan during the Muromachi period (1336-1573 CE). Although hard to pinpoint an exact musical style, basket sekki is generally characterized by unison playing and a two-part melodic structure.

Throughout the Edo period (1603-1868 CE), basket sekki became a cornerstone of the Japanese Bon dance scene, a summer tradition of spiritual celebration to honor one’s ancestors. It pairs perfectly with the lively rhythm of a taiko drum, a traditional Japanese instrumental that adds depth and energy to the genre. To this day, basket sekki continues to remain an indispensable part of Japanese folk dance music.

2. Balkan Bass (USA)

A relatively new player in the dance music arena, Balkan bass emerged in the underground clubs of the United States around the early 2000s. Born out of a fusion of traditional Balkan brass band music, with sounds and beats from electronica and dubstep, Balkan bass excites the senses with its unique, funky, and big-band approach to music production.

Music genres like gypsy punk, klezmer, and ghevontik all contribute to Balkan bass’s complex arrangement, but its captivating sound wouldn’t be complete without the use of brass. The characteristic brass and percussion envelope unique vocal harmonizations and rib-ticklingly tight ensemble playing - all enhanced by modern electronica.

3. Changa Tounde (Togo, West Africa)

Changa tounde, an indigenous music form from Togo in West Africa, is characterized by its collective calls and responses and pulsating rhythms called Saw Cave. Dating back to Togo’s ancestral past, changa tounde’s musical roots are shaped around different aspects of Togolese spiritual beliefs, incarnating the forces of nature and their essential roles in daily life.

The tompinet, a small single-stringed instrument, and calabash drums accompany the lively vocal interplay - creating a hypnotizing atmosphere packed with pounding drumming, gregarious chants, and melodic interludes. Imbued with warm, soul-stirring vocals, changa tounde dances energetically throughout villages, prayer spaces, and various festive occasions.

4. Gabber (Netherlands)

A hardcore timebomb that took the underground scene by storm in the early 1990s, gabber emerged from the unlikely streets of the Netherlands. The underground genre seeps with intense sub-bass frequencies, kick drums, and razor-sharp, machine-gun-like rhythms.

Embracing explicit use of amplified kick drums, gabber can be compared to its faster and more chaotic cousin speedcore. Gabber’s gruff, growling vocals, known as “schreeen” within the scene, amplifies the emotional intensity that fuels the crowd’s adrenaline-fuelled dancing binges. Despite initial controversy and legal restrictions on its production and reproduction, gabber’s rebellious spirit has only gained momentum over the years.

5. Sufi Whirling Dervishes (Middle Eastern)

Calling upon the mysticism and spiritualism embedded deeply in Sufi Islam, the Whirling Dervishes of the Middle East offer a transcendent dance experience known as Sema. This meditative form of dance hails back to the 13th-century whirling mystic and poet Rumi, who believed in dancing as the physical manifestation of reaching ecstatic union with the divine.

Incorporating the practice of spinning, Sema captivates an audience by showcasing the dervishes’ simplicity, oneness, and spiritual connection to God. The dervishes don their iconic white skirts while they whirl around with their arms outstretched, creating a visual feast to enthrall those fascinated by the art.

Conclusion

The diverse and colorful world of dance music never fails to surprise us. From Japan’s ancient basket sekki to the intense beats of gabber, these hidden dance genres give us a fresh and unique perspective of the dance music spectrum. By exploring their origins and intricate compositions, we get a richer insight into the world of dance music and the unity in our diversity.

While mainstream genres have their undeniable charm, diving into the uncharted territories of less explored genres will leave you with an unforgettably diverse dance experience, eager to further enrich your knowledge of global music heritage. So, next time you hit the club, remember there is more to discover beyond the usual groove!

The Most Interesting Asteroids and Comets to Watch


The universe has always been a source of intrigue and fascination for humanity, and celestial objects like asteroids and comets only amplify that curiosity. These rocky wanderers and icy travelers serve as time capsules, preserving clues about the origins of our solar system. From dazzling cometary displays to asteroids harboring secrets of extraterrestrial water, these small bodies have stories to tell—stories woven into the vast cosmos. Some of these objects are particularly captivating, both because of their scientific significance and their ability to connect us with the broader universe.

Comet Halley – The Iconic Messenger

Comet Halley, officially known as 1P/Halley, is arguably one of the most famous comets ever recorded. Making its presence known to the naked eye approximately every 76 years, Comet Halley has been observed for over two millennia, with historical accounts dating back to ancient Chinese, Babylonian, and medieval European chronicles. It last visited Earth’s inner neighborhood in 1986, captivating astronomers and stargazers alike.

Why is Halley so interesting beyond its fame? It holds the distinction of being a “periodic comet” with a well-documented orbit. This repeatability makes it a valuable research subject. Early spacecraft missions, such as the Giotto mission by the European Space Agency, sent back vivid images of Halley’s nucleus in the 1980s, shedding light on its volatile nature. As 2061 approaches, the anticipation for Halley’s return builds yet again, reminding us of its enduring allure.

Bennu and Ryugu – Time Capsules in Space

Asteroids like Bennu and Ryugu push the boundaries of scientific exploration, offering an unprecedented chance to study primordial material from the early solar system. These near-Earth asteroids have garnered attention largely due to space missions that have closely studied—and even sampled—them.

Bennu, a roughly half-kilometer-wide asteroid, became globally recognized thanks to NASA’s OSIRIS-REx mission. By collecting surface samples, researchers aimed to better understand the composition of carbon-rich asteroids, which may hold organic molecules and water-bearing minerals. Such findings could illuminate the processes of planetary formation and the origins of life. Bennu also stands out because of its high probability of passing near Earth, with a 1-in-2,700 chance of impacting the planet sometime in the 22nd century—a statistic that keeps it under close observation.

Similarly intriguing is Ryugu, another near-Earth object visited by Japan’s Hayabusa2 spacecraft. This mission successfully returned samples to Earth, revealing a wealth of scientific data, including evidence of hydrated minerals. Ryugu’s dark and rocky surface appears ancient and holds potential clues about the early solar system. Together, Bennu and Ryugu represent humanity’s bold quest to reach out and touch the past.

Comet NEOWISE – A Recent Visitor

For many amateur and professional astronomers, Comet NEOWISE (C/2020 F3) was the celestial event of 2020. Discovered by NASA’s NEOWISE space telescope, this comet became visible to the naked eye and inspired awe across the globe. Its bright coma, majestic tail, and the novelty of a spectacular comet in modern times made it unforgettable.

With a reported nucleus diameter of about 5 kilometers, NEOWISE left scientists in awe over its intact, icy core. What truly set it apart was its unexpectedly vivid display, visible from both hemispheres, which captivated millions during a period when many around the world sought moments of hope and wonder. Its long trajectory means it won’t return for nearly 7,000 years—a bittersweet reminder of the finite opportunities to experience such interstellar phenomena.

The Tunguska Event Mystery – Asteroid or Comet?

On June 30, 1908, a remote region near the Tunguska River in Siberia experienced a massive explosion that flattened over 2,000 square kilometers of forest. Without leaving behind an impact crater, it became one of the most famous and baffling cosmic events in modern history. The widely accepted theory is that it was caused by an asteroid or comet fragment exploding in Earth’s atmosphere.

While the exact nature of the Tunguska object remains uncertain, its significance lies in its demonstration of the power such celestial bodies can unleash, even without direct impact. It serves as a reminder of the necessity of monitoring smaller asteroids and the importance of planetary defense systems designed to mitigate potential hazards.

Oumuamua – A Visitor from Beyond

In 2017, astronomers were startled by the discovery of a cigar-shaped object hurtling through the solar system. Named Oumuamua—which means “scout” or “messenger” in Hawaiian—it was the first known interstellar object detected passing through our cosmic neighborhood. Unlike the vast majority of asteroids and comets, which are native to the solar system, Oumuamua originated from another star.

This mysterious object defied expectations. Its elongated shape, unusual trajectory, and lack of a typical cometary tail sparked debates about its true nature. Some speculated it might even be a fragment of alien technology, but scientific consensus leans toward its classification as a peculiar interstellar asteroid or planetary shard. Its brief visit inspired worldwide fascination and highlighted how little we know about objects beyond our solar system.

Comet Encke – The Meteor Shower Artist

If you’ve ever marveled at a meteor shower, there’s a chance it had ties to Comet Encke. With the shortest orbital period of any major comet—about 3.3 years—Comet Encke has visited Earth’s neighborhood countless times throughout history. It plays a key role in producing meteor showers, such as the Taurids, due to the debris it leaves in its wake as it orbits the Sun.

What makes Encke unique is its resilience. This comet has gradually lost mass over millennia, yet it continues to brighten the skies on its periodic journey. Its fragments serve as a humbling reminder of time’s passage and the transformative forces at play in the vastness of space.

Apophis – The Asteroid that Sparks Debate

Asteroid Apophis made headlines in the early 2000s for what seemed like a significant collision risk with Earth in 2029. Since then, advancements in tracking and orbital calculation have ruled out such a scenario, but the asteroid remains a point of fascination.

With a diameter of approximately 340 meters, Apophis passes close enough to Earth to allow detailed observation and open discussions on planetary defense strategies, should a collision ever become imminent. Its “close shave” in 2029, where it will come within roughly 31,000 kilometers of our planet, will make it one of the closest asteroids to skim past Earth in recorded history. For scientists, it will be an unprecedented opportunity to study such a body up close—without launching a mission.

Comet 67P – A Milestone of Exploration

Another household name among celestial enthusiasts is Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko, famously visited by the Rosetta mission in 2014. The European Space Agency made history by not only orbiting the comet but also deploying a lander, Philae, onto its icy surface. While the landing itself faced challenges, the mission revealed fascinating details about the comet’s composition.

Scientists found organic compounds, including complex hydrocarbons, stirring new enthusiasm about the possibility that comets delivered the building blocks of life to early Earth. Comet 67P’s bizarre double-lobed shape and dramatic jets of vaporized material make it an iconic testament to the rugged beauty of comets.

Galactic Travelers that Captivate

Whether asteroids silently gliding past Earth or comets painting the night sky with brilliant tails, these cosmic objects remind us of the solar system’s dynamism. They compel us to look outward, to consider the small but meaningful role we play in the grand tapestry of the universe. Through science, exploration, and wonder, humanity continues to connect with these celestial visitors, unraveling their secrets and embracing their timeless stories. Whatever the sky brings next, you can be sure there’s more to discover among the stars.

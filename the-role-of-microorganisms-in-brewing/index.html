The Role of Microorganisms in Brewing


Microorganisms play a crucial role in the brewing process, contributing to the flavor, aroma, and overall quality of beer. While many people associate brewing with yeast, there are several types of microorganisms involved in different stages of fermentation. In this article, we will explore the diverse world of microorganisms and their impact on brewing.

Yeast: The Mighty Fermenters

Yeast is the superstar of brewing. It is responsible for fermentation, the process that converts sugars into alcohol and carbon dioxide. There are two main types of yeast used in brewing: ale yeast and lager yeast.

Ale yeast, also known as Saccharomyces cerevisiae, ferments beer at warmer temperatures, typically between 15-24°C (59-75°F). It produces a wide range of flavors, including fruity, spicy, and estery notes. Ale yeast is commonly used in ales, stouts, and wheat beers.

Lager yeast, on the other hand, prefers cooler temperatures, around 7-13°C (45-55°F). It ferments beer more slowly and produces fewer fruity flavors. Lager yeast is used in the production of lagers and pilsners, resulting in clean, crisp, and smooth-tasting beers.

Bacteria: Friend or Foe?

While yeast is the main protagonist in brewing, certain bacteria can also influence the beer’s flavor profile. Some bacteria strains, such as Lactobacillus and Pediococcus, contribute to the production of sour beers.

Sour beers are known for their tart and acidic taste, often accompanied by fruity or funky flavors. These bacteria produce lactic acid, which gives sour beers their characteristic tanginess. Brewers intentionally introduce these bacteria during fermentation or aging to create unique and complex flavors.

However, not all bacteria are welcomed in the brewing process. Contamination by unwanted bacteria can lead to off-flavors, spoilage, and even batch loss. Brewers employ strict sanitation practices to ensure that only the desired microorganisms are present in the brewing environment.

Wild Yeast: A Wildcard in Brewing

In addition to cultivated yeast strains, wild yeast can also play a role in brewing. Wild yeast refers to yeast strains found naturally in the environment, such as on fruit skins or in the air. These yeasts can introduce unpredictable flavors and aromas, adding an element of surprise to the brewing process.

Some brewers embrace wild yeast by using spontaneous fermentation, a technique commonly employed in traditional Belgian lambic beers. During spontaneous fermentation, wort (the liquid extracted from malted grains) is exposed to the open air, allowing wild yeast and bacteria to inoculate the beer. This process results in complex, funky, and often sour beers.

Wrapping Up

Microorganisms, particularly yeast and bacteria, have a significant impact on the brewing process. They contribute to the diverse range of flavors and aromas found in different beer styles. From the fruity esters produced by ale yeast to the tartness of sour beers, microorganisms add depth and complexity to our favorite beverages.

Next time you raise a glass of beer, take a moment to appreciate the microscopic organisms that made it possible. Cheers to the fascinating world of microorganisms in brewing!

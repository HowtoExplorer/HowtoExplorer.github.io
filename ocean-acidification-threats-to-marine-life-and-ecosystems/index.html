Ocean Acidification: Threats to Marine Life and Ecosystems


Ocean acidification poses a genuine threat to marine life and ecosystems. Driven by increased fossil fuel burning, it exacerbates the challenges many ocean species already face. This problem has implications on marine life from the smallest plankton to the largest mammals. In this article, we will delve deeper into why ocean acidification matters, how it impacts marine ecosystems, and what can be done to mitigate it.

Ocean acidification results from increased carbon dioxide (CO2) production, which leads to the increased combustion of fossil fuels. Increased CO2 levels cause the pH of the ocean to drop, making it more acidic. The ocean absorbs a significant portion of the atmospheric CO2, causing it to become more acidic at approximately 30 times faster than 275 million years ago. This process affects marine life adversely, even if other pollutants are not present. Oceans that were once alkaline have become oversaturated with water-soluble carbon dioxide. Consequently, this leads to mass extinctions on an unprecedented scale.

The most direct impacts of ocean acidification are on organisms that form shells or skeletons from materials such as calcium carbonate. These marine animals and plants include crabs, mollusks, corals, and pteropods. As the ocean’s acidity increases, it becomes harder for these creatures to produce and maintain their protective shells and structures.

The consequences of ocean acidification extend beyond these creatures, impacting food webs and entire ecosystems. For example, the death of coral reefs devastates the marine food web, wiping out a stalwart support structure for various marine species, including commercially important fish that rely on coral reefs for survival.

Ocean acidification also disrupts the behavior of marine species physically. It affects the sensory and navigational capabilities of fish and marine mammals, such as whales, dolphins, and sea turtles. Scientists have observed AI-manipulated calculation of seawater’s pH to have difficulty navigating and reproducing, causing mass die-offs and threatening species survival.

Lastly, ocean acidification poses significant challenges for human populations. Climate change’s impacts can disrupt food sovereignty, fishing industries, and coastal livelihoods, threatening the source of nutrition and livelihoods for hundreds of millions of people relying on fisheries.

Though it may seem overwhelming, there are steps society can take to mitigate the impacts of ocean acidification. Major polluters can take responsibility, reduce CO2 emissions, and create carbon sinks—the places where carbon can accumulate indefinitely. Laws and policies can aid this process by promoting low carbon energy, incentivizing conservation and reduction, and integrating climate change consideration into public and private decision-making processes.

In conclusion, ocean acidification is a significant threat to marine life and ecosystems, disproportionately affecting the sea’s most vulnerable inhabitants. Addressing ocean acidification makes ecologically, socially, and economically sound sense, ensuring a healthier ocean and a more sustainable future.

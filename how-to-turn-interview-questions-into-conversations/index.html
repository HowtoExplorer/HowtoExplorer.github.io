How to Turn Interview Questions into Conversations


When preparing for a job interview, it's common to focus on researching the company, practicing common interview questions, and perfecting your responses. However, sometimes the questions asked during an interview can leave you feeling like you're stuck in a narrow, predictable conversation. In reality, interviews are not just about verifying a candidate's qualifications or experience; they are a chance for both the company and the individual to learn, discover, and consider compatibility. This is precisely why we need to shift our focus from simply answering questions to actually engaging in conversations. What's the difference between an interview and a conversation? The difference between the two lies in the way the conversation unfolds and the quality of information exchanged. Interviews typically revolve around a set of preset questions, focusing on the candidate's background, skills, and accomplishments. These questions are often designed to test the candidate's expertise but can leave the conversation feeling one-sided. Conversational interviews, on the other hand, go beyond these preconceived questions. They delve into what makes a candidate unique, what their future goals are, and how they plan to contribute to the organization. Effective conversations like these not only verify the candidate's fit but also give insight into their thought process, their ability to think beyond their immediate role, and their vision for the future. Turning Interviews into Conversations: Tips and Strategies To make your interview conversations stand out, follow these steps: Show genuine interest It's essential to demonstrate a genuine interest in the company and the role you've applied for. This isn't about pretending; it's about bringing a sense of authenticity to your responses. When the interviewer senses truly how invested you are in the company, it opens up the doors to conversations that are mutually beneficial. Ask thoughtful questions Don't wait for questions to come your way - be proactive and ask thoughtful questions in return. These can range from asking about the company culture, current challenges, or future plans. The goal is not to ask the questions you've found on the company's website (though that can be part of it); instead, aim for questions that hit on the unique elements you've discovered during your research. Tell stories Use storytelling to bring your answers to life. When sharing experiences, explain the situation, provide a critical moment, the solution you arrived at, and what you learned from it. This approach not only makes your answers more compelling but also gives the interviewer an opportunity to see your leadership skills in action. Be open to change Remaining flexible can go a long way in changing the direction of the conversation from formulaic to open-ended. If the interviewer asks a question, but you sense they are looking for something different, don't be afraid to ask clarifying questions to get back on track. The goal is to understand their concerns, requirements, and vision. Steering the Conversation In an ideal world, the interviewer and the candidate would navigate the conversation together, each one adding value to the discussion. However, since that's not always the case, implement these strategies to steer the conversation in the direction you desire: Reflective listening Practice reflective listening by paraphrasing back what the interviewer says. This sends strong signals that you're both willing to engage in a meaningful conversation. Lead questions Using open-ended questions isn't enough; you also need to use your words to guide the conversation towards the aspects you believe are crucial. Do this by highlighting certain points or making a link between the current discussion and how it affects your approach. Self-Assessment: Are Your Interviews Setting Up for Success? Before entering any discussion about interview strategies, it's wise to do some soul-searching about whether you're naturally apt for this style. Assess your experiences so far and approach this upcoming interview with a mindset that isn't just about delivering right answers but opening up space for true dialogue.

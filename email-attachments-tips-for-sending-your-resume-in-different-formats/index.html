Email Attachments: Tips for Sending Your Resume in Different Formats


Sending your resume by email might seem like a simple task, but the choice of file format and how you send it can have a huge impact on whether your resume gets noticed or ends up in the spam folder. With more emphasis on digital communication, you need to be aware of the best practices in sending email attachments. In this blog post, we’ll arm you with insider tips and tricks to successfully send your resume in different formats.

Choosing the Right Format

When picking a file format for your resume, consider the compatibility, compression, and readability factors. The two most predominant formats are PDF and Microsoft Word (.doc/.docx).

PDF

If you want your resume to retain its formatting across all platforms, a PDF file is your best bet. This type of file preserves the layout and appearance of your resume, regardless of the device or software it is viewed on. Plus, it doesn’t let recipients edit your resume easily, which is typically what you want to avoid unintended changes.

Microsoft Word (.doc/.docx)

Word documents are the most common format and are easily editable. The downside is that they can change appearance when opened in different versions of Word or on different operating systems. So unless you trust the applicant tracking system (ATS) to keep your formatting intact, this might not be the best choice.

Some people also use .rtf (Rich Text Format) because of its compatibility across many word processors. However, it doesn’t generally retain as many formatting features as PDF or .docx.

Once you’ve chosen the format, here’s how you can increase the chances your resume lands in the ‘Accepted’ pile.

Preparing Your Email

Before attaching your resume, pay attention to the details. Here’s how you can improve your email send out strategy:

Personalization is Key

While it can be time-consuming, tailoring your email to each potential employer can dramatically increase your chances of getting noticed.

Simplify Your Email

Keep the body of the email concise, clear and professional. Stick to a simple subject line that includes the job title and your name. For example, ‘[Marketing Position] - Jane Doe Application’.

Don’t Forget a Cover Letter

An accompanying cover letter can help your resume stand out. It’s a great place to discuss why you’re interested in the position and why you’re the right person for it.

Sending the Email

Email Size Limit

Be mindful of email size limits. Large attachments can be problematic for both the sender and recipient. If your file is too big, try a compression tool like .zip or a cloud storage platform like Google Drive or Dropbox to share with the recipient via a link.

Attachments and Email Clients

Ensure your resume is renamed descriptively for easy reference, and add it to the email. Lastly, check compatibility - some older email clients can’t open newer file formats.

Test Your Email

Before hitting send, check your email on different devices and email clients to ensure everything appears as intended.

Post-Send Follow-Up

Even after you’ve clicked send, there are steps you can take to further improve your odds of success:

Thank You Note

Send a follow-up email to thank the recipient for their time and consideration. This not only keeps you on the potential employer’s radar but also demonstrates good manners and professionalism.

Be Responsive

Keep an eye on your email for potential follow-up questions or inquiries from the employer. Answer promptly and thoroughly to show your interest and availability.

In conclusion, the selection of file format, structure of your email, and your follow-up strategy all play a crucial role. Offering a well-presented, clean, and optimised resume can make all the difference. As you send out your tailored resume and look forward to your next interview, remember – these seemingly small details can set you apart from other candidates and take you one step closer to your dream job. Happy job hunting!

Literary Criticism: Different Approaches Explained


Literary criticism, a practice that probes deeply into the foundations, structures, and implicit meanings of literary works, is a multifaceted art. It involves not just critique but an in-depth understanding of the intertwining thereof of literature, its contexts, history, and realities. Literary criticism approaches do not seek to arrive at a definitive interpretation, but rather to explore diverse facets of a work, giving room to the complexity and richness inherent in any piece of literature. Structuralist Marxism: Combining Structure and Class Offered by Pierre Macherey, this approach particularly focuses on how literary texts both express and repress the social conditions and conflicts of the time in which they were created. Here, structure and ideology receive equal attention, emphasizing how structure may both reflect and shape ideology. It goes beyond interpreting a work merely as a reflection of reality, diving deeper into how its narrative structure, character development, and thematic motifs are linked to, yet also mask, social and class realities. This perspective thus highlights the dialectical relationship between structure and ideology in any literary work. Deconstructionism: Unpacking Ambiguity and Ignoring Ansers Led by the works of Jacques Derrida, deconstruction emphasizes the deliberate ambiguity, paradox, or contradiction in literary texts as their truth. Deconstructionism discards the traditional dichotomy of significance and invisibility, suggesting that ambiguity in narrative, plots, and characters is not mistakes, but a synthesis. By arguing against the idea of a singular 'authorial' interpretation and instead embracing multiplicity and ambiguity, readers are invited to leave traditional notions of meaning behind.

How to Create a Reading Challenge for Yourself


Setting up a reading challenge for yourself can be one of the most rewarding ways to fuel your passion for books. Whether you’re trying to read more, expand your literary horizons, or create a meaningful goal for the year, a personalized reading challenge can provide structure and motivation. With a bit of planning, you can craft a challenge that pushes boundaries and strengthens your relationship with reading. Here’s how you can do it.

Define Your Purpose

Before jumping into the specifics of your reading challenge, it’s essential to define your “why.” Understanding your motivation will make the process more personal and enjoyable. Ask yourself questions such as:

  • Are you trying to read more books this year?
  • Do you want to explore different literary genres?
  • Is your goal to focus on classics, contemporary works, or diverse authors?
  • Are you looking to improve your knowledge or expand your perspective on certain topics?

Your “why” will become your guiding anchor, shaping the challenge and keeping you motivated throughout the journey.

Decide on a Target

Next, determine a realistic but slightly ambitious goal. The target could be the number of books you want to read over a specific period, such as a month, quarter, or year. Alternatively, it could revolve around the types of books you want to include in your reading list—for instance, 10 historical fiction novels, 5 self-help books, or 12 books by authors from different countries.

When setting this target, remember to be flexible. It’s important to challenge yourself, but your goals should also be attainable considering your schedule, energy, and reading speed.

Choose a Theme

A theme can make your reading challenge more engaging and help narrow down your book list. Think of themes that align with your interests, values, or curiosities. Here are some ideas for themed challenges:

  • Genre-Based: Explore sci-fi, memoirs, graphic novels, or poetry for a month.
  • Regional Focus: Dive into literature from a specific country or culture.
  • Time Periods: Read Victorian novels, 21st-century releases, or historical accounts from particular eras.
  • Author Spotlights: Focus on works by one author or discover books by underrated writers.

Choosing a theme gives your reading a sense of purpose and enables you to dive deeper into areas of literature that you may not have explored before.

Mix it Up with Sub-Challenges

Adding sub-challenges within your main challenge can prevent monotony and keep your motivation high. You can mix up your challenge by adding smaller objectives, such as:

  • Reading a book recommended by a friend or family member.
  • Tackling a book that has been on your to-be-read (TBR) list for years.
  • Picking up a bestseller or award-winning title.
  • Reading a book in a format you don’t usually use, such as audiobooks or e-books.

These smaller, varied goals make the challenge dynamic while allowing you to appreciate books in multiple ways.

Craft a Balanced TBR List

Your “to be read” list will serve as the foundation of your reading challenge. Here’s how to carefully curate it:

  1. Incorporate Variety: Don’t stick to one type of book. Include novels, novellas, non-fiction, short stories, and more.
  2. Explore Diverse Voices: Make space for writers from different cultural, ethnic, and linguistic backgrounds.
  3. Look for Recommendations: Use platforms like Goodreads, blog posts, and social media to discover highly-rated books.
  4. Balance Fun and Challenge: Include lighthearted reads alongside more challenging books to maintain a balance across your list.

Remember, the goal is to keep your list enjoyable while aligning with the theme or objectives of your challenge. Don’t overwhelm yourself with too many dense or lengthy books, especially if reading time is limited.

Set a Timeline

A timeline provides structure and prevents procrastination. Break down your larger goal into smaller, measurable milestones:

  • Set monthly targets, such as reading 3 books each month.
  • Dedicate specific time slots for reading, such as 30 minutes every evening or during your commute.
  • If you’re reading a particularly hefty book, give yourself interim deadlines, like finishing a certain percentage by the end of the week.

Consistency is key to meeting your target without feeling rushed. Treat your timeline as a flexible guideline rather than a rigid mandate to avoid undue pressure.

Track Your Progress

Keeping track of what you’ve read is not only motivating but also satisfying. Use a method that works best for you:

  • Journaling: Write brief summaries or important takeaways after completing each book.
  • Digital Tools: Use apps like Goodreads or StoryGraph to log finished books, rate them, and even discover your reading trends.
  • Vision Boards: Create a visual representation of your accomplishments with titles or images of the books you’ve read so far.

Regularly revisiting your progress can give you a sense of achievement and encourage you to keep going.

Stay Accountable

Accountability can make a significant difference in successfully completing your reading challenge. Here are a few ways to cultivate accountability:

  • Share Your Goals: Tell your friends, family, or social media followers about your reading challenge.
  • Join a Community: Participate in online book clubs, forums, or hashtags related to reading challenges.
  • Buddy Up: Set joint reading goals with a friend, which you can discuss weekly or monthly.

Having external accountability creates extra motivation, especially on days when you feel less inclined to pick up a book.

Experiment with Creative Formats

To make your reading challenge exciting and diverse, embrace various formats:

  • Read-Alongs: Organize or join groups that read the same book and discuss it together.
  • Interactive Challenges: Combine your love of books with creative platforms like Instagram (bookstagram challenges) or YouTube (reading vlog challenges).
  • Themed Months: Assign specific months to distinct themes. For example, dedicate February to Black History Month reads or October to spooky thrillers.

These formats offer variety and foster engagement, ensuring your challenge doesn’t lose momentum.

Don’t Be Afraid to Adjust

It’s important to remember that your reading challenge is for your personal growth and enjoyment. If something isn’t working, adjust your goal or shift the direction of your challenge. If you find yourself struggling with one book, it’s okay to put it down and move on to something that inspires you. Flexibility is key to sustaining your passion for reading.

Celebrate Achievements

Every milestone matters, so don’t forget to celebrate along the way! Whether you’ve finished a tough book, reached a monthly reading target, or crossed the finish line of your challenge, acknowledge every success. Rewards like treating yourself to a new book, a special outing, or even quiet time with your favorite drink can make the journey even more rewarding.

Reflect on the Process

At the end of the challenge, take some time to reflect on your experience. Ask yourself:

  • Did I achieve my initial goals?
  • Which books left the biggest impression on me?
  • How has this challenge influenced my reading habits or perspective?

Use these reflections to prepare for your next challenge. By continuing to evolve as a reader, you’ll find endless opportunities for growth and enjoyment.

Creating a reading challenge for yourself is not just about organizing books to tick off a list. It’s about self-discovery, exploration, and enrichment. Whether you set a modest or ambitious target, the key is to enjoy the journey and discover the magic that only reading can offer.

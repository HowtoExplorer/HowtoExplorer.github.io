Navigating the Intricacies of Postmodern Dance on Stage


Postmodern dance emerged in the mid-20th century as a response to the conventional norms of traditional dance. This artistic movement sought to break free from the constraints of rigid techniques, aesthetic rules, and formal patterns. Instead, it encouraged experimentation, improvisation, and the blending of various dance styles, resulting in a wide array of unique and intriguing performances. Navigating the intricacies of postmodern dance on stage requires not only a deep understanding of the history and evolution of the art form but also an appreciation for its innovative and groundbreaking qualities.

Breaking the Mold: Elements of Postmodern Dance

Postmodern dance stands out for its departure from the structured and rule-based nature of classical ballet. It encompasses various aspects, such as:

  1. Absence of Clear Choreography: Traditional dance routines come with fixed choreographies, while postmodern dance allows for improvisation, spontaneity, and shared decision-making between dancers.
  2. Integration of Various Dance Styles: Postmodern dance encourages the fusion of different dance forms, ranging from contemporary to modern, folk, and even experimental styles.
  3. Emphasis on Conceptual Art: The spectacle of the performance is secondary to the artistic idea that underpins it. Postmodern dance is heavily influenced by conceptual art, which prioritizes the concept or idea over the final product.
  4. Silence and Sound: Just as postmodern art values silence as an essential component, postmodern dance frequently utilizes the absence of music or the juxtaposition of sound to challenge conventional notions of movement and rhythm.
  5. Inclusion of Contact Improvisation: A defining feature of postmodern dance is contact improvisation, where dancers rely on physical contact and continuous interaction to create movements.
  6. Postmodern Dance’s Visual Language: Dancers in postmodern performances often explore non-traditional movements, such as slacking, stretching, tilting, rolling, and falling, along with unconventional ways of using their bodies.

Navigating these elements demands an understanding of how they contribute to the unique identity of postmodern dance.

Integrating the Audience: Postmodern Dance and Mis-en-scène

Postmodern dance often seeks to involve and actively engage the audience in the performance. A well-executed mis-en-scène (French for “putting-in-space”) refers to the overall visual and spatial impact of everything on stage – from the dancers’ movements to the lighting, props, and set design. Key aspects include:

  1. Scenic Design: Postmodern dance encourages experimentation with sets and backdrops in ways that defy conventional norms. Designers and choreographers often work together to create captivating and immersive experiences on stage.
  2. Lighting and Costume: Lighting and costume play a crucial role in reinforcing the conceptual ideas of postmodern dance performances. A carefully orchestrated and thought-provoking combination of these elements can completely alter the audience’s perception of the performance.
  3. Use of Technology: The avant-garde nature of postmodern dance has paved the way for the integration of technology in performances. From interactive installations to projections, technology can enhance the audience’s experience and reshape the perception of the art form.

A good postmodern dance performance must blur the lines between performer and audience, allowing the spectators to engage with the narratives on stage and create an intimate connection with the performers.

Captivating the Critics: The Evolution of Postmodern Dance Criticism

Postmodern dance’s unconventional nature has led to unique approaches to criticism and evaluation. Critics are encouraged to:

  1. Go Beyond the Physical Aspect: The physicality of postmodern dance is only part of the picture. Critics must delve into the layers of meaning, context, and conceptual art to grasp the essence of the performance.
  2. Adopt a Multiplicity of Viewing Positions: Postmodern dance challenges the objective distance a traditional dance reviewer may take. Instead, critics must be open to multiple perspectives and viewpoints to understand the diverse meaning viewers may derive from the same performance.
  3. Incorporate Cultural Analysis: Postmodern dance loves to blur the boundaries of cultural norms. The performances often provoke thought on social and political issues like race, sexuality, and gender. A comprehensive evaluation of a postmodern dance performance should encompass these cultural dimensions.

Conclusion

Postmodern dance remains a powerful medium for artistic exploration and self-expression. Its fluidity, experimentation, and unique visual language have inspired generations of dancers and choreographers worldwide. By understanding its intricacies, navigating the complexities of postmodern dance on stage, and embracing the symbiotic relationship between performer and audience, postmodern dance continues to push the boundaries of what dance can be. And, as attentive viewers, critics play a crucial role in the exploration and documentation of this enigmatic art form.

The Scandalous Secrets of Royal Hygiene: Excesses and Eccentricities of the Rich and Famous


From the lavish courts of Europe to the splendid palaces of Asia, the royal households of the past have been steeped in opulence, extravagance, and unpredictability. But one aspect that often remains hidden from the annals of history books is the intimate lives of the privileged few, particularly when it comes to their personal hygiene practices. Today we are going to delve into the outrageous and bizarre habits of the rich and famous, with a particular focus on the royals.

Firstly, it is crucial to note that cleanliness was not always a priority for the privileged. As people of wealth, status, and influence, many believed they were above such earthly concerns. In fact, it wasn’t until the Victorian era that regular bathing became a common practice even among the middle class. For members of the nobility, the concept of hygiene was an alien concept.

Take, for example, Marie Antoinette, the last queen of France. She was known to indulge in elaborate baths, filled with rose petals and scented oils, only ever deigning to immerse herself for just a few minutes before retiring to her quarters, with the infamous “Let them eat cake” declaration echoing in her wake.

Elsewhere in Europe, Louis XV of France had a bathtub constructed in the shape of a wooden shell for his mistress. Meanwhile, in Russia, Catherine the Great allegedly ordered her personal physician to build her a giant golden bathtub, a tribute to her own magnificence.

Moving beyond the borders of Europe, we find eccentricities widespread across the globe. In India during the Mughal Empire, bathing was viewed as a rare luxury. Akbar the Great built luxurious bathrooms with marble steps and a separate dressing room while his son Jahangir preferred to bathe in honey.

But perhaps one of the most bizarre hygiene rituals came from Emperor Wu of Han China, who took baths in wine. His preference was a vat of the finest vintage, a peculiar penchant that may have led to a rather unique signature scent.

The obesity crisis didn’t affect the royalty. Their daily diets could make any nutritionist cringe. Austrian Emperor Franz Joseph had a penchant for Sachertorte, while Ptolemy II of Egypt was known for indulging in lavish feasts fit for kings. These diets coupled with their limited bathing routines often led to a rotund and ill appearance. King Louis XIV of France, known for his lengthy reign as the Sun King, was morbidly obese, with his subjects famously dubbing him “the fat + sun.”

The rich and famous also had various products and treatments used to retain their perceived pristine and divine status. Cleopatra’s famed milk baths were a mixture of donkey’s milk and honey, believed to preserve her radiant complexion and strengthen her body. In Japan, the famous geishas used traditional Japanese baths to maintain their flawless beauty, polishing their skin with a mixture of rice and availability for a smooth and flawless finish.

Royalty and eccentric hygiene practices often went hand in hand. From the lavish indulgence in fragrant baths and exotic concoctions to bizarre and sometimes unhealthy habits in their daily regimes, the affluent of yesteryear had a unique approach to personal hygiene, one that often blurred the lines between opulent living and excess.

In conclusion, royal hygiene has evolved significantly over time, shifting from the often shocking and excessive routines of the past to the modern-day obsession with cleanliness and well-being we see today. Though perhaps not the stuff of royal elegance, these outrageous and exotic routines teach us a valuable lesson in the importance of basic hygiene and the pursuit of health for all, regardless of social status.

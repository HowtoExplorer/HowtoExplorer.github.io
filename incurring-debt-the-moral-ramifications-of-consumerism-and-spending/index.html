Incurring Debt: The Moral Ramifications of Consumerism and Spending


Consumerism has seeped into every aspect of modern life, influencing the way we view our selves, our relationships, and our society. With the abundance of readily available credit and the constant barrage of marketing messages driving us to acquire more and more, incurring debt has become almost synonymous with the adult experience. The moral implications of living paycheck-to-paycheck or drowning in debt are profound, touching on our sense of responsibility, humanity, and personal integrity. As we reckon with the moral ramifications of consumerism and spending, we must ask ourselves: how did we arrive here, and what can we do to break free from this cycle?

A Culture of Acquisition

At its core, consumerism is a philosophy that encourages the acquisition of goods and services in ever-increasing amounts. Through relentless advertising, we are molded into consumption machines, constantly fueling the growth of the economy while often putting our financial well-being at risk. Credit cards offer a means for people to manage their finances and build credit, but they also enable individuals and households to incur substantial debt in the pursuit of material possessions.

Advertising campaigns tell us that the key to happiness lies in the latest smartphones, designer clothing, or extravagant vacations. The images and stories we see in commercials, internet ads, and social media feeds create a desire that is nearly impossible to ignore. It is this desire that drives the constant search for more — more possessions, more experiences, and more debt.

Debt and the Moral Self

Living life with a balance of debt is increasingly becoming the norm. Many people begin their working careers with student loan debt, and may also face credit card debt for daily expenses. While occasionally using debt might not have long-term negative consequences, relying on it excessively can blur the line between necessity and want.

When we consistently live beyond our means, we betray our moral self, by choosing immediate gratification over long-term financial stability. Fulfilling our responsibilities to ourselves and our families — owning up to our financial situation and learning to live within our means — is crucial in regaining our sense of moral integrity.

Moral Responsibilities in Modern Society

Our society emphasizes individualism and self-reliance, which can make it difficult for people to openly discuss or confront their financial struggles. It is crucial to democratize the conversation about incurring debt and promote transparency in our attitudes toward consumerism.

Taking control of your finances, setting realistic goals, and making informed decisions are ways to better navigate personal debt. Avoiding drugs, alcohol, and gambling can reduce the risk of accumulating further debt. Finally, it is essential to communicate with lenders, seek professional financial advice, and, if possible, establish an emergency fund to mitigate financial uncertainties.

Finding Balance and Reconnecting with Values

Deeming oneself a “good person” should be intimately linked with personal responsibility and a conscious effort to live within one’s means. Recognizing the difference between needs and wants, constraining impulse buying, and knowing the long-term consequences of taking on credit is vital in creating a stable financial future.

Clearly, incurring debt has multifaceted moral ramifications. Once we understand the connection between our spending patterns and our responsibilities, we can reclaim our power over our finances. With that power comes the ability to cultivate a more balanced life, guided by our values, rather than by incessant consumption, advertising, and the allure of debt. By breaking free from the cycle, we can rediscover personal and financial satisfaction, reducing the burden on our wallets and the insidious impact consumerism has on society.

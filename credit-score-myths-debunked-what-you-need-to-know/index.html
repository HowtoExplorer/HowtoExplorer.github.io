Credit Score Myths Debunked: What You Need to Know


In today’s world, credit scores play a vital role in our financial lives. Whether you’re applying for a mortgage, a car loan, or even renting an apartment, your credit score can make or break the deal. Unfortunately, there are many myths surrounding credit scores that can lead to confusion and misinformation. In this blog post, we will debunk some of the most common credit score myths and provide you with the information you need to make informed financial decisions.

Myth #1: Checking Your Credit Score Lowers It

One of the biggest misconceptions about credit scores is that checking your score will negatively impact it. Fortunately, this is not true. When you check your own credit score, it’s considered a “soft inquiry,” which does not affect your score. However, if a lender or creditor checks your credit score as part of a loan application or credit approval process, it is considered a “hard inquiry,” which can have a slight impact on your score. So go ahead and check your credit score without worrying about damaging it!

Myth #2: Closing Credit Cards Boosts Your Score

Another common myth is that closing credit cards will improve your credit score. While it may seem like closing unused credit cards would be a good idea, it can actually have the opposite effect. Closing a credit card reduces your available credit, which can increase your credit utilization ratio. This ratio compares the amount of credit you’re using to the total credit available to you. A higher credit utilization ratio can negatively impact your score. Instead of closing credit cards, consider keeping them open and using them sparingly to maintain a healthy credit utilization ratio.

Myth #3: Paying Off a Debt Removes It from Your Credit Report

Paying off a debt is an important step in improving your financial health, but it doesn’t mean the debt will disappear from your credit report. Credit reports are a record of your credit history, and they typically include information about your debts for up to seven years. When you pay off a debt, it will be marked as “paid” on your credit report, but it will still be visible to lenders and can impact your credit score. It’s important to continue making timely payments and manage your debts responsibly to maintain a positive credit history.

Myth #4: Income Affects Your Credit Score

Contrary to popular belief, your income does not directly impact your credit score. Your credit score is based on factors such as your payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, and types of credit used. While having a higher income can make it easier to manage your debts and make timely payments, it does not directly affect your credit score. However, some lenders may consider your income when evaluating your creditworthiness for certain types of loans.

Myth #5: Closing Old Accounts Will Improve Your Credit Score

Closing old accounts may seem like a good idea to simplify your financial life, but it can actually harm your credit score. The length of your credit history is an important factor in determining your credit score. Closing old accounts can shorten your credit history and lower your score. Instead, consider keeping old accounts open and using them occasionally to maintain a positive credit history.

Conclusion

Understanding the truth behind credit score myths is crucial for making smart financial decisions. By debunking these common misconceptions, you can take control of your credit score and work towards building a solid financial foundation. Remember to check your credit score regularly, keep old accounts open, and continue making timely payments to maintain a positive credit history. With this knowledge in hand, you’ll be well-equipped to navigate the world of credit scores and achieve your financial goals.

What to Do if an Interview Goes Awry


Imagine you've spent weeks preparing for the most important job interview of your life. You've researched the company's mission, practiced your responses to common questions, and rehearsed your presentation. You walk into the conference room feeling confident and ready to nail the interview. But just as you're about to introduce yourself, the company's CEO walks in late, clearly distracted by his phone. The conversation is awkward from the start, and you can tell the interviewer is in a bad mood. You've been in this situation before, or so you're thinking. What do you do when an interview goes awry? Panicking is not an option. Instead, focus on salvaging what you can from the situation and turning it into a learning experience. Take a Deep Breath and Assess the Situation When things go wrong, the first instinct is to let tension take over. But before you allow panic to set in, take a deep breath and step back. Assess the situation objectively. Ask yourself: What happened to throw the interview off track? Was it an unusual circumstance, or a result of my own actions? Consider the context of the situation. Did the interviewer's tardiness, for example, throw off the momentum of the conversation? Or, did your nervousness throw you off your game, leading to a series of awkward exchanges? Understanding what went wrong is key to recovery. Reframe Your Thinking When you start labeling the situation as "awry" or "disastrous," it creates a negative association. Instead, try to reframe your thinking. Remember that an interview, whether good or bad, is an opportunity to showcase your skills, experience, and personality. You control your reaction and response. Focus on the things you did right during the interview. Even if the situation was chaotic, there must have been moments when you felt confident or articulate. Celebrate those accomplishments and note them down so you can learn from them and improve for future interviews. Re-engage with the Interviewer After taking a moment to reassess and reframe your thinking, it's essential to re-engage with the interviewer. Show them that you're adaptable and can regroup quickly.

Politely acknowledge the delay or unusual circumstance. Re-state your interest in the company and the role, matching the tone and energy of the interviewer. Bring the conversation back on track by posing open-ended questions about the company or position that you hadn't covered yet.

This not only salvages some of the interview's momentum but also demonstrates your resilience and professionalism. Conclude Strongly Regardless of the interview's outcome, it's essential to conclude on a high note. This shows that you're capable of ending on a positive note even when faced with challenges.

Recap your key qualifications and how they fit the role. Express your enthusiasm for the company and your eagerness to contribute. Thank the interviewer for their time and show gratitude for the opportunity.

Learn from the Experience The goal of turning a bad interview into a good one is not just about salvaging the situation but also about learning from it. Think about what you could have done differently, whether it was preparation, your body language, or your responses. Note down your observations and reflect on how they can help you in future interviews.

Identify specific moments where you lost your composure or struggled with a question. Think about how you can improve your response to those situations in the future. Consider updating your interview strategy if necessary.

Don't Let it Get to You An interview that goes awry can leave a lasting impression, making you question your worth and abilities. But the secret to distinguishing between a disaster and an experience is perspective. Try not to take the failure personally. Recognize that an interviewer's mood or behavior is often a reflection of their own stress or pressure, not your qualifications or potential. Maintain a positive outlook and focus on what went wrong so you can improve for future interviews. By applying these strategies, you can turn an interview that's gone awry into a learning experience that steers you on the path to success. Whether you land the job or not, the confidence and poise developed from pivoting an awkward situation will serve you well in both your professional and personal life.

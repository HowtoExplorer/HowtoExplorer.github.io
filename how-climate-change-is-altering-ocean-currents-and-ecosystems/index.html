How Climate Change Is Altering Ocean Currents and Ecosystems


The world's oceans have long been a vital component in regulating the planet's climate. They absorb roughly 25% of the carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere and cover about 70% of the Earth's surface. However, due to the alarming rate of climate change, the delicate balance of the ocean's ecosystems is undergoing a transformation. In recent years, there has been an observable shift in ocean currents, threatening the diverse marine life that inhabits the waters. Ocean conveyor belts or currents are complex systems that distribute heat energy from the equator towards the poles through thermohaline circulation. However, human activities have disrupted these natural processes, leading to faster-than-expected warming trends. A natural temperature difference drive global circulation patterns. As greenland ice melts and enters into the North Atlantic Ocean, it becomes diluted with freshwater and increases the surface density of water in colder regions. This process decreases the density, reducing the formation of low-salinity water which fuels this conveyor belt driving cold water towards the poles. In turn, this leads to reduced upwelling of the warm deep waters. Similarly, these alterations hinder nutrient-rich waters to travel northwards allowing further stress in marine life. The alterations in ocean currents threaten marine ecosystems in numerous ways. Many marine species continue to face unparalleled stress, displacement and extinction due to warming water temperatures, as discovered in various research studies conducted on tropical and polar seas.

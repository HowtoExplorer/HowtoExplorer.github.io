A Beginner's Guide to Existentialism and its Impact on Philosophy


Existentialism is a philosophical movement that emerged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It focuses on the individual’s existence and emphasizes the freedom and responsibility each person has in creating meaning in their own life. In this beginner’s guide, we will explore the key ideas and thinkers of existentialism and delve into its profound impact on philosophy.

What is Existentialism?

At its core, existentialism asserts that existence precedes essence, meaning that individuals exist first and then define their own essence or nature through their actions and choices. This philosophy stands in contrast to essentialism, which posits that individuals have predetermined natures or essences that determine their existence.

Existentialism also emphasizes the subjective experience of human existence. It recognizes the inherent absurdity and meaninglessness of life, but instead of succumbing to despair, it encourages individuals to embrace their freedom and take responsibility for their own lives.

Key Ideas of Existentialism

1. Authenticity

Authenticity is a central concept in existentialism. It refers to living in accordance with one’s true self and values, rather than conforming to societal expectations or norms. Existentialists believe that embracing authenticity leads to a more meaningful and fulfilling life.

2. Angst and Dread

Existentialism acknowledges the existence of angst and dread, which arise from the awareness of one’s freedom and responsibility. The realization that one must make choices without any predetermined guidance can be overwhelming, leading to feelings of anxiety and uncertainty.

3. Existential Freedom

Existentialists emphasize the radical freedom of individuals. They argue that while humans are constrained by external circumstances, they always have the freedom to choose how to respond to those circumstances. This freedom comes with great responsibility, as individuals are solely responsible for the consequences of their choices.

4. Existential Despair

Existential despair arises from the recognition of life’s inherent meaninglessness. However, existentialists believe that by embracing this despair and taking responsibility for creating meaning, individuals can ultimately find purpose and fulfillment.

Influential Existentialist Thinkers

Several philosophers have greatly contributed to the development and popularization of existentialism. Here are a few notable figures:

1. Søren Kierkegaard (1813-1855)

Kierkegaard is often considered the father of existentialism. He explored themes of individuality, subjectivity, and the role of faith in human existence. Kierkegaard emphasized the importance of personal choice and subjective truth.

2. Friedrich Nietzsche (1844-1900)

Nietzsche’s philosophy challenged traditional morality and championed the individual’s will to power. He believed that individuals should create their own values and live authentically, free from societal constraints.

3. Jean-Paul Sartre (1905-1980)

Sartre is one of the most prominent existentialist thinkers. His concept of “existence precedes essence” encapsulates the core idea of existentialism. Sartre also explored the concept of “bad faith,” which refers to individuals denying their freedom and instead conforming to societal expectations.

4. Simone de Beauvoir (1908-1986)

De Beauvoir’s work focused on existential feminism, examining the unique challenges faced by women in a male-dominated society. She emphasized the importance of women embracing their freedom and creating their own identities.

Impact on Philosophy

Existentialism has had a profound impact on philosophy, influencing various fields and thinkers. Its emphasis on individual freedom, responsibility, and authenticity has shaped discussions on ethics, existential psychotherapy, and even literature and art.

Existentialist ideas have also influenced the development of other philosophical movements, such as phenomenology and post-structuralism. The concepts of existentialism continue to resonate with individuals seeking to navigate the complexities of existence and find meaning in a seemingly chaotic world.

Conclusion

Existentialism offers a unique perspective on human existence, emphasizing the freedom and responsibility individuals have in shaping their own lives. By embracing authenticity and taking responsibility for their choices, individuals can find purpose and meaning even in the face of life’s inherent absurdity. The impact of existentialism on philosophy is undeniable, as it continues to shape discussions and inspire individuals to explore the depths of their own existence.

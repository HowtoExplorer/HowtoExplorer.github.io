The Do's and Don'ts of Resume Writing


Creating a standout resume is one of the most important steps in securing your next job. Whether you are applying for your first position or looking to move ahead in your career, your resume serves as your first impression to potential employers. A resume that is well-written, clear, and properly formatted can be the difference between landing an interview or being passed over. In this guide, we’ll explore the essential do’s and don’ts of resume writing to help you craft a document that not only gets noticed but also showcases your skills, experience, and potential effectively.

The Do’s of Resume Writing

1. Do Keep It Concise

Your resume should be a snapshot of your qualifications, experience, and skills. Employers typically spend only a few seconds initially reviewing each resume, so clarity and brevity are crucial. Keep your resume to one or two pages—anything longer might overwhelm the reader and detract from the key points.

Make sure that each word and section adds value, cutting any unnecessary information. Only include relevant experiences, focusing on accomplishments and skills that align with the job you’re applying for.

2. Do Tailor Your Resume for Each Job

One of the most effective ways to stand out is by tailoring your resume for each position. Research the job description carefully and highlight the skills and experiences that match the employer’s needs. Customizing your resume shows that you have put thought and effort into the application process, making it more likely that your resume will get noticed.

For example, if you are applying for a project management position, emphasize your leadership skills, project coordination, and experience with timelines and deadlines. A generic resume is less likely to resonate with hiring managers because it doesn’t speak directly to the unique qualifications they’re seeking.

3. Do Use Action Verbs

To make your achievements stand out, use action verbs such as “led,” “designed,” “managed,” “developed,” “created,” “implemented,” “improved,” and others. These verbs show that you actively contributed to the success of previous projects and roles.

For instance, rather than saying “Responsible for managing a team,” you can say “Led a team of five to achieve project goals ahead of schedule.” Action verbs help to convey a sense of accomplishment and initiative.

4. Do Use a Clean, Professional Format

The design of your resume is just as important as its content. A cluttered, hard-to-read resume can be a huge turn-off for employers. Choose a clean, easy-to-read layout with clear section headings, bullet points, and adequate white space to make the document easy to navigate.

Stick to simple fonts like Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman in a readable size (10 to 12 points). Avoid using too many colors or elaborate graphics unless you’re in a creative field where design matters. In most cases, a traditional and professional format is best.

5. Do Quantify Your Achievements

Whenever possible, quantify your accomplishments. Numbers provide context and demonstrate the scope of your impact. For instance, instead of saying “Improved sales,” you could say “Increased sales by 20% over a six-month period.”

Quantifying your achievements not only highlights your effectiveness but also gives hiring managers concrete evidence of your capabilities.

6. Do Highlight Relevant Skills

Employers are always on the lookout for candidates with the right skill set. Include both hard and soft skills relevant to the job you’re applying for. Hard skills include technical abilities, software proficiency, or certifications, while soft skills might include communication, teamwork, and problem-solving.

For example, if you’re applying for a software development role, highlight your proficiency with specific programming languages, tools, or platforms. For a customer service role, emphasize your communication, problem-solving, and conflict resolution skills.

7. Do Include a Professional Summary

At the top of your resume, consider including a brief professional summary that highlights your key strengths and qualifications. This section provides a snapshot of your skills and experience and gives the reader an overview of what you bring to the table.

Keep your summary concise—three to four sentences should suffice—and make sure it speaks directly to the role you’re applying for. Focus on your achievements, core competencies, and what you can contribute to the company.

8. Do Include Relevant Education and Certifications

Your educational background and any certifications or additional training can significantly impact your candidacy, particularly if you are a recent graduate or transitioning to a new industry. Be sure to list your degrees, certifications, and any specialized training that directly relates to the job you’re seeking.

If you’ve completed any courses, attended workshops, or earned certifications that demonstrate your commitment to professional development, include those as well.

9. Do Proofread Your Resume

Errors and typos on your resume are a quick way to ensure it gets tossed aside. Even a small mistake can leave a negative impression and suggest a lack of attention to detail. Take the time to proofread your resume carefully, checking for grammar, spelling, and punctuation errors.

If possible, have someone else review your resume as well. A fresh set of eyes may catch errors you’ve overlooked.

The Don’ts of Resume Writing

1. Don’t Use Unnecessary Personal Information

Your resume should focus on your professional qualifications and experience. Avoid including personal details like your age, marital status, social security number, or a photo unless specifically requested by the employer. In many cases, personal details are irrelevant and can be seen as unprofessional or intrusive.

Additionally, some of this information can even lead to unconscious bias during the hiring process. It’s better to keep the focus on your skills and experience.

2. Don’t Include Irrelevant Job Experiences

Your resume should focus on roles and experiences that are relevant to the position you’re applying for. Including irrelevant job experiences can make your resume longer than necessary and distract from your most important qualifications.

For example, if you’re applying for a marketing role, including a high school job as a cashier may not add value unless it directly connects to your ability to engage with customers or manage a team. Focus on roles that showcase your relevant skills and accomplishments.

3. Don’t Use a One-Size-Fits-All Resume

A common mistake job seekers make is using the same resume for every application. While it’s tempting to use a generic resume for all positions, this approach doesn’t effectively communicate how your qualifications match the specific needs of the employer.

Tailoring your resume to each position is essential. Even small changes, like emphasizing certain skills or rearranging your experience to match the job description, can make a big difference in whether you get noticed.

4. Don’t Include Too Many Details

While it’s important to highlight your achievements, you also want to avoid overwhelming the reader with excessive details. Your resume is a tool to showcase your strengths, but it should not be cluttered with every minor task or responsibility you’ve ever had.

Focus on the most impactful experiences and provide enough information to show your expertise, but don’t bog down the reader with irrelevant or overly detailed descriptions.

5. Don’t Use Jargon or Buzzwords

While it’s tempting to use industry-specific jargon or buzzwords, these can come across as empty filler if not used carefully. Phrases like “hardworking,” “team player,” or “dynamic” are vague and overused. Instead, focus on demonstrating these qualities through specific examples of your work and accomplishments.

Be sure that your resume is accessible to a broad audience, especially if it might be reviewed by someone who isn’t familiar with your industry-specific language.

6. Don’t Lie or Exaggerate

Honesty is crucial when writing your resume. Misleading information or exaggerated claims may help you get an interview, but they could cost you the job or harm your professional reputation down the line. Always be truthful about your experience, education, and skills.

If there’s something you’re lacking, it’s better to be upfront about it rather than attempting to cover it up with a fabricated skill or experience. Employers value integrity and will appreciate your authenticity.

7. Don’t Use Unprofessional Email Addresses

Your email address should be simple and professional. Avoid using personal or silly email addresses like “partylover1234@gmail.com” or “crazycatlady@hotmail.com.” Create a straightforward email address using your name or initials for a more professional impression.

If you don’t already have a professional-sounding email address, it’s worth taking the time to set one up. A clean, professional email helps create a good first impression.

8. Don’t Use the Same Resume for All Industries

While some skills are transferable between industries, different sectors may require different emphasis in resumes. For example, if you’re switching from a marketing career to a project management role, your resume should emphasize skills like leadership, organization, and cross-functional collaboration rather than marketing-specific achievements.

Adapting your resume for each industry and job type helps position you as a strong candidate by focusing on the qualifications most relevant to the role.

9. Don’t Forget to Include Keywords for Applicant Tracking Systems

Many companies use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) to screen resumes before they even reach a hiring manager. To ensure your resume makes it past these systems, include relevant keywords from the job description in your resume. Use these terms naturally within your experience and skills sections to improve your chances of getting noticed.

Conclusion

Crafting an effective resume requires time, effort, and attention to detail. By following these essential do’s and don’ts, you can create a resume that not only highlights your strengths but also aligns with the needs of the employer. Remember that your resume is a tool designed to help you stand out, so make sure it accurately reflects your skills, experience, and potential. Keep it concise, tailored, and error-free, and you’re on your way to making a lasting impression with recruiters and hiring managers.

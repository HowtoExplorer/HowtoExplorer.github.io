Breaking and Pointe: The Surprising Connection Between Street and Ballet


Ballet and street dance have long been thought of as separate entities, each with their distinct styles, histories, and audiences. One is often seen as elegant and classic, while the other is considered edgy and contemporary. However, the truth is that these two styles of dance have more in common than one might think. In fact, some key elements of street dance have their roots in ballet, and both styles have continually influenced each other throughout history.

In the world of street dance, many have heard of genres such as hip-hop, popping, and locking. However, fewer people know that breaking, the foundation for many of these styles, found its inspiration in ballet. Breaking, also known as breakdancing, emerged in the United States in the 1970s. It’s a fusion of various dance styles including classical dance techniques, but its distinctive style emerged from the African-American and Latin-American communities.

The name “breaking” itself emphasizes the connection to ballet. Breakers move in specific “breaks” which require considerable strength, flexibility and balance, akin to those found in pointe work. In particular, complex movements such as to dying and air flipping resemble ballet’s advanced techniques, except with a mix of acrobatics and rhythmic synchronization.

Another significant aspect, the freezeframes or “top rocking,” borrowed from classical ballet basics. Additionally, the elegant and almost gravity-defying freeze poses that performers hold during a breakdance are reminiscent of the ballet icicles or arabesques. Furthermore, the timing, precision, and musicality which are crucial in both styles, share a common thread.

If we delve deeper into the world of ballet, we can find another intersection with street dance – modern dance. Throughout the 20th century, modern dance became a melting pot where influences from various cultural dance forms, including African-American and Latin-American dances, were absorbed, blended, and reinterpreted. Individuals like Martha Graham and Alvin Ailey looked towards the energy and emotion of street dances to enrich their choreographic work, thus opening up new possibilities for ballet itself.

To provide a perspective from a dancer’s point of view, consider Anna Pavlova, a leading prima ballerina of the early 20th century. She was known for her innovation in merging the classicism of ballet with a more fluid and dynamic style which was inspired by her journeys and exposure to different cultures. Another example is Rudolf Nureyev. Renowned for his earth-shaking leaps and dramatic emotion, his dancing style carried elements of street-like passion and vigor that have influenced generations of ballet dancers.

Moreover, contemporary ballet has blossomed in recent years, showcasing creative choreography and embracing the freedom and originality of street movements. Dance companies such as the Royal Ballet and New York City Ballet have integrated street-inspired moves into their performances. The fusion of ballet and street dance, like that in Akram Khan’s “Dust,” showcases a meeting of the two worlds on stage.

While street and ballet appear to be worlds apart, their connection is undeniable. Their mutual influences and shared technical values speak to the universality of dance. By embracing the relevance that each style holds for the other, all forms of dance continue to evolve and flourish. As barriers begin to dissolve, dancers are free to explore the rich intersections between ballet and street dance, inspiring new choreographers, audiences, and dancers to push boundaries even further.

In conclusion, ballet and street dance are not as distant as one might think. Through sharing techniques, embracing new forms of expression, and continually borrowing from one another, these two dance genres enrich each other, creating a dynamic and ever-evolving dance landscape. Whether at a street corner, in a ballet studio or on a concert stage, dancers create a bridge that fosters an enriching dialogue between the two, unlocking new heights in dance for all to enjoy.

Climate Change and the Built Environment: Strategies for Energy Efficiency


As the world wakes up to the reality of climate change, Buildings and their environ have become a central point in the struggle to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Adaptation of energy efficiency strategies on the built environment potentially has substantial savings for owners while equally substantially reducing our carbon footprint. A series of sweeping actions that ingest behavioral, policy, and technological change can reshape our built environment.

Behavioral change may not immediately catch the eye, but its impact on energy efficiency is tremendous. There is a multitude of change one can achieve, for instance changing thermostat settings, using high-efficiency lighting, adopting HVAC technology, amongst others. These are all attainable rights at the owner’s level. They say a journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step. So, let’s initiate this journey by making simple but powerful behavioral changes in our homes and offices.

Moving onto the policy level, Governments, Municipal bodies, and private-sector entities have a significant role to play in driving systemic change. This translates to energy-efficient building codes, smarter building designs, and the offer of incentives for businesses and homeowners to invest in green technology. Policies that have been proven effective in driving energy efficiency include energy-efficient building codes, rebate programs, and municipal energy benchmarking programs. For instance, the London Energy Transformation Initiative (LETI) incentivizes building owners to retrofit to high energy-performance standards.

At last, let us look at technological change. Technology is the most potent weapon in our arsenal to combat climate change. It manifests in a plethora of ways - smart home systems, energy-efficient building materials, renewable energy systems, amongst others. One unique contemporary advancement is the idea of buildings producing as much energy as they consume or maybe even more. This concept known as “Grid-interactive Buildings,” is now increasingly gaining prominence.

Grid-interactive buildings interact with the grid by utilizing smart sensors and energy management systems to manage their energy consumption and production. The envisionable future builds with such architectural innovation that can buy the time we need to shift to renewables to the extent we needs it.

The building sphere, with the involvement of everyone at different levels - individual building owners to government agencies – has, therefore, the enormous potential to be both a force for economic progress and a tool for reducing carbon emissions. Adopting energy-efficient practices not only cuts operating costs but also offers copious environmental benefits. The time is now, and the hour is ripe to turn the tide.

So, how do we construct a greener, energy-efficient built environment? The answer is a multi-faceted approach that involves policy change, investment in technology, and behavioral change. All stakeholders need to join hands and work together to adopt energy-saving practices. The result of this union will be a built environment that combats climate change and contributes positively to the environment. A lasting legacy of our effort will be a greener and more sustainable world for future generations.

Common Credit Myths Debunked


Understanding credit can be a daunting task, especially with the plethora of myths and misconceptions that surround it. Many individuals find themselves misinformed about how credit works, which can lead to poor financial decisions. In this blog post, we will debunk some of the most common credit myths, providing clarity and insight into the world of credit. By the end, you will have a better understanding of how to manage your credit effectively and make informed financial choices.

Myth 1: Checking Your Credit Score Will Hurt It

One of the most pervasive myths is that checking your own credit score will negatively impact it. This is simply not true. When you check your own credit score, it is considered a “soft inquiry,” which does not affect your credit score. In contrast, when a lender checks your credit as part of a loan application, it is known as a “hard inquiry,” which can have a minor impact on your score.

The Importance of Monitoring Your Credit

Regularly checking your credit score is crucial for several reasons:

  • Identify Errors: Mistakes can occur on your credit report. By monitoring your score, you can catch these errors early and dispute them.
  • Understand Your Financial Health: Knowing your credit score helps you gauge your financial standing and make informed decisions about loans and credit cards.
  • Prevent Identity Theft: Regular checks can help you spot any unauthorized accounts or inquiries, allowing you to take action quickly.

Myth 2: Closing Old Credit Accounts Improves Your Score

Many people believe that closing old credit accounts will improve their credit score. However, this is a misconception. In fact, closing old accounts can actually harm your credit score for several reasons:

  • Credit History Length: A significant factor in your credit score is the length of your credit history. Closing old accounts reduces the average age of your accounts, which can lower your score.
  • Credit Utilization Ratio: This ratio is calculated by dividing your total credit card balances by your total credit limits. Closing an account reduces your total credit limit, which can increase your utilization ratio and negatively impact your score.

Best Practices for Managing Old Accounts

Instead of closing old accounts, consider these strategies:

  • Keep Accounts Open: If there are no annual fees, keep the account open to maintain your credit history.
  • Use Accounts Occasionally: Make small purchases on old accounts to keep them active, but ensure you pay off the balance in full each month.

Myth 3: You Only Have One Credit Score

Another common myth is that there is a single credit score that everyone uses. In reality, there are multiple credit scoring models, and your score can vary depending on the model used. The two most widely recognized scoring models are FICO and VantageScore, but even within these models, there are different versions.

Factors Influencing Your Credit Score

While the specific scoring models may differ, they generally consider similar factors:

  • Payment History: This is the most significant factor, accounting for about 35% of your score. Timely payments boost your score, while late payments can significantly lower it.
  • Credit Utilization: As mentioned earlier, this ratio is crucial. Keeping your utilization below 30% is generally recommended.
  • Length of Credit History: The longer your credit history, the better it is for your score.
  • Types of Credit: A mix of credit types (credit cards, mortgages, installment loans) can positively impact your score.
  • New Credit: Opening multiple new accounts in a short period can be seen as risky behavior and may lower your score.

Myth 4: Paying Off Debt Will Immediately Boost Your Score

While paying off debt is a positive step towards improving your credit score, it may not lead to an immediate boost. The impact of paying off debt can vary based on several factors:

  • Type of Debt: Paying off installment loans (like a car loan) may not have the same immediate effect as paying down credit card debt, which directly affects your credit utilization ratio.
  • Timing of Reporting: Creditors report to credit bureaus at different times. If you pay off a debt but the creditor hasn’t reported it yet, you may not see an immediate change in your score.

Long-Term Strategies for Improving Your Score

To effectively improve your credit score over time, consider these strategies:

  • Make Payments on Time: Consistently paying your bills on time is one of the best ways to improve your score.
  • Reduce Credit Card Balances: Focus on paying down high-interest credit card debt first.
  • Avoid New Hard Inquiries: Limit the number of new credit applications to avoid multiple hard inquiries in a short period.

Myth 5: You Need to Carry a Balance to Build Credit

Many people believe that carrying a balance on their credit cards is necessary to build credit. This is a myth. In fact, carrying a balance can lead to unnecessary interest charges and debt accumulation.

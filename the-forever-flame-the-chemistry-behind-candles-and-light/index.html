The Forever Flame: The Chemistry Behind Candles and Light


Candles have captivated humans since antiquity, serving as symbols of warmth during cold nights and romantic moods during intimate gatherings. Beyond the aesthetic appeal, this age-old phenomenon has roots in some interesting chemistry.

The magic behind the candlelight starts with a simple chemical compound: wax. Wax stores energy released by the burning process and forms the solid structure that we see in candlesticks. Specifically, the most common type of wax is paraffin, made from petroleum byproducts. Like tree resin, paraffin consists of a mixture of hydrocarbons, which burn clean and steadily.

The wick, which serves as the bridge between the flame and the wax, usually consists of a fibrous material such as cotton or wood. In the early stages of burning, the wick heats up, causing the wax near it to melt. The melted wax won’t ignite, but it gets drawn up the wick by capillary action, which is one of nature’s marvels - the way it carries the molten wax up fine fibers. At the tip, the wax meets the heat, vaporizing it into tiny droplets that ignite, contributing to the flame.

However, a typical wick would allow the hot wax vapor to rise freely for an undesired effect: an uneven burn and, more dangerously, gigantic flames. To prevent this, candle manufacturers dope or coat the wick with an adhesive substance like zinc stearate or ceresin. This substance forms a crust on the top of the melted wax, preventing it from prematurely catching fire. The base of the wick itself is also treated with chemicals to slow down the process of wick consumption, ensuring a longer-lasting burn.

The heat generated from the wick causes the wax to convert from a solid state to a gaseous one, and as it reaches the flame’s heat, a combustion reaction takes place. The carbon-hydrogen bonds within the wax break, releasing energy in the form of light and heat. The light generated comes in different colors, depending on the types of hydrocarbon molecules in the wax.

This process creates light that’s highly luminescent but comparatively dim to modern forms of electric lighting. Despite this, candles historically offered practical light sources during times when electric power was unavailable or in short supply.

However, burning a candle in an unsupervised or congested area can be risky, as the heat can ignite nearby materials and lead to fires. Furthermore, prolonged exposure to cigarette-smoke-scented candles has been linked to respiratory distress. That’s why it’s essential to follow safety standards when burning a candle, such as keeping it away from flammable objects, being careful of children or pets, and keeping a proper watch over flames.

In conclusion, the chemistry behind candle and light is not only fascinating but also important. Desirable qualities like steady burning, soft light, and pleasant scents are all a result of clever and purposeful formulations in this seemingly simple phenomenon. So, the next time you light a candle on a romantic evening or use one for spiritual rituals, remember that it is much more than just a source of light—it’s a testament to human ingenuity. Keep experimenting with waxes, scents, and wicks to create that perfect ambiance in your own little world.

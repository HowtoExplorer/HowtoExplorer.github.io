Credit Score Myths Debunked


Credit scores play a significant role in our financial lives. They are used by lenders, landlords, and even potential employers to assess our creditworthiness and make important decisions about us. However, there are several myths surrounding credit scores that can lead to confusion and misinformation. In this blog post, we will debunk some of the most common credit score myths and provide you with accurate information to help you better understand your credit score.

Myth 1: Checking Your Credit Score Will Lower It

One of the most prevalent credit score myths is that checking your credit score will have a negative impact on it. This is simply not true. When you check your own credit score, it is considered a “soft inquiry,” which does not affect your credit score. However, if a lender or creditor checks your credit score as part of a loan application or credit card application, it may result in a “hard inquiry,” which can have a minor impact on your score. So, feel free to monitor your credit score regularly without worrying about any negative consequences.

Myth 2: Closing Unused Credit Cards Improves Your Score

Another common myth is that closing unused credit cards can improve your credit score. In reality, closing unused credit cards can actually harm your credit score. Credit scoring models take into account your credit utilization ratio, which is the amount of credit you use compared to the total credit available to you. By closing unused credit cards, you reduce your available credit, which can increase your credit utilization ratio and negatively impact your score. Instead, consider keeping those unused credit cards open, as long as they do not charge any annual fees.

Myth 3: Having No Debt Means a Perfect Credit Score

Many people believe that having no debt automatically translates into a perfect credit score. While it is true that carrying a high amount of debt can negatively impact your credit score, having no debt does not guarantee a perfect score. Your credit score is based on a variety of factors, including payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, and types of credit. By responsibly managing your credit and making timely payments, you can build a solid credit history, even if you do not have any outstanding debt.

Myth 4: Your Income Affects Your Credit Score

Contrary to popular belief, your income does not directly impact your credit score. Your credit score is a reflection of your credit history and how you manage your debts, not your income level. However, lenders may consider your income when evaluating your creditworthiness for certain types of loans or credit applications. For example, when applying for a mortgage, lenders may assess your income to ensure you have the financial means to repay the loan. But as far as your credit score is concerned, it is primarily determined by your credit history and not your income.

Myth 5: Closing Credit Cards with a Balance Will Improve Your Score

Some people believe that closing credit cards with an outstanding balance will help improve their credit score. Unfortunately, this is not the case. When you close a credit card with a balance, you are still responsible for paying off the debt. Closing the card does not erase the debt or improve your credit score. In fact, it may even have a negative impact on your score, as it can increase your credit utilization ratio. If you have a balance on a credit card that you no longer wish to use, it is better to continue making regular payments until the balance is paid off.

Conclusion

Understanding the truth behind credit score myths is essential for making informed financial decisions. By debunking these common misconceptions, we hope to provide you with a clearer understanding of how credit scores work. Remember, regularly checking your credit score, keeping unused credit cards open, responsibly managing your debts, focusing on your credit history rather than your income, and avoiding closing credit cards with balances are all important factors in improving and maintaining a healthy credit score.

Conservation Genetics: Preserving Genetic Diversity


Genetic variation is crucial to survival and evolution of species. Preservation of this variation has given rise to a new sub-disciplinary field known as Conservation Genetics. This field is concerned with the study of genetic heritage and its influence on the efflux and evolution of species. Understanding the importance of genetic variation in species, the process of losing that variation, and what we can do to conserve this variation is fundamental to the success of preserving life forms at risk.

The primary reason for loss of genetic variation is habitat loss or fragmentation. When habitats shrink or become fragmented, species occupying these areas face drastic alteration of their surroundings. This manifests in form of reduced feeding ground, mating opportunities, and even competitive exclusion by other species. For species of limited range, areas of occupancy may be highly susceptible to disturbance and this often has negative genetic consequences for the species.

What intrigues scientists is the loss of genetic variation doesn’t just impact in the present, it spills over to future generations, destabilizing the net of genetic variety and subsequently jeopardizing the species’ longevity. According to population genetics, the minimal viable population size is an important threshold below which genetic deterioration or speciation is inevitable. Conservation Genetics seeks to circumvent this arbitrary threshold by suggesting mechanisms to assist vulnerable populations.

Some very practical techniques in the realm of Conservation Genetics include Captive Breeding and Gene Banking. Captive breeding involves maintaining a species in an artificial environment for the purpose of promoting genetic continuity when wild population is endangered. For example, the California Condor, once brought to the brink of extinction due to accidental electrocutions and lead ingestion, has been propagated via captive breeding programs. Similarly, Gene Banking stores the genetic matter of an endangered species, providing a tangible genetic backup that can be used for repopulation when required - a bit like a ‘gene-savings’ account.

Conservation Genetics also advises about translocation and reintroduction of species by conducting genetic assessments, which are fundamental to outlining a conservation strategy. Translocations are considered when species are displaced between two suitable habitats. For instance, translocation of Corncrake from overpopulated areas to underrepresented ones. Reintroductions, once extinct or locally extinct species are restored to their natural habitats, are routinely seen in the conservation of species experiencing depletion.

Furthermore, genetic characterization of species can be used to formulate ancestry maps and understanding the migratory paths of species. This provides an essential framework for enacting species’ conservation strategies especially for migratory animals whose journeys span thousands of kilometers.

Flywheels of evolution, genetic makeup is unique to every species, embodying the cumulative history of their existence. Conservation Genetics underscores the necessity for maintaining this genetic quintessence for the mere purpose of protracted existence. Ultimately, the aspiration is to promote coexistence, not just among humans but all of lifeforms on our shared canvas.

In conclusion, conservation genetics seeks to chart new course in the conservation of species by not just safeguarding their physical form, but their genetic legacy. Understanding and implementing these principles requires the concerted effort of scientists, policy-makers, and citizens equally; after all, it’s our world and these species are our cohabitants.

This blog post elaborates on the criticality of genetic variation, the factors that cause its loss, and the various measures that can be taken to preserve it. Understanding these concepts isn’t just about theoretical knowledge, it’s about saving the world as we know it.

Understanding the Geography of Arctic and Antarctic Regions


The Arctic and Antarctic regions, comprising the Arctic Ocean and the Antarctic Plateau, are the coldest and driest places on Earth, respectively. Engineered behind the massiveness of immense glaciers and ice sheets that converge to form these extreme landscapes, these areas, have captured the imagination of scientists, adventurers, and explorers for centuries. While they share a similar harsh reputation, the Arctic and Antarctic regions possess unique geological and climatic characteristics that set them apart from other parts of the world. Geography of the Arctic The Arctic Circle consists of Alaska (United States), Canada, Greenland (Denmark), Norway, Sweden, and Russia. Stretching across approximately 14 million square miles, this vast and diverse landscape boasts mountains, valleys, rivers, and several exit roads – showcasing adaptability and a gratifying predisposition towards nature. The islands that dot the Arctic Circle include Spitsbergen, Iceland, Jan Mayen, the Svalbard archipelago, and other isolated outposts of the boreal forest, known as the Arctic tundra.

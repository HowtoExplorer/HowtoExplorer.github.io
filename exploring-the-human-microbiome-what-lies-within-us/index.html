Exploring the Human Microbiome: What Lies Within Us?


The human body is often referred to as a microcosm of the world outside our skin, teeming with diverse life forms that shape our well-being and influence our interactions with the environment. But what lies beneath the surface of our bodies, hiding in plain sight, is a complex ecosystem that has only recently begun to capture the imagination of scientists and the general public alike. Welcome to the fascinating realm of the human microbiome. Unveiling the Mysteries of the Microbiome In 1970, the Hungarian-American scientist Elie Metchnikoff discovered that Micrococcus lactilyticus (now known as Citrobacter freundii) could ferment milk into lactic acid, producing as many calories as other dairy products. This pioneering research sparked interest in the invisible world of microbes that inhabit our bodies. Nearly a century later, we now understand that human bodies harbor 39 trillion microorganisms, outnumbering human cells by tenfold. The human microbiome, comprising trillions of microorganisms, plays a crucial role in maintaining our bodily functions. These minuscule companions participate in critical processes that affect our body weight, digestion, immune system, brain function, and even behavior. Microbes inhabit a multitude of locations, with the largest taxonomic diversity found in the gut, followed closely by the skin and the respiratory tract. The metagenomics of the human microbiome involves studying the sequencing and gene expression of microbial communities. Exploring the Interconnected Web of Human Cells The nascent field of human microbiome research has paved the way for numerous important studies. According to early investigations, studies analyzing the symbiotic relationship between humans and microbes demonstrate how these tiny beings work in tandem. When we consume food, microbes help in the digestion of food components, enhancing nutrient intake. Microbiota can become risky actors when lacking balance (dysbiosis). The loss of normal flora microorganisms may result in an imbalance leading to illness. Metchnikoff was convinced of the significance of Lactobacillus (now subfamily Lacticaseibria) bacteria. He attempted an initial transfer to the big and controversial consumption of probiotics in Russia in 1901s. Broths he supplemented with this microorganism and waited a little time, adverse components disappeared from patient's urine. This work as we now see, was crucial to studying natural attenuators which researchers in turn developed to assist host's circulation.
